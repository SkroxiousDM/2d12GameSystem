## THE SHAPE OF THE WORLD

Many of the modern notions of maps, including easy access to accurate depictions of the lay of the land, simply do not exist for most people in the ancient era. Knowledge of location is highly localized. It is usually based on key landmarks and sets of directions that take a traveler from one place to another as safely as possible. This can be seen in the highly abstracted format of maps used by people of the day, such as merchants traveling from one city to the next. An ancient Roman road map does not show the actual lay of the land or geography; it is more a sketch of where to turn at various junctions to reach a given town or city. Such maps have little use when travelers venture off the roads.

A number of great minds in ancient Greece and India had already well established the Earth to be a globe, and individuals like Eratosthenes had even calculated fairly accurate estimations of its diameter. Such knowledge did not imply an accurate perspective of the world by most people, especially those without formal education. Despite advanced astronomy across multiple cultures, most people still thought the Earth was the center of all things. Heliocentric models, with the sun correctly at the center of the planets' orbits, had been proposed as early as 300 BCE (BC) by such individuals as Aristarchus of Samos, but they had not gained much ground.

This notion of centering could also be applied to Rome itself, and many in the Empire were convinced this city was at the center of all things. To such minds, the world extended seemingly endlessly in all directions from Rome; beyond its borders one might encounter all manner of barbarity, strangeness, and terrifying peril. As Rome extended its reach through conquest, so did it extend its considerable influence on everything from infrastructure (such as roads) to legal systems.

Many people felt little need to understand aspects of geography beyond their immediate surroundings. As a result, stories of far-off places took on a particularly compelling but unknowable aspect. The great lengths of time required for travel, either by land or sea, added to the sense of the world being a vast and largely untracked place. One did not have to step far from a local village to wind up in entirely unfamiliar territory, either lost in the wilds or among people with different customs, cultures, beliefs, and languages. That said, Rome itself was relatively cosmopolitan, attracting a diversity of peoples from across the empire and its affiliated regions.

This might impact the course of a Aetas Obscura game in a variety of ways, in particular ones related to ease of travel and securing accurate directions to relatively remote locations. Maps of the period should be loose and built more as a means to guide a party from one known location to another, leaving opportunities for mistakes. This dearth of knowledge about what might be found in a given area makes it easy for PCs to stumble across previously undiscovered places just off the beaten path.

## SLAVERY

One of the trickiest and most unpalatable aspects of depicting this era relates to the widespread and openly pervasive institution of slavery. We recommend that GMs who are considering incorporating this subject first speak with their players about it and receive players' consent and agreement on how best to do so.

> ***SLAVERY IN THE MODERN WORLD***
>It is an unfortunately common misapprehension that slavery is a problem of the past. This is not at all the case. Human trafficking is a $150 billion dollar industry, with 40 million people enslaved across the world. There are more people enslaved today than at any other time in human history, and it is the third biggest criminal activity in the world. There are numerous charities which help to fight slavery and human trafficking, and we encourage you to seek them out to find out how you can help join the fight to abolish slavery today.


In this era, the utilization of enslaved people is commonplace and nearly unavoidable. This practice includes a broad array of different types of enslavement, some more inhumane and brutal than others. Some people in this position have entered enslavement voluntarily, as a means to avoid debt or more severe punishments for crimes. People may be subjected to enslavement after being captured in conflicts, with captives seen as valuable commodities and an essential aspect of the spoils of war. Soldiers might be captured directly after any battle, particularly during surrender, while civilians are seized when towns or villages are invaded.

Enslavement after battle is practiced as much among the Germanic and Celtic tribes and most other nations as it is within the Roman Empire. Abhorrent as this practice is, it should be understood in the context of its time as being one that chooses its targets relatively indiscriminately. It is expected that victors may enslave those they defeat regardless of cultural origin. People are usually not enslaved simply for being of a particular ethnic, religious, or cultural group. At this time, enslaving defeated combatants is widely viewed as a relative mercy to them: taking them prisoner and sparing their life rather than executing them, so they can be useful to their captors in a new capacity. This is a rough but brutal reality that many people are forced to endure, one that irrevocably changes lives for the worse and places people in a position of subjugation, deprived of freedom.

Those who are enslaved have little to no rights and are treated as property. For many, this results in enduring extremely harsh conditions. Those subjected to hard labor might be put to inhumane toil, sent to work in mines or quarries, or assigned to other tasks where life expectancy was short and quality of life extremely poor. Enslaved captives can be beaten with little or no consequences. Though hope might be found in the possibility of eventually earning one's freedom, this is not an easy or simple .task, and most enslaved captives fail to regain higher stature.

The living conditions, lifestyle, and treatment of enslaved people in the era varies widely. Because the Roman Empire relies so heavily on slavery, enslaved people are considered a valuable and important commodity, so efforts are taken to preserve their value. There are pragmatic and practical benefits for refraining from abusing such people, though only so long as they are obedient. Some individual enslaved people are given special liberties and comparatively decent living conditions, treated more akin to ordinary servants in modern society. Enslaved servants who are literate and versed in numbers might become scribes or clerks, which were positions of some value. Any privileges afforded such people would be subject to the whims of those claiming them as property. Even someone who is allowed to live inside a family estate and has accrued favor may be subject to harsh punishments for trivial infractions and could lose that standing without warning.

Those attached to wealthy households as servants do not endure as terrible conditions as those slated for hard labor. It should nevertheless be understood that the lack of liberty and frequent degradations of daily tasks still take a toll regardless of 'better' treatment. While it is rare and infrequent, individuals might arrange to purchase their freedom or be granted that liberty after years of persistent service. More often, such promises are used as an enticement to encourage dedication to assigned tasks without any intention of being followed through. The more an enslaved servant is valued, the less likely the person holding them in slavery is to release them.

Another possible fate for an enslaved captive is to become a gladiator, taken to one of various arenas to fight for the entertainment of others. Not all gladiators are enslaved, but many are, including those deemed criminals. There are some few who volunteer to become gladiators, but for most, this is an involuntary condition. Gladiatorial games are widespread and popular across the Empire and are a vital institution, especially in the larger cities. High-profile gladiators are considered relatively privileged compared to other enslaved people. They are given training and can earn certain luxuries, as well as fame, glory, and potentially public devotion, but with the caveat that their lives are always potentially forfeit in the next game.

Revolts by enslaved groups happen from time to time, though such efforts do not generally end well. Some few might escape and flee to freedom elsewhere, however this course carries considerable peril. Mercenaries are hired to find and recapture people who have escaped their enslavers, requiring those seeking freedom to go to great lengths to avoid being taken into custody, including assuming false identities.

## PREJUDICES BETWEEN CULTURES

One unfortunate human constant is that nearly every culture disdains outsiders, especially when there are marked differences in appearances, religion, and language. This is certainly true in the era of Aetas Obscura, though there are aspects of the Roman Empire that create interreliance and a grudging acceptance of the "other," at least in specific contexts. In this era, the Empire sees a significant degree of cooperative contact between members of different cultures, particularly along the frontier.

The Empire's desire and skill at conquest require it to encompass numerous lands and to bring into its fold various peoples, who in turn are given the "opportunity" to serve its military and potentially rise in station. Not enough 'true Romans" live on the Empire's fringes to rule there or to fill the ranks of the legions, which increasingly forces Rome to rely on foreign-born auxiliaries to fight its wars. Romans tend to afford those born into powerful families in other lands greater respect, and they may elevate such people to positions of power to oversee subjugated lands. Some individuals are born of two cultures; for example, they may have one Roman parent and one parent from a Germanic tribe. Such individuals might receive preferential treatment and opportunities for advancement based on their parental ties. It is also possible for talented individuals born of other cultures to be adopted into Roman families and even become heirs.

It should be taken as a given that nearly every culture in Aetas Obscura considers itself superior to others; pride in one's people and tribe is pervasive. This is especially the case with the Romans themselves, who have a very high opinion of their civilization and what they perceive to be its divinely sanctioned imperatives. To a Roman, only Romans are civilized, all others are viewed as barbarians of one strain or another. Some groups are afforded a degree of begrudging respect, such as the Greeks, whose pantheon of gods has many direct parallels with the Romans'. The great heroes of ancient Greece are thought to have given rise to Rome, though many aspects of Greek culture are held in disdain. Roman and Greek values are not the same.

Within the Roman Empire, citizens possess a special status and view each other as the true people of the Empire, with others as their proper subjects. Even though Rome has several means by which those born of other cultures can become citizens, there always remains a degree of separation between those born as Roman citizens and those who must work to gain that status. Perceived inadequacies from a non-Roman background or lineage are never entirely erased.

Nonetheless the Roman Empire is more interested in assimilation than eradication, driven by the necessities of its growth. Those who are willing to serve the Empire and obey its mandates can earn acceptance, privilege, and even respect. The more one is willing to advance Rome's interests, the more one may find a place within its society despite originating from the outside. Similarly, Rome allows the beliefs and practices of other cultures to persist, so long as they do not create problems for Roman rulers or Roman law. Roman cults were cager to absorb and claim the legendary accomplishments of other faiths, where possible. But this also leads to animosity with groups that do not readily endure such assimilation, such as Christians, Jews, and certain other cultures.

Where warfare has been the most bitter and protracted is where deeper animosities rest, and this goes in both directions. Rome's longstanding struggles to conquer various Germanic and Gaelic tribes have given rise to lingering hostilities, some of them apt to boil over at the slightest provocation. Many tribes on the frontier know the martial might of Rome and pay lip service to obedience, but have not embraced being subjects of the Empire.

While some degree of xenophobia and prejudice are widespread in the Aetas Obscura era, they are less based on skin color and modern notions of race and are more often a matter of clashing cultures, with one side finding the other "barbaric." Regardless, such attitudes do not need to be a pervasive element of most games. While this is a natural theme to explore, especially between warring groups, in many Aetas Obscura games, one's cultural rivals and enemies may be eclipsed by sinister threats from Mythos elements or those in league with them. Similarly, many Aetas Obscura heroes come from diverse backgrounds and must trust and rely upon one another, learning to appreciate the skills and perspectives of those who are not like themselves.

## COMMUNICATION

The Aetas Obscura era is no different from others in that there are a myriad of languages, many distinct cultures and peoples giving rise to distinct vocabularies and dialects. Communication between groups is not always straightforward or easy.

Among populations within the empire and its environs, it is safe to presume at least some degree of familiarity with Latin. Soldiers born outside Rome joining the legions as auxiliaries are able to rely on their native tongue, as those with the same language are grouped together, however they might still pick up a smattering of Greek or Latin, especially curses and simple phrases related to common orders. Fluency varies widely, but anyone needing to conduct trade or politics with Romans learns so me of these languages or will be accompanied by a translator.

Throughout the ancient world in this period, as well as some time before and after, Greek is considered a universal language of the formally educated. Most people who have studied philosophy, mathematics, history, oratory, and other such disciplines are familiar with Greek in addition to other languages. Translation efforts in a highly mixed group might involve multiple language jumps: some people may speak to each other in Greek, which gets translated to someone else in Latin, etc.

For those living on or near the empire's periphery, it is not uncommon to have some mastery of multiple tongues. This can include a localized dialect as well as a broader language such as Latin or Greek, together with words or phrases from other useful languages of the region. It is not uncommon for the heroic individuals who become Aetas Obscura characters to be familiar with three or more languages, depending on their backgrounds.

A group of characters created together should have ample overlap in tongues to facilitate communication. Language barriers between player characters should not become the focus, but they can create dramatic misunderstandings and reinforce the gulf that sometimes exists between different tribes and nations. The need for translation affords opportunities for characters with mastery of several languages-often through a high level of formal education or having lived where multiple languages are commonly spoken-to shine. Facility as a translator is one way for a character to become invaluable to those in power, even if their background would not normally give them access to such people. Language barriers and distinctions should therefore be used primarily for exploring cultures or creating opportunities for PCs with relevant backgrounds or knowledge to be useful in scenes that require them.

Literacy is another matter, varying highly among cultures and less pervasive than vocal fluency. Some degree of literacy in Rome is not uncommon, even if sometimes limited to what is needed day to day. News was often spoken aloud by criers in town squares, and many trades required little to no reading or writing. Even merchants might only require a facility with written numbers or might delegate tasks to a properly skilled servant. This makes true literacy a valuable commodity, at least in some circles, though on the frontier such talents might be in less demand. Aetas Obscura characters are more likely to get embroiled in occult matters where literacy can be useful, though a group might get by with one or two people with this expertise.

The era has limited means of communicating across distances, and these suffer from the same problems as travel more generally. Even within the Roman Empire, with its fully functional bureaucracy, it might take months for letters or orders to reach far-flung outposts and legions. The use of specially trained animals like carrier pigeons is employed in the empire, though such methods are not always reliable. The best way to transmit vital messages from one place to another is to send a courier to carry or memorize them. News of major events travels only as quickly as the pace of individuals bringing that information, and verbal messages are subject to inevitable inaccuracies. These communication delays have an impact on the pace of both commerce and war.

The vagaries of long-distance communication offer GMs opportunities for plot twists and missions involving PCs arriving ill-informed or with woefully out-of-date intelligence. It is much easier in the ancient world for a given community, patrol, or even entire city to become isolated and cut off from external support in a time of crisis.

## ACCEPTANCE OF THE SUPERNATURAL

In the ancient world, there is widespread acceptance of supernatural phenomena. Gods and spirits are taken for granted as a normal part of the world, thought to be behind many inexplicable chains of events. The scientific method does not exist, which means there is no expectation that the same result will always follow from the same instigating events, or that these interactions can be deduced through experimentation. Superstitions abound, as do the beliefs in traditional remedies and ritual protections.

There is a broad spectrum of education, and there are those who have spent their lives in study and learning, including philosophers and other dedicated thinkers. However, even the most learned believe that the world is affected by pantheons of various gods and other powerful beings. To their minds, reality is shaped by unpredictable and unknowable forces, and the only way to regain some control is to appease the gods with prayers, offerings, and rites. When the unexpected happens, it is interpreted through this perspective, meaning that coincidental or unrelated matters can be interpreted as omens and portents that may predict the will of the divine.

This is complicated and compounded in the world of Aetas Obscura, as some aspects of the supernatural are demonstrably real. Occult efforts, including religious beliefs and either sacred or profane rituals, can in fact bend or shape reality. The healing prayers of a pious priest may stanch bleeding and close wounds, creating minor miracles. The tattoos on the skin of a Germanic warrior might connect them to the spirits of their ancestors, augmenting their ferocity in battle. Whether as a result of the interference of Elder Gods or other forces, belief has a power and can sometimes be wielded as a weapon or shield.

This isn't to say that regular people are unaffected by strange doings. They still expect their day-to-day lives to be ordinary and dull, and they react with shock and fear to strange phenomena. However, they do not consider the intrusion of the supernatural to be impossible, and they are often willing to attribute such events to aspects of their beliefs, such as the hand of the gods. For this reason, the people of this period are in some respects better able than we are today to adapt and react to the strange, and can be comforted by rites and rituals. Some practices we would consider "superstitious" can provide a useful function.

Belief doesn't always win out, of course. True magic is rare and generally only practiced by those who have access to special occult lore. The laws of physics and chemistry usually hold, without the interference of Mythos beings to warp reality, and the people of the era do not have a good understanding of such matters. There are architects and engineers who understand many aspects of practical mathematics and simple mechanical systems, but other fields are rife with misapprehensions, bad practices, and ignorance. This is especially true of surgery and medicine. Such practitioners may do more harm than good, and people of the era might in fact be better off turning to the gods instead.

Accepting that the supernatural is real does not negate the shock of coming face to face with horrible monsters from myth and legend, but it does provide context. That a priest of Sutekh, the Egyptian god of disorder and desert storms, could summon darkness and give it shape is not surprising. Nor is the notion that followers of such a cult can ignore their wounds and fight on past the limits of death. An ancient witnessing these events is less likely than we to be frozen into paralysis by proof that their reality is unraveling before their eyes.

Some Mythos manifestations may be interpreted within the scope of known legends and myths. Entities from another world, such as the alien Mi-Go and nightgaunts, might be thought instead to arise from the underworld. Germanic tribespeople may believe them to be minions of Hel; Romans that they are minions of Orcus. This does not change the nature of these beings and can lead to mistaken assumptions about how to combat them, but it provides a context under which they can be confronted. Belief in the gods and the efficacy of prayer can strengthen the resolve of people who would otherwise be overcome by terror.

To a certain degree, this insulates people of this era from some of the mental trauma and loss of reason that might come from exposure to the supernatural. Indeed, the conception of "madness" was different in this era. Those seeing hallucinations, behaving irrationally, or speaking incoherently might be thought to be oracles connected to divine forces, receiving visions, or possessed by spirits. In Aetas Obscura, some of these perceptions might be literally true, as godlike beings exert pressure on mortal minds to advance their agendas. Minds can still be shaken or unraveled by confrontations with violence and trauma, especially when experiencing the helplessness of batt ling forces beyond a person's control.

In this time, humanity understood its place in the cosmos to be small and insignificant. Given the accepted reality of beings like Jupiter, Wotan, and Mars, it is not difficult to adapt to confronting cults devoted to Nyarlathotep or Cthulhu.
