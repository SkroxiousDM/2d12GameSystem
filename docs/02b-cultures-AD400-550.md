# 02b — Cultures of the Late Roman World (AD 400–550)

*A research companion to [02-cultures-backgrounds-and-roles.md](./02-cultures-backgrounds-and-roles.md), expanding the roster of peoples available to characters in the 2d12 system. Each entry follows the existing format — region, flavour text, attribute spread, and Culture Talent — and adds a brief **Historical Lens** summarising the people's situation between AD 400 and 550, the languages they spoke, the deities they carried, and the legacy they left behind. Use these alongside, or in place of, the heartland cultures already in the core document.*

> **Period note.** From AD 400 to 550 the Western Empire dissolved into successor kingdoms while the Eastern Empire endured as the early Byzantine state. Most peoples in this register are bilingual, bicultural, or in the long process of becoming so. A character's culture is rarely "pure"; it is a blend of Roman institutions, ancestral memory, and the pressure of new neighbours from beyond the limes.

---

## I. The Western Provinces

The lands west of the Adriatic — Italy, Gaul, Hispania, Britannia, and the African shore — pass under the rule of federate kings during this period. Roman law, the Latin tongue, and the urban grid persist; new aristocracies wear them like borrowed clothes ([Britannica — End of Roman Gaul](https://www.britannica.com/place/France/The-end-of-Roman-Gaul-c-400-c-500)).

### Gallo-Roman

The descendants of the Gauls after five centuries under Rome — vinedressers of the Rhône, senators of Lyon and Arles, bishops of Tours and Clermont. They speak Vulgar Latin shot through with Gaulish loanwords (still attested as late as the 6th century by Gregory of Tours), worship the Christian God in basilicas raised over older sanctuaries, and remember Epona, Cernunnos, and the Matronae in roadside shrines that never quite went out of use ([Brewminate — Roman Gaul](https://brewminate.com/ancient-roman-gaul-cultural-annihilation-and-replacement/)).

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, CHA +1, INS +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Bilingual Heritage** — When dealing with both Imperial officials and rural folk, gain advantage on Manipulation tests that hinge on shared cultural reference (a saint's day, a market custom, a half-remembered local god).
- *Historical Lens:* Gaul remained "thoroughly Romanized" through the 5th century; Visigothic, Burgundian, and Frankish settlers slotted into a Gallo-Roman administrative skeleton rather than replacing it. Languages: Vulgar Latin (ancestor of Old French and Occitan), residual Gaulish. Deities absorbed: Mercury Dumias, Mars Lenus, Apollo Grannus, Sulis-Minerva-style pairings, Sequana, Rosmerta. Lasting influence: the parish map of medieval France traces directly onto Gallo-Roman *civitates*.

### Aquitanian / Vascon

Mountain folk of the western Pyrenees and the Garonne basin — herders, smugglers, and ambushers, never quite conquered by anyone. Their language is the ancestor of Basque, the last survivor of pre-Indo-European Europe, and their personal names litter Latin inscriptions from Bordeaux to Soria ([Vasconic languages — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasconic_languages)).

- *Attribute Points:* CON +1, AGI +1, INS +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Mountain-Born** — Gain advantage on Survival and Athletics tests in highland terrain, and on Stealth tests when ambushing from elevated ground.
- *Historical Lens:* Both Rome and the incoming Visigoths and Franks largely ignored the Pyrenean uplands as "poor soil." Late antiquity may have seen a *re-expansion* of Vasconic speech as the lowland Latin order frayed. Languages: Aquitanian / Proto-Basque, Vulgar Latin. Deities: Lurra (earth), Ilargi (moon), Urtzi (sky); spirit-cults of springs and caves. Lasting influence: phonological substrate beneath Gascon, Castilian, and Navarro-Aragonese.

### Hispano-Roman

Wealthy provincials of Tarraconensis, Baetica, and Lusitania — owners of olive presses and seaside villas, masters of a senatorial Latin as polished as anything in Rome itself. Hispania's prosperity dipped less sharply than Gaul's; its bishops and bureaucrats remained the practical rulers of the peninsula even after the Visigoths arrived in 415 ([Gothic Hispania — Pronovost](https://www.facebook.com/ToddAndrewPronovost/posts/1117012953760769/)).

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, INS +1, CHA +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Estate-Born** — When dealing with land, contracts, or the management of dependants, you may treat your Reason as one tier higher for the purpose of the result tier achieved.
- *Historical Lens:* The Visigothic kings adopted Roman law (the *Breviary of Alaric*, 506), Roman titles, and bishops as their chief civil servants. Languages: Late Latin (ancestor of Castilian, Catalan, Galician-Portuguese). Deities absorbed: largely Christianised, but household *Lares*, fountain-cults, and Phoenician-survival figures (Endovelicus, Ataecina) lingered in the countryside. Lasting influence: the *fundus* villa system became the medieval Spanish *aldea*.

### Lusitanian Tribesman

Backcountry hillmen of what is now western Iberia — shepherds, raiders, and seasonal labourers on Hispano-Roman estates. Their pre-Roman gods are remembered in dolmen-shadowed shrines that Christian bishops grumble about for centuries.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, AGI +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Slinger's Birthright** — Gain advantage on Fighting (Thrown Weapons) tests with sling or javelin.
- *Historical Lens:* Lusitanians were famous slingers in earlier Roman armies and still supplied auxiliary troops. Languages: Vulgar Latin, vestigial Lusitanian (Celtic / Para-Celtic). Deities: Endovelicus (oracular healer), Ataecina (chthonic moon), Bandua (war/treaties). Lasting influence: pastoral transhumance routes preserved into the medieval *cañadas*.

### Romano-Briton

The southern islanders after the legions departed in 410 — town councillors of Calleva and Cirencester, hill-fort kings, monk-scholars in seaside scriptoria. They keep Latin as a written tongue, speak Brittonic at home, and watch the Saxon shore with growing dread ([Tastes of History — Pict or Scot](https://www.tastesofhistory.co.uk/post/pict-or-scot-who-s-who)).

- *Attribute Points:* INS +1, REA +1, CHA +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Last Light of Rome** — Once per session, when narrating, citing precedent, or invoking lost authority, gain advantage on a Manipulation or Academia test.
- *Historical Lens:* Sub-Roman Britain (c. 410–550) saw fragmented kingdoms (Dumnonia, Powys, Gwynedd, Elmet) maintaining late-Roman material culture while gradually losing literacy. Languages: Brittonic (ancestor of Welsh, Cornish, Breton), Latin. Deities: Sulis (Bath), Nodens (Lydney), Coventina, Belatucadrus, increasingly the Christian saints. Lasting influence: the Arthurian matter, the *civitas*-to-kingdom transition, Breton colonisation of Armorica.

### Pict

Tattooed folk of the northern firths — confederated tribes north of the Forth and Clyde, fielding cavalry, longships, and symbol-stones whose meaning is still argued over. They never spoke Gaelic; their tongue was a P-Celtic relative of British, perhaps with a non-Indo-European substrate ([Pictavia Leather — The Picts](https://www.pictavialeather.co.uk/post/the-picts-when-and-where-a-timeline)).

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, AGI +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Symbol-Marked** — Your tattoos and carved tokens function as letters of safe-conduct among other Pictish bands; gain advantage on first-contact Charisma tests with northern peoples.
- *Historical Lens:* The Picts emerge in the historical record c. 297 and dominate the north until merging with the Gaels into Alba in the 9th century. Languages: Pictish (P-Celtic), later Gaelic; some scholars argue for a non-Celtic substrate. Deities: largely lost; symbol-stones suggest bull, eagle, and salmon cults. Lasting influence: the Pictish symbol-stone tradition, the kingdom of Alba.

### Gaelic Scot

Sea-raiding folk of Dál Riata — by AD 500 they have crossed the Irish Sea and planted a kingdom in Argyll. Half their year is spent on the water; their tongue, Old Irish, replaces Pictish across the northern half of Britain over the following centuries.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, AGI +1, CHA +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Sea-Raider** — Gain advantage on Survival (Boating) tests and on Fighting tests during a boarding action or beach assault.
- *Historical Lens:* The Dál Riatans were the first non-Roman Christians to colonise British soil. Languages: Old Irish (Q-Celtic), Latin among monastics. Deities: Lugh, Brigid (soon Saint Brigid), Manannán mac Lir, the *síde*. Lasting influence: the name "Scotland," the Iona-Lindisfarne monastic network, the Gaelic poetic tradition.

### Saxon / Anglian / Jutish Settler

A North-Sea warrior come to Britain as federate, raider, or colonist — variously called Saxon, Angle, or Jute depending on the homeland. He fights on foot with spear and seax, swears oaths in mead-halls, and burns or buries his dead with bead and brooch.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, WIL +1, AGI +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Shield-Wall** — When fighting in formation with at least one ally adjacent, gain +1 Defence against melee attacks.
- *Historical Lens:* Adventus Saxonum traditionally dated 449; by 550 the eastern third of Britain is a patchwork of Germanic-ruled hides. Languages: Old English (Ingvaeonic West Germanic). Deities: Woden, Thunor, Tiw, Frige, Eostre. Lasting influence: the English language and the day-names of the week.

### Frank

Hard-handed warriors of the lower Rhine, bound by their long hair and their oaths to the Merovingian house. By 500 they are masters of northern Gaul; by 550 they rule from the Loire to the Rhine and have made themselves the first Catholic Germanic kingdom in the West ([Merovingian dynasty — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty)).

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, AGI +1, CHA +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Throwing-Axe** — Once per combat, you may hurl an axe (*francisca*) as a free action before closing into melee. Treat as Thrown Weapon, Severity 2.
- *Historical Lens:* Clovis's conversion (c. 496/508) to Nicene Christianity gave the Franks the backing of the Gallo-Roman bishops against their Arian neighbours. Languages: Old Frankish (West Germanic), Vulgar Latin (which they ultimately adopted south of the Loire). Deities: pre-conversion Wodan, Donar, *Frōja*; post-conversion the Catholic saints. Lasting influence: the kingdom of France itself, Salic Law, Merovingian church architecture.

### Burgundian

A people who crossed the Rhine in 406, were broken by Huns in 437 (a disaster remembered in the *Nibelungenlied*), and resettled by Aetius in Savoy. By 500 their kings rule a courtly Romanised polity around Lyon and Geneva, producing the *Lex Burgundionum* for their warriors and the *Lex Romana Burgundionum* for their subjects.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, REA +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Twofold Law** — When negotiating between a Roman provincial and a Germanic warrior, you may invoke either code; gain advantage on the resulting Manipulation test.
- *Historical Lens:* Burgundians were Arian Christians who tolerated their Catholic subjects and intermarried freely with Gallo-Roman aristocracy. Languages: East Germanic Burgundian (extinct by 600), Vulgar Latin. Deities: largely Christianised. Lasting influence: the region and duchy of Burgundy, French wine country.

### Visigoth

Federate warriors turned kings of Toulouse and (after 507) of Toledo. They are Arian Christians, literate in Gothic thanks to Ulfilas's bible, and surprisingly devoted to written law: the *Codex Euricianus* and the *Breviary of Alaric* survive as monuments to their administrative ambition.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, WIL +1, REA +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Federate Discipline** — Gain advantage on Tactics tests when commanding a mixed force of Goths and provincials.
- *Historical Lens:* The Visigoths sacked Rome in 410, settled Aquitaine in 418, and after losing Vouillé to the Franks (507) consolidated in Spain. Languages: Gothic (East Germanic, written in the Ulfilan alphabet), Latin in administration. Deities: nominally Arian Christian; older Gothic *Ansiwa* (the gods) survived in personal names. Lasting influence: Spanish onomastics (Rodrigo, Fernando, Alfonso), the Visigothic script, the Iberian church organisation.

### Ostrogoth

Theodoric's people — Goths who carved out a kingdom in Italy after 489 under what was effectively a Roman regency in barbarian dress. Theodoric, raised as a hostage in Constantinople, ran his realm as a dyarchy: Goths fought, Romans administered ([Theodoric the Great — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodoric_the_Great)).

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, CHA +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Hospitalitas** — Once per session, when guesting in a household of different culture, gain advantage on a single social test relating to that hospitality.
- *Historical Lens:* The Ostrogothic kingdom (493–553) preserved the Roman Senate, the consulship, and the city of Rome itself; it fell to Justinian's Gothic War. Languages: Gothic, Latin. Deities: Arian Christianity with surviving Gothic ancestral cult. Lasting influence: Cassiodorus's *Variae*, the Mausoleum at Ravenna, the survival of late Roman material culture into the early Middle Ages.

### Vandal

A people who marched from Pannonia to Hispania to Africa in twenty-five years, sailed across to seize Carthage in 439, and built a fleet that sacked Rome in 455. Their Arian kings ruled the richest province of the old West and held it for nearly a century before Belisarius reclaimed it in 533 ([Vandal Kingdom — Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Africa/The-Vandal-conquest)).

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, DEX +1, AGI +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Sea-Borne Conqueror** — Gain advantage on Tactics tests when planning amphibious assaults, ship boardings, or coastal raids.
- *Historical Lens:* The Vandals were Arian persecutors of Catholic Africa, but their state preserved Roman fiscal machinery and an enormous late-antique villa culture. Languages: Vandalic (East Germanic, lost by 700), Latin. Deities: Arian Christian with traces of older Germanic devotion. Lasting influence: the term "Vandal," the genetic and toponymic substrate of Tunisia, and a peculiarly long-lived Romano-Berber Christianity.

### Alamannic Highlander

Border-folk of the upper Rhine and Danube — small kingdoms of mixed Suebic and indigenous Raetian stock. Broken by Clovis at Tolbiac (496) and absorbed piecemeal into the Frankish orbit. They retain a strongly traditionalist religion and a reputation for stubborn paganism well into the 7th century.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, WIL +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Oath-Sworn** — When you swear a binding oath, you gain advantage on Resilience tests to keep it; breaking it imposes disadvantage on all tests until atonement.
- *Historical Lens:* Languages: Alemannic (Upper German), Latin in towns. Deities: Wodan (Wuotan), Donar, Ziu, Frīja; sacred groves and springs. Lasting influence: the Alemannic dialects, Swiss-German place names, and the French and Spanish exonyms for Germany itself (*Allemagne*, *Alemania*).

## II. The Mediterranean Heartland

The Middle Sea remains a single economic basin even as its political unity fractures. The cultures of its core provinces — Italy, Greece, Egypt, and Africa — are urban, literate, and confessionally divided.

### Italo-Roman

Senators of Rome, curiales of Milan, monks of Monte Cassino. They are the keepers of the old order — heirs to the *cursus honorum*, patrons of basilicas, often more Roman in dress and learning under Theodoric than they were under the last Valentinian.

- *Attribute Points:* REA +2, CHA +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Eternal Heritage** — Gain advantage on Academia tests pertaining to Roman law, history, or institutional precedent.
- *Historical Lens:* Despite three sacks (410 Visigothic, 455 Vandal, 546 Ostrogothic) the senatorial families and the bishopric of Rome retained social authority. Languages: Latin (literary and vulgar). Deities: Christianised, but household *Lares*, the city-cult of Vesta, and the *Genius loci* lingered in private practice. Lasting influence: canon law, papal primacy, the survival of classical learning through Cassiodorus and Boethius.

### Hellene / Ionian Greek

Citizens of Athens, Corinth, Smyrna, Ephesus — *Rhomaioi* in their political identity, Greek to the bone in their language and education. From the 6th century Greek displaces Latin as the working tongue of the Eastern court ([Byzantines — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantines)).

- *Attribute Points:* REA +2, CHA +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Schooled Tongue** — Gain advantage on Academia (Philosophy, Rhetoric) tests and on Manipulation tests when arguing before an educated audience.
- *Historical Lens:* The neoplatonic schools of Athens were closed by Justinian in 529, but Constantinople, Alexandria, and Antioch remained engines of theological and philosophical production. Languages: Koine and increasingly Attic-revivalist Greek. Deities: Christianised; the old Olympians persisted in literary culture and occasional rural festivals (the *brumalia*, *kalandai*). Lasting influence: Byzantine liturgy, Orthodox theology, the transmission of Aristotle to Islam and back to the West.

### Macedonian Highlander

Tough upland folk of Thessaloniki's hinterland and the upper Vardar. Already heavily Hellenised in classical times, they preserved a distinctively rural identity and supplied cavalry and shepherd-soldiers to every regime from Philip II to Justinian.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, AGI +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Phalanx Memory** — When fighting with spear or pike in formation, gain +1 to Tactics tests.
- *Historical Lens:* In 400–550 Macedonia was a battlefield for Visigothic, Hunnic, and Slavic raids that the local population endured with grim continuity. Languages: Greek, surviving traces of ancient Macedonian (debated as Hellenic dialect or sister tongue). Deities: Christianised; older cults of Bendis, Sabazios, the Kabeiroi continued in local festivals. Lasting influence: the Macedonian dynasty of Byzantium, the Slavic-Greek synthesis after 600.

### Coptic Egyptian

Heirs to four thousand years of Pharaonic civilisation, now Christian monastics in the Thebaid and grain-shippers on the Nile. Their language — the last form of ancient Egyptian, written in Greek letters — is the everyday speech of village and monastery alike ([Copts — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copts)).

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, INS +1, WIL +1, CON +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Desert Fathers** — Once per session, you may invoke an ascetic discipline; gain advantage on a Resilience test against hunger, thirst, fear, or temptation.
- *Historical Lens:* The Council of Chalcedon (451) split the Egyptian church from the imperial one; the Coptic Miaphysite identity dates from this schism. Languages: Coptic (Sahidic and Bohairic dialects), Greek in cities. Deities: Christ replaced Osiris and Horus, but Isis-imagery influenced Marian devotion; older funerary practices survived in mummified saints. Lasting influence: Christian monasticism (Anthony, Pachomius, Shenoute), Marian iconography, the Coptic calendar, the deciphering key for the hieroglyphs themselves.

### Alexandrian Greek

A different breed from the Ionian Hellene — cosmopolitan, mercantile, philosophical, and dangerously theological. Alexandria is the city of Origen, Athanasius, Cyril, and Hypatia, and the riots that follow each new patriarchal election.

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, CHA +1, INS +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Catechetical Argument** — Once per scene, in a debate of doctrine, philosophy, or natural science, you may force an opponent to re-roll a successful Manipulation test.
- *Historical Lens:* Alexandria in this period housed the Catechetical School, the Library's remnants (the Serapeum was destroyed in 391), the patriarchate, and a Jewish community that ran trade as far as India. Languages: Greek, Coptic. Deities: Christian; the temple of Serapis was rubble, but mystery-cult attitudes shaped emerging Marian devotion. Lasting influence: the Trinitarian and Christological vocabulary of mainstream Christianity, the medical and astronomical canon transmitted via Arabic.

### Punic / African-Roman

Descendants of Carthage who, under five centuries of Roman rule, never quite stopped being Carthaginians. Saint Augustine's mother Monica bore a Berber name, his hometown of Thagaste was African, and *Africitas* — the distinctive Latin of the province — remained the empire's most prolific literary dialect.

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, CHA +1, INS +1, CON +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Punic Tongue** — Gain advantage on Manipulation tests involving negotiation, contract, or trade in any market touched by the African grain shipments.
- *Historical Lens:* Augustine reports that Punic was still spoken in the countryside around Hippo c. 400; Punic religion persisted in name-substitution into the 4th century ([Punic religion — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punic_religion)). Languages: Vulgar Latin (*Africitas*), Punic (Neo-Punic). Deities: Saturn (Baal Hammon), Juno Caelestis (Tanit), Liber and Ceres, syncretised under Christian surface. Lasting influence: the Latin theology of Tertullian, Cyprian, and Augustine; the Tunisian olive-oil economy.

### Mauri / Romano-Berber

Federate kingdoms of the African mountains and steppe — the Aures, Hodna, Kabylia, and the Saharan fringe. After the Vandal collapse and Byzantine reconquest these polities (Altava, Ouarsenis, Dorsale) blend Roman titulature, Latin epitaphs, Christian basilicas, and Berber tribal organisation ([Mauro-Roman Kingdom — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauro-Roman_Kingdom)).

- *Attribute Points:* CON +1, AGI +1, WIL +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Mountain Raider** — Gain advantage on Survival tests in arid uplands and on Stealth tests when laying ambushes from broken terrain.
- *Historical Lens:* Mauri rulers used Roman titles (*rex Maurorum et Romanorum*), built Christian funerary monuments (the Djedars), and resisted both Vandals and Byzantines. Languages: Late Latin, Berber (Tamazight, written in Tifinagh). Deities: Christian on the surface, with persistent reverence for ancestor-tombs and the goddess *Ifri* (the cave). Lasting influence: the Berber Christianity that survived into the early Islamic period, the matriline-strong social structures of the Atlas.

### Numidian

Cavalry stock of the high plains — heirs to Massinissa and Jugurtha, now horse-breeders, light skirmishers, and seasonal labourers on Roman olive estates. Many serve as auxiliaries; some remember the old Punic gods their grandparents called *Baal*.

- *Attribute Points:* AGI +1, DEX +1, INS +1, CON +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Numidian Rider** — When mounted, gain advantage on Fighting tests with javelins and on Athletics tests to remain in the saddle under duress.
- *Historical Lens:* Numidia remained a settled, urbanised African province through the 4th century before fraying under Vandal and Berber pressure. Languages: Latin, Libyan (Old Berber). Deities: Saturn, Caelestis, the dii Mauri; later Christian. Lasting influence: the *cavalry tradition* of North Africa, foundational to Mauretanian, Almoravid, and Spanish-Moorish equestrianism.

### Garamantian Caravaner

People of the deep Sahara — Fezzan oasis-dwellers who run trans-desert trade in salt, gold, ivory, slaves, and carnelian. Their kingdom is past its peak by AD 400 but still trades with both the Roman and the Aksumite worlds.

- *Attribute Points:* CON +1, INS +1, WIL +1, REA +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Star-Navigator** — Gain advantage on Survival (Navigation) tests in desert or trackless wastes, day or night.
- *Historical Lens:* The Garamantes maintained a sophisticated foggara-irrigation civilisation in modern Libya, with palace-cities like Garama. Languages: Old Berber, Punic and Latin in dealings with the coast. Deities: ancestor-tombs, sun and moon worship, syncretisms with Ammon. Lasting influence: the trans-Saharan trade routes inherited by the medieval Tuareg.

## III. The Balkan and Danubian Frontier

The arc from the Julian Alps to the Black Sea is the empire's most contested ground in this period — invaded by Goths, Huns, Gepids, Lombards, and Slavs in succession, yet still the cradle of Roman emperors and the spine of the Eastern army.

### Illyrian Soldier-Stock

Highlanders of Dalmatia, Praevalitana, and the Dinaric chain — the empire's most reliable recruits since the 3rd century. Diocletian, Constantine, Justin, and Justinian were all of Illyrian or Dardanian extraction.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, WIL +1, AGI +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Soldier's Roman** — Gain advantage on Resilience tests and once per session may reroll a failed Discipline check while on watch, march, or campaign.
- *Historical Lens:* Illyricum provided the *limitanei* who held the Danube under Anastasius and Justinian. Languages: Latin (with surviving Illyrian onomastics), Greek in the coastal cities, fading Illyrian. Deities: Silvanus (a peculiarly Illyrian favourite), Diana, Bindus-Neptunus; Christianised on the surface. Lasting influence: the Albanian language, descended from a late form of Illyrian; the Roman highway grid of the western Balkans.

### Dacian Survivor

People of the lost province north of the lower Danube — Roman Dacia was abandoned in 271 but Dacian-Roman populations remained, and a Latin-speaking provincial culture clung to the Carpathian foothills throughout the migration period.

- *Attribute Points:* CON +2, MIG +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Forest-Forged** — Gain advantage on Survival tests in mountainous woodland and on Resilience tests against cold or exhaustion.
- *Historical Lens:* Dacian-Roman continuity is argued via Romanian linguistic descent. Languages: Vulgar Latin (ancestor of Romanian), residual Dacian. Deities: Zalmoxis (chthonic immortality), Gebeleizis (sky-thunder), Bendis (huntress), Christianised slowly. Lasting influence: the Romanian language and Vlach pastoral identity.

### Thracian Highlander

Mountain folk of the Rhodopes, Haemus, and Strandzha — the empire's other great recruiting ground. The Thracian tongue was still spoken in the 6th century: monks at a Sinai monastery prayed in Bessian alongside Greek, Latin, Syriac, and Coptic ([Thracians — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracians)).

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, INS +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Mountain Mystic** — Gain advantage on tests involving omen-reading, herb-lore, and the recognition of holy or haunted ground.
- *Historical Lens:* Thracians supplied light cavalry (*equites Thraces*) and gladiators (Spartacus was Thracian) and produced the emperor Leo I. Languages: Thracian (extinct by 600), Greek, Latin. Deities: Sabazios (a horseman-god), Bendis, Zalmoxis, the "Thracian Horseman" (Heros) whose stelae cover the Balkans. Lasting influence: the Heros-cult fed Christian iconography of saints George, Theodore, and Demetrius.

### Goth (Tervingian / Greuthungian)

Beyond the empire — the great Gothic confederation of the lower Danube and Pontic steppe. Already Christianised in their Arian form by Ulfilas (Wulfila, c. 311–383), they cross into the empire from 376 onwards and within a century have founded two of its successor states.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +2, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Gothic Oath** — When you swear a sworn-companion bond with another character, you both gain +1 to Resilience tests while fighting in sight of each other.
- *Historical Lens:* The Goths produced the first Germanic literature — the Gothic Bible — and the first Germanic written law. Languages: Gothic (East Germanic, only Germanic language with a surviving full alphabet and bible). Deities: Arian Christianity, with Gothic gods (Tiwaz, Wōðanaz) lingering in personal names. Lasting influence: see Visigoth and Ostrogoth above.

### Gepid

Cousins of the Goths who held Dacia and parts of Pannonia in the wake of Attila's collapse. They are the Huns' former vassals turned the chief power of the middle Danube until the Lombards and Avars destroyed them in 567.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, CON +1, AGI +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Riverlord** — Gain advantage on Tactics tests in any battle fought on or beside a major river, and on Survival (Boating) tests on inland waters.
- *Historical Lens:* Gepid kings minted coins, hosted Latin notaries, and competed with the Lombards for the post-Hunnic carpathian basin. Languages: Gepidic (East Germanic). Deities: Arian Christian over older Gothic-style ancestral cult. Lasting influence: the Avar-period substrate of the Carpathian basin.

### Hunnic Horse-Lord

A people of the Pontic and later Pannonian steppe, blending Inner Asian tribes with subjugated Sarmatians, Alans, Goths, and Gepids. Under Attila (r. c. 434–453) they extort tribute from both Roman empires; after his death the confederation shatters but its descendants (Kutrigurs, Utigurs, Bulgars) remain a frontier force.

- *Attribute Points:* AGI +2, DEX +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Composite Bow** — Gain advantage on Fighting (Archery) tests made from horseback, and treat the reflex bow as one Severity higher when used mounted.
- *Historical Lens:* Hunnic society was multi-ethnic, multilingual, and oriented around a charismatic warlord. Languages: a Hunnic tongue (Turkic or Yeniseian? — unresolved), Gothic and Latin as lingua francas. Deities: Tengri-style sky-cult, shamanic horse and sword rites (the famous "Sword of Mars"). Lasting influence: the steppe-warfare doctrine inherited by Avars, Bulgars, Magyars, and Mongols.

### Alan

Iranian-speaking riders of the western steppe who joined the Vandals and Suevi in the 406 Rhine crossing. By 550 some Alan groups have settled in Gaul (around Orléans and Valence), in Spain (briefly), and in the Caucasus (the ancestors of the Ossetians).

- *Attribute Points:* AGI +1, DEX +1, MIG +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Lance-and-Bow** — You may switch between lance and bow on horseback as a free action, with no penalty for shifting weapons in the same round.
- *Historical Lens:* Languages: Alanic (Northeast Iranian, ancestor of Ossetian). Deities: an Iranian pantheon (Wacilla, Watsilla — the thunder-god — later Christianised); Christian baptism widespread by the 6th century in the Caucasus. Lasting influence: Ossetian Nart sagas, possibly the Arthurian "sword in the stone" motif (via the Sarmatian-Alan cavalry stationed in Britain).

### Slavic Settler

By 500 Slavic-speaking groups (Sclaveni, Antes) press south of the Danube. They farm small clearings, raid in summer, and worship sky and storm gods unknown to the Roman census-taker. Within a century they will be the demographic majority of much of the Balkans.

- *Attribute Points:* CON +1, INS +1, WIL +1, AGI +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Forest-Hidden** — Gain advantage on Stealth tests in dense woodland or wetland, and on Survival (Foraging) tests in temperate forest.
- *Historical Lens:* The Sclaveni and Antes are described by Procopius and Jordanes c. 550 as raiding south of the Danube and serving as Byzantine federates. Languages: Common Slavic. Deities: Perun (thunder), Veles (cattle, underworld), Mokosh (earth, weaving), Svarog (smith-sky). Lasting influence: the South-Slavic majority population of the Balkans from the 7th century onward.

## IV. The Eastern Provinces and Beyond

The empire's eastern frontier is a kaleidoscope of literate, ancient peoples in continual exchange — and rivalry — with the Sasanian Persian world.

### Syrian Aramaean

Native peoples of the Levant — Syriac-speaking peasants of Edessa, Antioch's hinterland, and the Tur Abdin. Their tongue, Aramaic, is the everyday speech of villages from the Mediterranean to the Tigris, while Greek dominates the cities ([Syriac sources — IAS](https://www.ias.edu/ideas/2017/debie-syriac)).

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, INS +1, WIL +1, CHA +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Three Tongues** — Gain advantage on Linguistics tests and on Manipulation tests that hinge on subtle distinctions between high and low speech.
- *Historical Lens:* The Syriac literary tradition — Ephrem the Syrian, the Peshitta, the schools of Edessa and Nisibis — became, after Chalcedon (451), the carrier of Miaphysite and Nestorian Christianity from the Mediterranean to China. Languages: Syriac (Edessan Aramaic), Greek. Deities: Christ (now in Miaphysite, Nestorian, and Chalcedonian forms), older Bel-Shamin and Atargatis surviving in folk-practice. Lasting influence: the Syriac churches surviving today; the philosophical and medical bridge to Arabic Islam.

### Samaritan

A community surviving on Mount Gerizim and around Neapolis — keepers of the Pentateuch and the priestly line, distinct from Judaism since classical times. The Samaritan revolts of 484, 529, and 556 against Byzantine taxation and church-building were crushed savagely.

- *Attribute Points:* WIL +2, REA +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Holy Mountain** — Once per session, when defending faith, family, or place, you may treat a single failed Resilience test as a success.
- *Historical Lens:* The mid-6th century saw the Samaritan population fall from perhaps a million to a remnant of tens of thousands. Languages: Samaritan Aramaic, Samaritan Hebrew (liturgical). Deities: the One God of the Pentateuch alone. Lasting influence: the Samaritan community endures today, the oldest continuously practising priesthood in the world.

### Judaean Diaspora Jew

A community spread from the Atlantic to the Caspian — rabbinic academies in Tiberias and Sura, merchant houses in Alexandria, Antioch, and Rome, scattered villages from Spain to the Persian Gulf. The two Talmuds reach their classical shape during this very period.

- *Attribute Points:* REA +2, INS +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Talmudic Disputation** — Gain advantage on Academia tests of law, ethics, or exegesis, and once per session you may compel an interlocutor to reframe their argument.
- *Historical Lens:* The Jerusalem Talmud was completed c. 400; the Babylonian Talmud reached substantial form by c. 500. Languages: Aramaic (Jewish Babylonian), Hebrew (liturgical and learned), Greek, Latin, regional vernaculars. Deities: the One God; older folk-angelology (Sambation, Metatron) flourished. Lasting influence: the foundational corpus of rabbinic Judaism.

### Phoenician Coastal

The harbour-folk of Tyre, Sidon, Berytus, and Tripoli — Hellenised in language but Phoenician in trade, ship-craft, and dye-houses. Berytus (Beirut) was the most prestigious school of Roman law in the empire until its destruction by earthquake in 551.

- *Attribute Points:* DEX +1, REA +1, CHA +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Purple-Dyer's Eye** — Gain advantage on Crafting tests with dyes, perfumes, glass, or fine textiles, and on Manipulation tests when appraising luxury goods.
- *Historical Lens:* The Phoenician language survived in personal names and in Africa; the cities themselves were Greek-speaking and Christian. Languages: Greek, Latin (legal), Phoenician (in remnant). Deities: nominally Christian, with persistent folk-cult of Adonis-Tammuz at Byblos and of Astarte's springs. Lasting influence: Roman law itself, through the Berytus jurists Papinian, Ulpian, and Modestinus — and indirectly through the Justinianic codification.

### Armenian

Mountain people of the great plateau — Christianised by Gregory the Illuminator (c. 301) before any other state, and given a unique alphabet by Mesrop Mashtots in 405. Armenia is a perpetual buffer between Roman and Sasanian power; its noble *nakharar* houses navigate both ([Christianization of Armenia — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianization_of_Armenia)).

- *Attribute Points:* CON +1, REA +1, WIL +1, MIG +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Mashtots's Letters** — Gain advantage on Academia (Linguistics, Religion) and on any test made to copy, decipher, or compose in an unfamiliar script.
- *Historical Lens:* The Battle of Avarayr (451) cemented Armenian Christian identity against Sasanian Zoroastrian pressure. Languages: Classical Armenian (Grabar). Deities: Christianised; the older pantheon (Vahagn the dragon-slayer, Anahit the mother, Mihr the sun, Aramazd the supreme) endured in folklore and personal names. Lasting influence: the Armenian Apostolic Church, the Mashtotsian alphabet, the *Bagratuni* noble tradition.

### Georgian (Iberian / Lazic / Colchian)

People of the Caucasus — the kingdom of Iberia (Kartli) Christianised c. 326, the kingdom of Lazica (Egrisi) a Roman client on the Black Sea. Both serve as the empire's eastern hinge in its wars with Persia.

- *Attribute Points:* CON +1, MIG +1, CHA +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Caucasian Hospitality** — When a guest in a stranger's home, gain advantage on Manipulation tests and may treat all hostility as merely cool reception until the guest-bond is broken.
- *Historical Lens:* The Lazic War (541–562) was fought over precisely this frontier. Languages: Old Georgian (the asomtavruli script was created in this period). Deities: Christianised; older worship of Armazi, Gatsi, Ga survived. Lasting influence: the Georgian Orthodox Church, the *Mokvi* and *Bichvinta* church-building traditions.

### Ghassanid Arab

Banū Ghassān — a Christianised Arab dynasty federated to Byzantium and stationed along the limes Arabicus from Damascus to Petra. By the 6th century they are the empire's chief Arab proxy against the Persian-aligned Lakhmids ([Ghassanids — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghassanids)).

- *Attribute Points:* AGI +1, CHA +1, INS +1, WIL +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Phylarch** — Gain advantage on Manipulation (Negotiation) and on Tactics tests when commanding light cavalry of mixed Arab tribes.
- *Historical Lens:* Phylarch al-Harith ibn Jabala was raised to *patricius* by Justinian (529) and led federate Arab armies in the Persian wars. Languages: Arabic, Syriac, Greek. Deities: Miaphysite Christian, with pre-Islamic Arab gods (Allat, Dushara) lingering in poetry and oath. Lasting influence: the Ghassanid model of Arab-Byzantine kingship anticipated the Umayyads; their architecture survives at al-Resafa and Hawran.

### Lakhmid Arab

Banū Lakhm — the Christian-and-pagan Arab dynasty of al-Hira, federated to the Sasanian Persians. They were the Ghassanids' mirror image: gateway between settled and nomadic Arabia, sponsors of pre-Islamic poetry, defenders of the Persian western flank ([Lakhmid kingdom — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lakhmid_kingdom)).

- *Attribute Points:* AGI +1, CHA +1, DEX +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Court of al-Hira** — Gain advantage on Manipulation (Rhetoric, Innuendo) tests in any setting where poetry, lineage, or honour-debt is in play.
- *Historical Lens:* Al-Mundhir III (r. c. 505–554) defeated Belisarius's Ghassanid allies at Callinicum (531). Languages: Arabic, Syriac, Middle Persian. Deities: a mixed court — the king's family were variously Nestorian, Manichaean, or pagan. Lasting influence: the *Mu'allaqat* poetic tradition, the bridge from pre-Islamic Arabia to the early caliphate.

### Sasanian Persian

Subjects of the King of Kings, Šāhanšāh — Persians of the imperial core (Fars, Media, Khuzistan) and the bureaucratic and military classes from Ctesiphon. The Sasanian state is the great civilizational peer and rival of Rome ([Wikipedia — Did the Eastern Romans or Sassanids perceive the other as peer](https://www.reddit.com/r/AskHistorians/comments/2x72z1/)).

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, CHA +1, AGI +1, DEX +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Royal Bearing** — When negotiating, persuading, or issuing commands, treat your Charisma as 1 higher for determining the result tier.
- *Historical Lens:* Khosrow I Anushirvan (r. 531–579) reformed taxation, founded the academy of Gondishapur, and reset Roman-Persian relations. Languages: Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Aramaic in administration. Deities: Zoroastrian — Ahura Mazda, Mithra, Anahita, the *Amesha Spentas*; Christianity tolerated through the Church of the East. Lasting influence: the Pahlavi literary corpus, much of Sasanian court ceremonial inherited by both the Umayyads and Byzantium.

### Median Highlander

Subjects of the Sasanian crown from the Zagros highlands — herders, miners, and ancestors of the Kurdish peoples. The Median nobility still mattered at the Sasanian court; their cavalry tradition was older than the empire itself.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, AGI +1, INS +1, CON +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Highland Cavalry** — Gain advantage on Athletics (Riding) tests in broken terrain and on Survival tests in cold mountain conditions.
- *Historical Lens:* Languages: Old Iranian dialects (ancestors of Kurdish, Zaza-Gorani). Deities: Zoroastrian, with surviving Yazidi-precursor angelology (Peacock figures). Lasting influence: the Kurdish nation; the Mithraic substrate of Roman soldier-religion came partly through this region.

### Aksumite Ethiopian

People of the Red Sea trade kingdom — masters of incense, ivory, gold, and obsidian, the only African state minting its own coinage alongside Rome and Persia. Aksum adopted Christianity under King Ezana c. 330 and remained a Roman ally throughout the 5th and 6th centuries ([Kingdom of Aksum — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum)).

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, CON +1, CHA +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Two-Sea Trader** — Gain advantage on Manipulation (Negotiation) tests in any port, market, or caravanserai touching the Red Sea or Indian Ocean trade.
- *Historical Lens:* King Kaleb invaded Himyar in 525 at Justin I's behest to avenge persecuted Christians. Languages: Ge'ez (with its own Ethiopic syllabary, derived from Sabaic), Greek. Deities: Christian (the Ethiopian Tewahedo tradition emerged from Egyptian monastic influence); older South-Semitic pantheon (Astar, Beher, Meder) remembered in inscriptions. Lasting influence: the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, the Ge'ez literary tradition, the Solomonic dynasty's legitimising mythology.

### Himyarite South Arabian

People of the incense kingdoms of Yemen — by the 5th century a kingdom that converted to Judaism (Dhu Nuwas), persecuted its Christians (the Najran martyrs, 523), and was conquered by Aksum (525) in response. A sophisticated, literate, irrigation-civilisation collapsing into the long Islamic prelude.

- *Attribute Points:* REA +1, CHA +1, CON +1, INS +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Marib Engineer** — Gain advantage on Crafting (Architecture) and Engineering tests involving dams, irrigation, and stone-cut hydraulics.
- *Historical Lens:* The Great Dam of Marib failed catastrophically in this period — an event remembered in the Qur'an. Languages: Sabaic, Himyaritic. Deities: a South-Semitic pantheon (Almaqah, Athtar, Wadd) gave way first to monotheism (*Rahmanan*), then to Christianity and Judaism alongside it. Lasting influence: the Ethiopic alphabet, the *qabili* tribal-legal tradition of Yemen.

### Nubian / Nobatian

Heirs to Meroe, by AD 400 reorganised into the Christian kingdoms of Nobatia, Makuria, and Alodia (Christianised in the 6th century under Justinian's missionaries). They traded gold, ivory, slaves, and emeralds northwards into Egypt and held the upper Nile.

- *Attribute Points:* MIG +1, INS +1, DEX +1, CHA +1
- *Culture Talent:* **Bowman of Kush** — Gain advantage on Fighting (Archery) tests at long range, and on Survival tests on the river.
- *Historical Lens:* Procopius records Justinian's and Theodora's competing missions to convert Nobatia. Languages: Old Nubian (with a script descended from Coptic and Meroitic). Deities: Christianised under Theodora's missionaries by c. 543; older worship of Isis at Philae continued in Nubian hands until Justinian closed the temple c. 537. Lasting influence: the medieval Nubian Christian kingdoms (Makuria, Alodia), the Old Nubian Bible.

## VI. How to Use These at the Table

- **Pick the lens, not the label.** A "Romano-Briton" who fled to Armorica plays very differently from one defending a hill-fort in Powys. The Historical Lens is there to give the GM and player a shared frame; details should be tailored to the campaign.
- **Treat AD 400–550 as a single creative window.** A character may have grown up in a still-functioning Roman *civitas* and ended their youth in a new federate kingdom — bilingualism, dual loyalties, and shifting religion are the *expected* texture of this era.
- **Deities are tools, not décor.** Most of these peoples are nominally Christian by 550, but the older gods leave persistent fingerprints — on names, oaths, festivals, and saints. A character may pray to Saint Brigid and still pour milk on the threshold for the *Brigid* she replaced.
- **Pair cultures with the existing classes and backgrounds.** A *Vascon Outcast Hunter*, a *Ghassanid Noble Cataphract*, a *Coptic Scribe Philosopher* — the established class, background, and role rules from the core document plug straight into these new cultures with no extra bookkeeping.
