# Magic

Magic is power drawn through mortal will, sacred relationship, inherited rite, forbidden research, or desperate improvisation. However a practitioner reaches it, the basic truth is the same: magic is real, dangerous, and never entirely under control.

This chapter presents magic in the order most players need it at the table: what kind of practitioner a character is, how spells are prepared and cast, what risks attend the use of magic, how traditions shape magical practice, and how new workings are learned. The underlying resolution engine, attributes, and derived ratings used by magic remain unchanged.

Magic is divided into two broad disciplines. **Evocation** is fast magic used in the moment: curses, blessings, attacks, wards, signs, and other workings that can be brought to bear in a scene. **Thaumaturgy** is ritual magic: slower, more exacting, and capable of effects far beyond the reach of ordinary spellcasting.

---

## Paths to Power

Not every practitioner reaches magic by the same road. Some are initiated into an established tradition, some assemble occult understanding from text and experiment, and some seize fragments of power they do not fully understand.

Your path determines how you learn, how you prepare magic, and which mental attribute you use when magic demands a test.

### Traditional Practitioners

Traditional spellcasters draw power from an established magical tradition. Their magic is taught, inherited, initiated, or received under the guidance of a recognised line of practice.

Traditional spellcasters begin play with three battlefield spells drawn from their tradition's spellbook.

- May learn spells only from their own magical tradition.
- May perform rituals.
- Learn new spells through study with a mentor.
- Use Insight + Survival when binding spells and making learning tests.
- Pneuma = 2 + INS + Traits.

Traditional magic is the most stable path. It offers the clearest route to refinement, but it also binds the practitioner to the teachings, symbols, taboos, and inherited assumptions of a specific tradition.

### Research Practitioners

Research spellcasters assemble magical knowledge from ancient texts, forbidden manuscripts, spirit bargains, recovered formulae, temple archives, and other unstable sources. They are less constrained by lineage, but far more dependent on access to knowledge.

Research spellcasters begin play with two battlefield spells drawn from any magical tradition's spellbook.

- May learn spells from any magical tradition.
- May perform rituals.
- Learn new spells by acquiring research materials, typically rare or ancient texts found during adventures and studied during downtime. A research spellcaster may also learn from a mentor or a supernatural entity.
- Some spells may be too deeply embedded in a specific tradition's lore for an outsider to master. The GM determines whether a given spell is accessible.
- Use Reason + Academia when binding spells and making learning tests.
- Pneuma = 2 + REA + Traits.

Research practice is broad, flexible, and dangerous. Its strength lies in range and adaptation; its weakness lies in incomplete context, unreliable sources, and the constant temptation to go one manuscript too far.

### Dabblers

Dabblers have pieced together an incomplete understanding of magic from stolen notes, half-heard rites, second-hand commentary, strange dreams, or contact with forces they were never meant to approach. They know enough to touch power, but not enough to trust it.

Dabblers begin play with either one standard battlefield spell or two flawed spells drawn from any magical tradition's spellbook.

- May attempt to learn any spell, but each spell learned is flawed.
- May perform rituals.
- Non-spellcasters may also learn rituals using the dabbler's process, though they cannot learn spells.
- Use Willpower + Resilience when binding spells and making learning tests.
- Pneuma = 1 + WIL + Traits.

Dabbling is the fastest path into magic and the least reliable. It produces practitioners who can surprise everyone at the table, including themselves.

### Flawed Spells

A flawed spell is a working the caster knows only in part. The dabbler can call it up and release it, but cannot fully regulate its shape, emphasis, or aftershock.

- When a flawed spell is cast, it automatically generates one complication in addition to any produced by the skill test. Even a successful cast is treated as a miscast.
- Success Levels specific to the spell's profile cannot be applied when casting a flawed version.
- Some spell profiles include a **Flawed** entry specifying additional adjustments.

To remove a spell's flawed status, the dabbler must cast it successfully at least once during play, not during downtime, then refine their technique through dedicated study during a downtime period. No other significant activities may be undertaken during that downtime.

## Core Procedure

Magic uses the same action resolution structure as the rest of the game. Spells rely on action rolls, opposed tests when resisted, and the usual rules for complications and Success Levels.

Unless a spell or ritual says otherwise, the caster's attribute depends on path:

- **Traditional:** Insight
- **Research:** Reason
- **Dabbling:** Willpower
- **Non-spellcaster ritualists:** Willpower

The skill used for the test is listed in the spell or ritual profile. Possessing an occultly relevant specialisation reflects useful knowledge, but it does not by itself grant the ability to cast battlefield spells. Casting battlefield spells requires a Talent with the Spellcaster keyword. Rituals do not share this requirement.

### Magical Skills

Which skill a spell or ritual requires depends on its nature and on how the character has learned to approach the supernatural. Several skills have specialisations that commonly support occult practice.

- **Academia (Religion):** A common route into the study of the arcane and the supernatural, especially where magic is intertwined with sacrifice, liturgy, and divine observance.
- **Academia (Linguistics):** Useful for translating dead languages, temple formulae, and ancient occult texts whose warnings are often clearer after the fact.
- **Fighting (War Magic):** A martial approach to blessing, battle-invocation, and the use of sacred force in combat.
- **Medicine (Hieratic Healing):** Healing rites, intercessory practice, and sacred procedures that induce gods or spirits to relieve suffering.
- **Observation (Instincts):** Sensitivity to unnatural presences, hidden influences, and the subtle pressure of the supernatural on the world.
- **Manipulation (Invocation):** Command, persuasion, bargaining, emotional shaping, and formal address directed toward spirits, powers, or the human mind.
- **Resilience (Discipline):** Self-control, endurance, and the ability to remain intact while channeling forces that strain body and mind.
- **Survival (Mysticism):** Oral, animistic, land-bound, and inherited practices often transmitted through wilderness tradition rather than text.
- **Tactics (Omen Reading):** Reading signs, interpreting portent, and extracting guidance from pattern, motion, and coincidence.

### Pneuma

Pneuma represents the intensity a practitioner can bring to a spell or ritual. It determines the number of d3 rolled when magic deals Stress or when ritual progress is generated.

Pneuma is calculated as follows:

- **Traditional:** Pneuma = 2 + INS + Traits
- **Research:** Pneuma = 2 + REA + Traits
- **Dabbling:** Pneuma = 1 + WIL + Traits

A dabbler may increase their Base Pneuma to 3 whenever they roll a magic test in exchange for generating 1 Doom. Some Talents and other abilities may further increase Pneuma under specific circumstances.

### Spell and Ritual Stress

Not all spells and rituals deal Stress to their targets, though most impose a cost on the caster. Where a working deals Stress to a target, the profile specifies whether that Stress is physical or mental.

When a spell or ritual that deals Stress is successfully cast, resolve it as follows:

1. **Roll Pneuma:** Roll a number of d3 equal to your Pneuma rating, plus any additional dice specified by the spell. Total the results, then subtract the number of dice rolled. This is the amount of Stress dealt.
2. **Determine Resistance:** The target reduces incoming Stress by the appropriate resistance: Defence against physical Stress, Courage against mental Stress.
3. **Inflict Stress:** Apply any remaining Stress to the target's Sōma or Psychē, as appropriate.

A spell that deals **n** damage is therefore resolved as **nd3-n** or **n(d3-1)**, matching the normal damage convention used elsewhere in the game.

> **Example:** A traditional spellseer with Pneuma 5 casts a Pneuma +2 attack spell. They roll 7d3 and total the results, then subtract 7. A roll of 2, 3, 1, 2, 3, 2, 1 totals 14 and yields 7 Stress before resistance. The target's Defence then reduces that Stress before it is applied.

### Success Level Spends in Magic

Some spell and ritual profiles give additional ways to spend Success Levels. In all cases, spellcasters may also spend Success Levels on the following options:

- **Reduce Cost:** For each Success Level spent, gain +1 Courage resistance against the spell or ritual's casting cost.
- **Increase Stress:** For each Success Level spent, add +1 to the Stress dealt to targets.
- **Extend Duration:** For 2 Success Levels, double the duration of any spell or ritual with a duration other than instant.

## Preparing and Casting Spells

Evocation is the quick, practical face of magic. It is the kind of power a practitioner can bring into a tense negotiation, a sudden ambush, or a collapsing battle line.

### Focus and Binding

Spells must be bound to a Focus before they can be cast. A Focus is a totemic object or significant implement, such as a wand, runestone, staff, mask, carved token, inscribed knife, or similar item through which the caster stores prepared magic.

A bound spell remains in the Focus for as long as the spellcaster remains conscious. If the spellcaster is rendered unconscious, bound spells dissipate safely without miscasting.

A spellcaster cannot cast an unbound spell. A spellcaster may hold a number of bound spells equal to their Pneuma rating. Each bound spell may be cast any number of times, though each casting still carries the cost listed in the spell's profile.

**Binding Procedure:** Binding spells to a Focus requires a Challenging skill test and takes a number of minutes equal to the combined difficulty of all spells being prepared.

- Traditional spellcasters use Insight + Survival.
- Research spellcasters use Reason + Academia.
- Dabblers use Willpower + Resilience.

Once bound, spells remain in the Focus until the spellcaster sleeps or is rendered unconscious.

> **What Is a Focus?** A Focus is the bridge between the practitioner's method and the magic they are trying to shape. Its true function is metaphysical, but it is almost always expressed through a physical object invested with personal, ritual, or symbolic significance. Normally, a character finds or creates their own Focus.

### Casting a Battlefield Spell

During a scene, a spellcaster may attempt to cast any spell currently bound to their Focus. A spellcaster may cast at most one spell per turn.

In an action scene, casting a spell requires the **Cast a Spell** major action, which costs 2 Actions from the caster's Akmē pool. In a standard scene, casting a spell requires the appropriate skill test unless the spell's profile says otherwise.

Each spell lists the skill used to cast it. The caster uses the attribute determined by their path to power.

### Resisting Magic

Some spells are resisted. In those cases, the casting test becomes an **opposed test**.

The caster rolls using their spellcasting attribute and the spell's listed skill. The target rolls using the attribute and skill named in the spell's Difficulty entry. Both tests use the spell's listed difficulty.

Magic is formidable, but not irresistible. A resisted spell is a direct struggle between the will shaping the effect and the will, instinct, discipline, or awareness pushing back against it.

### Spell Format

Each battlefield spell is presented using the following profile elements:

- **Skill:** The skill used on tests to cast the spell. The attribute is determined by the caster's path.
- **Difficulty:** The difficulty of the casting test. If the spell is resisted, this entry also names the attribute and skill used to oppose it.
- **Cost:** The mental Stress the caster suffers on casting, applied after the casting test whether the spell succeeds or fails. This Stress is reduced by Courage as normal.
- **Duration:** How long the spell lasts. Most are instant. Others last rounds, hours, or until the end of the scene. A spellcaster may dismiss a spell early as a minor action costing 1 Action; otherwise it ends when its duration expires or when the caster is Staggered or forcibly distracted.
- **Category:** The spell's category or categories.
- **Spell Effect:** What happens on a successful casting, including targets and any profile-specific uses for Success Levels.
- **Flawed:** If present, changes that apply when a flawed version of the spell is cast.
- **Success Levels:** If present, extra benefits available by spending Success Levels.

### Spell Categories

Spells are grouped by what they do. A spell may belong to more than one category and gains the rules of each category it possesses.

#### Attack

Attack spells inflict physical or mental Stress through force, pain, terror, or directed supernatural assault. When successfully cast, the spell's Stress is resolved using the caster's Pneuma rating.

#### Banishment

Banishment spells function as attacks, but are especially effective against supernatural entities. If a banishment spell inflicts 1 or more Critical Wounds on a supernatural creature, that creature loses its next turn as it struggles to remain in this world. If a supernatural target is defeated by a banishment spell, it vanishes immediately, cast back whence it came.

#### Blessing

A blessing spell infuses its target with magical advantage, favour, resilience, clarity, or another beneficial supernatural effect.

#### Control

A control spell inflicts mental Stress and breaks down a target's autonomy. If a control spell causes the target to lose their last point of Psychē, the target falls under the spellcaster's control as described in the spell's profile rather than suffering a Madness.

#### Curse

Curse spells burden their targets with hindrance, weakness, ruin, ill luck, or hostile supernatural attention. Each curse spell's profile describes how it may be broken.

#### Divination

Divination spells reveal knowledge that could not otherwise be obtained. Their exact scope depends on the profile, but they always give the caster access to information beyond ordinary perception.

#### Manifestation

Manifestation spells alter reality directly, producing illusions, changes in the environment, apparitions, barriers, or other tangible supernatural phenomena.

#### Summoning

Summoning spells call objects, creatures, or forces into immediate presence.

#### Transmutation

Transmutation spells alter form, substance, or physical nature.

#### Ward

A ward spell protects, restores, repels, or punishes intrusion within a protected space or aura.

## Miscasts and Backlash

Magic always courts the possibility of spillover, distortion, and collateral consequence. Any spell or ritual that generates a complication is considered **miscast**.

The intended effect still occurs if the casting succeeded. A miscast does not turn success into failure. Instead, it adds instability, danger, side effects, hostile attention, flawed expression, or some other consequence appropriate to the fiction.

Miscasts should feel different from ordinary complications. They are not merely bad luck. They are signs that the magic got loose around the edges.

The GM is encouraged to make miscasts memorable. A curse might take hold too broadly, a ward might flare visibly, a spirit might hear its name without being called, or a divination might answer truthfully in a form the caster did not want.

## Traditions and Styles

Many roads lead into the supernatural, but most practitioners recognise patterns of doctrine, symbol, method, and expectation that amount to magical traditions. A tradition is not just a spell list. It is an explanation of what magic is, where it comes from, and how a practitioner makes themselves fit to wield it.

This chapter focuses on three traditions.

### Runic Magic

Runic magic draws power through symbols of Germanic and Norse origin. Practitioners, often called spellseers or runeweavers, carve, mark, inscribe, or arrange signs that shape the forces they invoke.

Runic practice tends to be direct, symbolic, and materially anchored. It is well suited to protection, force, fate-laden signs, and compact workings fixed into objects or gestures. Runic spells are detailed in the Runic Spellbook.

### Oracular Magic

Oracular magic concerns vision, insight, portent, hidden meaning, and the interpretation of what lies beyond ordinary sense. It is practised by Roman augurs, Greek oracles, Germanic seiðkonur, and others who treat the world as a field of signs awaiting correct reading.

Oracular practice excels in revelation, concealment, omen, foresight, and contact with truths that are difficult to approach safely. Oracular spells are detailed in the Oracular Spellbook.

### Shamanic Magic

Shamanic magic is rooted in animism, sacred relationship, and the powers of land, season, god, and living world. Its practitioners may be druids, semnothei, vates, or figures recognised under other local names.

Shamanic practice is particularly strong in healing, protection, natural force, and reciprocal dealings with place and spirit. It is often at its most potent when the practitioner is in right relationship with the land and the powers they invoke. Shamanic spells are detailed in the Shamanic Spellbook.

## Learning New Workings

New spells and rituals may only be learned during downtime between adventures. Learning magic is mentally demanding, spiritually invasive, and often accompanied by unsettling dreams, intuitive strain, or attention from things best left undisturbed.

A spellcaster may study only one spell or ritual at a time and may not begin learning a new one until the previous attempt has been fully completed or formally abandoned.

### Traditional Learning

Traditional spellcasters learn directly from a mentor or teacher within their magical tradition. Their method is the most orderly and usually the most efficient.

To learn a new spell or ritual, a traditional spellcaster must:

1. Spend a number of hours in study with a mentor equal to twice the spell or ritual's difficulty. Study may be spread across consecutive days, with a minimum of 2 hours per day. Missing a day requires starting over.
2. Suffer 2 Fatigue for each full day of study. A full day's rest removes this Fatigue.
3. Obtain ingredients, tools, and materials with a combined Restriction rating equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty.
4. Spend 10 XP.
5. Make an Insight + Survival test at a difficulty equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty. On a success, the spell or ritual is learned and available from the next adventure onward. On a failure, the spellcaster must repeat the study period after the next adventure to complete the process.

### Research Learning

Research spellcasters depend on recovered knowledge, commentary, fragments, and extrapolation. Their method is broader but slower and less forgiving.

To learn a new spell or ritual, a research spellcaster must:

1. Spend a number of hours studying research materials equal to four times the spell or ritual's difficulty. Study may be spread across consecutive days, with a minimum of 4 hours per day. Missing a day requires starting over.
2. Suffer 4 Fatigue for each day of study. A full day's rest removes this Fatigue.
3. Obtain ingredients, tools, and materials with a combined Restriction rating equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 1.
4. Spend 10 XP.
5. Make a Reason + Academia test at a difficulty equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 1. On a success, the spell or ritual is learned and available from the next adventure onward. On a failure, the spellcaster must repeat the study period after the next adventure.

### Dabbler Learning

Dabblers learn from fragments, accidents, theft, hearsay, dreams, and dangerous trial and error. Their route is erratic and often personally costly. Non-spellcasters learning rituals use this same process, though they cannot learn spells.

To learn a new spell or ritual, a dabbler must:

1. Spend a number of hours studying equal to a rolled amount determined by the GM, with a minimum of 1 hour per day. Study may be spread across consecutive days, but at least 1 hour must be spent per day. Missing a day requires starting over.
2. Suffer 2 Fatigue per day of study, plus additional Fatigue equal to the number of complications generated during the study period. A full day's rest removes this Fatigue.
3. Obtain ingredients, tools, and materials with a combined Restriction rating equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 2.
4. Spend 10 XP.
5. Make a Willpower + Resilience test at a difficulty equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 1. On a success, the spell or ritual is learned and available from the next adventure. If it is a spell, it is learned in a flawed state. On a failure, the process must be repeated after the next adventure.

A dabbler may repeat the learning process for a flawed spell to remove its flawed status, but must first have cast the spell successfully at least once during play.

### Complications During Learning

Occult study invites trouble even in downtime. In addition to ordinary complication results, the following outcomes are especially suitable when learning magic goes wrong:

- The character suffers 1 Sanity loss as the strain of learning magic taxes mind and spirit.
- The character's study draws the attention of something dangerous and otherworldly. The nature and timing of its arrival are left to the GM.
- The character begins the next adventure with 1 fewer Fate Point, as fortune appears to have turned against them.
- The GM adds to the Doom pool. Because learning occurs between adventures, this Doom is carried forward and added to the pool at the start of the next session.

## Ritual Magic

Rituals are the province of **Thaumaturgy**: long, intricate workings capable of effects far beyond the reach of battlefield spells. They require time, preparation, proper conditions, and sustained concentration. A ritual that is only partially or incompletely known always fails. A complete and reliable account of the ritual's requirements is a prerequisite for any attempt.

Rituals use extended procedures rather than quick casting. Where battlefield magic is loaded into a Focus and released under pressure, ritual magic is assembled step by step through performance, sacrifice, pattern, and endurance.

See [06.9-ritualistic-magic] for the full ritual procedures, including requirements, assistance, sacrifice, stress tracks, steps, abandonment, disruption, and miscast conditions.

> **Rituals: Roleplay vs. Extended Test**  
> The extended test format reflects the sustained effort a ritual demands beyond a single skill test. However, when characters have ample time and circumstances are not especially dangerous, roleplaying a ritual can be equally rewarding. Reserve extended tests for rituals performed under pressure: when time is limited, danger is present, or the outcome is genuinely uncertain.
