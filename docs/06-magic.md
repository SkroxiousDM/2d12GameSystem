# Magic

The use of magic is most often connected to divine favour. It is frequently seen in the hands of Mystics, most of whom are called by the gods; many Mystics also serve as priests. However, characters who crave magical power may discover other routes to it through diligent study and great personal sacrifice.

Magic is divided into two disciplines. **Evocation** (battlefield magic) encompasses spells of immediate effect and brief duration—curses, hexes, charms, blessings, and direct attacks—used primarily in combat and in the field, where controlled conditions are unavailable. **Thaumaturgy** (ritual magic) is longer in form, more intricate, and considerably more powerful. It is the discipline used to communicate with and summon supernatural entities, travel between planes, and effect permanent change on the mortal world.

---

## Types of Spellcaster

The three types of spellcaster each represent a different relationship with magic, with distinct strengths, limitations, and methods of learning.

### Traditional

Traditional spellcasters draw power from an established magical tradition. They begin play with three battlefield spells drawn from their tradition's spellbook.

- May learn spells only from their own magical tradition.
- May perform rituals.
- Learn new spells through study with a mentor.
- Use Insight + Survival when binding spells and making learning tests.
- Power = 2 + INS + Traits.

### Research

Research spellcasters draw on ancient texts, forbidden manuscripts, and the guidance of supernatural entities. They begin play with two battlefield spells drawn from any magical tradition's spellbook.

- May learn spells from any magical tradition.
- May perform rituals.
- Learn new spells by acquiring research materials—typically rare or ancient texts found during adventures and studied during downtime. A research spellcaster may also learn from a mentor or a supernatural entity.
- Some spells may be too deeply embedded in a specific tradition's lore for an outsider to master. The GM determines whether a given spell is accessible.
- Use Reason + Academia when binding spells and making learning tests.
- Power = 2 + REA + Traits.

### Dabbling

Dabblers have pieced together an incomplete understanding of magic through fragmentary texts, eavesdropped rituals, stolen notes, or strange dreams. They begin play with either one standard battlefield spell or two flawed spells drawn from any magical tradition's spellbook.

- May attempt to learn any spell, but each spell learned is flawed (see below).
- May perform rituals.
- Non-spellcasters may also learn rituals using the dabbler's process, though they cannot learn spells.
- Use Willpower + Resilience when binding spells and making learning tests.
- Power = 1 + WIL + Traits.

#### Flawed Spells

When a dabbler learns a spell, they grasp its core principles but not its nuances, making the spell harder to control and less reliable.

- When a flawed spell is cast, it automatically generates one complication in addition to any produced by the skill test. Even a successful cast is treated as a miscast.
- Success Levels specific to the spell's profile cannot be applied when casting a flawed version.
- Some spell profiles include a **Flawed** entry specifying additional adjustments.

To remove a spell's flawed status, the dabbler must cast it successfully at least once during play (not during downtime), then refine their technique through dedicated study during a downtime period. No other significant activities may be undertaken during that downtime.

## Miscast Spells and Rituals

Any spell or ritual that generates a complication is considered miscast. The power channelled through a miscast tears unpredictably at the edges of the caster's control, producing consequences that are more volatile and dangerous than ordinary complications.

The GM is encouraged to approach miscast complications with creativity and invention. Miscasts should feel consequential and memorable.

A miscast does not negate success. If a spell or ritual is cast successfully, its effect still occurs. Complications from a miscast can only produce additional or unintended consequences—they cannot turn a success into a failure.

## Learning New Spells and Rituals

New spells and rituals may only be learned during downtime between adventures. The process is mentally demanding, often accompanied by unsettling dreams, and may require multiple attempts to perfect. A spellcaster may study only one spell or ritual at a time and may not begin learning a new one until the previous has been fully learned or formally abandoned.

### Traditional Spellcasters

Traditional spellcasters learn directly from a mentor or teacher within their magical tradition, receiving structured guidance that makes the process more efficient than it is for other caster types.

To learn a new spell or ritual, a traditional spellcaster must:

1. Spend a number of hours in study with a mentor equal to twice the spell or ritual's difficulty. Study may be spread across consecutive days, with a minimum of 2 hours per day. Missing a day requires starting over.
2. Suffer 2 Fatigue for each full day of study. A full day's rest removes this Fatigue.
3. Obtain ingredients, tools, and materials with a combined Restriction rating equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty.
4. Spend 10 XP.
5. Make an Insight + Survival test at a difficulty equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty. On a success, the spell or ritual is learned and available from the next adventure onward. On a failure, the spellcaster must repeat the study period after the next adventure to complete the process.

### Research Spellcasters

Research spellcasters work largely without structured guidance, relying on cryptic texts or sporadic contact with supernatural entities. Their process takes longer as a result.

To learn a new spell or ritual, a research spellcaster must:

1. Spend a number of hours studying research materials equal to four times the spell or ritual's difficulty. Study may be spread across consecutive days, with a minimum of 4 hours per day. Missing a day requires starting over.
2. Suffer 4 Fatigue for each day of study. A full day's rest removes this Fatigue.
3. Obtain ingredients, tools, and materials with a combined Restriction rating equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 1.
4. Spend 10 XP.
5. Make a Reason + Academia test at a difficulty equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 1. On a success, the spell or ritual is learned and available from the next adventure onward. On a failure, the spellcaster must repeat the study period after the next adventure.

### Dabblers

Dabblers glean their knowledge from fragmentary and unreliable sources: partial scrolls, secondhand accounts, glimpsed notes, or fitful dreams. Their path to learning is unpredictable in duration and taxing in ways that structured traditions are not. Non-spellcasters learning rituals follow the same process, though they cannot learn spells.

To learn a new spell or ritual, a dabbler must:

1. Spend a number of hours studying equal to a rolled amount determined by the GM (minimum 1 hour per day). Study may be spread across consecutive days, but at least 1 hour must be spent per day. Missing a day requires starting over.
2. Suffer 2 Fatigue per day of study, plus additional Fatigue equal to the number of complications generated during the study period. A full day's rest removes this Fatigue.
3. Obtain ingredients, tools, and materials with a combined Restriction rating equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 2.
4. Spend 10 XP.
5. Make a Willpower + Resilience test at a difficulty equal to the spell or ritual's difficulty + 1. On a success, the spell or ritual is learned and available from the next adventure. If it is a spell, it is learned in a flawed state. On a failure, the process must be repeated after the next adventure.

A dabbler may repeat the learning process for a flawed spell to remove its flawed status. They must have successfully cast the spell at least once during play before attempting to refine it.

#### Complications During Learning

Because learning requires a skill test, complications are possible. In addition to standard complication results, the following are particularly fitting for the hazards of occult study:

- The character suffers 1 Sanity loss as the strain of learning magic taxes their mind and spirit.
- The character's study has drawn the attention of something dangerous and otherworldly. The nature and timing of its arrival are left to the GM's discretion.
- The character begins the next adventure with 1 fewer Fate Point, as fortune appears to have turned against them.
- The GM adds to the Doom pool. Because learning occurs between adventures, this Doom is carried forward and added to the pool at the start of the next session.

## Casting Spells and Performing Rituals

Unless otherwise noted in a spell's profile, casting a spell requires a skill test during a standard scene, or the **Cast a Spell** major action during an action scene. A major action costs 2 Actions from the caster's Alacrity pool (see Combat). Rituals take considerably more time than spells; completing one is treated as an extended test.

The attribute used for any spell or ritual test depends on caster type: traditional spellcasters use Insight, research spellcasters use Reason, and dabblers use Willpower. The same applies to ritualists who are not spellcasters. The skill required is listed in the spell or ritual's profile.

### Magic Skills

Which skill a spell or ritual requires depends on its nature and the circumstances of its use. Several skills have specialisations that lend themselves to occult applications.

- **Academia (Religion)** is a common route into the study of the arcane and the supernatural, as the practice of magic is often intertwined with religious ritual and sacrifice to the gods.
- **Academia (Linguistics)** is another common route, as scholars of language are often versed in dead tongues, enabling them to translate ancient religious texts—though they may not be prepared for the consequences.
- **Fighting (War Magic)** arises from zealously pious martial traditions, in which those who carry the blessings of the gods are expected to fight in battle and risk death.
- **Medicine (Hieratic Healing)** covers rites, rituals, and appropriate offerings through which a patron god or other supernatural being is induced to intercede on behalf of the sick or injured — spending, on the healer's behalf, a measure of the worship-harvest it has gathered from its faithful.
- **Observation (Instincts)** covers perception beyond the traditional senses. Characters with this specialisation are often sensitive to unnatural phenomena and can discern the influence of the supernatural more keenly than others.
- **Manipulation (Invocation)** encompasses unusual and esoteric methods of communicating with unnatural entities. It is also applicable to magic drawing on emotion or reason, and is the arcane tool of those who seek to rouse or calm, or to evoke awe or fear through the spoken word.
- **Resilience (Discipline)** is invaluable for enduring some of the nastier effects of dabbling in the occult.
- **Survival (Mysticism)** deals with spells and rituals passed down through ancient oral traditions rather than books and scrolls, particularly those connected to forces of nature or the wilderness.
- **Tactics (Omen Reading)** covers practices that involve reading signs in the natural world and interpreting the future from them. Countless undertakings are preceded by a reading of omens.

Possessing one of these specialisations reflects knowledge useful for dealing with the occult, but it does not confer the ability to cast spells. Casting battlefield spells requires a Talent with the Spellcaster keyword. Rituals do not share this requirement.

### Power

Power represents a character's reserves for casting spells and performing rituals. All spellcasters have a Power rating that indicates the number of d3 rolled when their character casts a spell or completes a ritual step. Power is calculated as follows:

- **Traditional**: Power = 2 + INS + Traits
- **Research**: Power = 2 + REA + Traits
- **Dabbling**: Power = 1 + WIL + Traits (can increase Base to 3 whenever they roll a magic test in exchange for generating 1 Doom)

Some Talents and other abilities may increase a spellcaster's Power rating under specific circumstances.

### Spell and Ritual Stress

Not all spells and rituals deal Stress to their targets, though most deal Stress to the caster. Of those that do affect targets, some deal physical Stress (reduced by Defence) and some deal mental Stress (reduced by Courage). The type of Stress a spell or ritual inflicts is specified in its profile.

When a spell or ritual that deals Stress is successfully cast, resolve it as follows:

1. **Roll Power**: Roll a number of d3 equal to your Power rating, plus any additional dice specified by the spell. Total the results, then subtract the number of dice rolled. This is the amount of Stress dealt. A spell that deals damage equal to *n*d3 is resolved as **nd3 − n**, matching the damage convention used throughout the game (see Weapons).
2. **Determine Resistance**: The target reduces incoming Stress by their relevant resistance—Defence for physical Stress, Courage for mental Stress.
3. **Inflict Stress**: The target suffers any remaining Stress against Vitality or Resolve, as appropriate.

> **Example.** A traditional spellseer with Power 5 casts a power+2 attack spell. They roll 7d3 and total the results, then subtract 7. A roll of 2, 3, 1, 2, 3, 2, 1 totals 14 and yields 7 Stress before resistance. The target's Defence further reduces this Stress before it is applied.

### Success Level Spends in Magic

Skilled practitioners can leverage their expertise to achieve heightened results. Some spell and ritual profiles specify additional ways to spend Success Levels. In all cases, spellcasters may also spend Success Levels on the following:

- **Reduce Cost**: For each Success Level spent, gain +1 Courage resistance against the spell or ritual's casting cost.
- **Increase Stress**: For each Success Level spent, add +1 to the Stress dealt to targets.
- **Extend Duration**: For 2 Success Levels, double the duration of any spell or ritual with a duration other than instant.

## Battlefield Magic

Battlefield magic brings immediate power to bear in the field—blessings and curses, direct attacks, hexes and wards—where there is no time for careful preparation. While less potent than ritual workings, it is far faster to deploy and may be turned to situations beyond combat where a ritual's lengthy casting time would be impractical.

### Binding Spells

Spells must be bound to a Focus before they can be cast. A Focus is a totemic object or significant item—a wand, runestone, staff, or similar implement—that a spellcaster uses to store prepared spells, allowing them to be released quickly when needed. A bound spell remains in the Focus for as long as the spellcaster remains conscious. If rendered unconscious, bound spells dissipate safely without miscasting.

A spellcaster cannot cast an unbound spell. A spellcaster may hold a number of bound spells equal to their Power rating. Each bound spell may be cast any number of times, though each casting carries a cost as detailed in the spell's profile.

**Binding Procedure.** Binding spells to a Focus requires a Challenging skill test and takes a number of minutes equal to the combined difficulty of all spells being prepared. The skill used depends on caster type: Traditional spellcasters use Insight + Survival; Research spellcasters use Reason + Academia; Dabblers use Willpower + Resilience. Once bound, spells remain in the Focus until the spellcaster sleeps or is rendered unconscious.

> **What Is a Focus?**
> A Focus can be thought of as a metaphysical anchor through which a spellcaster channels prepared magic. Though its effective core is intangible, spellcasters invariably possess a physical object—a wand, staff, runestone, or totem—to bridge the gap between the physical world and their magic. What matters is its significance to the character and how they practise magic, not any inherent cultural or historical properties. Normally, your character finds or creates their Focus.

### Casting a Battlefield Spell

During a scene, a spellcaster may attempt to cast any of the spells currently bound to their Focus. A spellcaster may cast at most one spell per turn. In an action scene, the spellcaster takes the **Cast a Spell** major action, which costs 2 Actions from their Alacrity pool. Each casting requires the skill test specified in the spell's profile.

The attribute used for the casting test depends on caster type: Traditional spellcasters use Insight, research spellcasters use Reason, and dabblers use Willpower.

### Resisting Magic

Magic is potent, but it is not absolute. Certain spells give their targets the opportunity to resist. The casting test for such a spell is an **opposed test**. The caster uses their spellcasting attribute and the spell's listed skill. The target uses the attribute and skill specified in the spell's Difficulty entry. Both tests use the spell's difficulty.

### Spell Format

Each battlefield spell is defined by the following entries.

- **Skill**: The skill used on tests to cast this spell. The attribute is determined by the caster's spellcaster type.
- **Difficulty**: The difficulty of the casting test. If the test is opposed, this entry also specifies the attribute and skill the resisting character must use.
- **Cost**: The mental Stress the spellcaster suffers upon casting, applied after the casting test regardless of success or failure. This Stress is reduced by the caster's Courage as normal.
- **Duration**: How long the spell's effects last. Most spells are instant. Others last a number of rounds, until end of scene, or a number of hours. A spellcaster may dismiss a spell early as a minor action (1 Action); otherwise it ends at the close of its stated duration, or if the caster is Staggered or forcibly distracted.
- **Category**: The spell's category or categories (see below).
- **Spell Effect**: The consequences of a successful casting, including valid targets and any Success Level options that expand or enhance the effect.
- **Flawed** *(if present)*: Adjustments that apply when casting a flawed version of this spell.
- **Success Levels** *(if present)*: Additional benefits available by spending Success Levels.

### Spell Categories

Spells are grouped into categories based on the type of result they achieve. A spell may belong to more than one category and gains the rules of each.

##### Attack

Attack spells inflict physical or mental Stress, typically through a deadly projection or psychological assault. When successfully cast, the spell's Stress is resolved using the caster's Power rating.

##### Banishment

Banishment spells inflict Stress as attack spells do, but are especially effective against supernatural entities. If a banishment spell inflicts 1 or more Critical Wounds on a supernatural creature, that creature loses its next turn as it struggles to remain in this world. If a supernatural target is defeated by a banishment spell, it vanishes immediately, cast back whence it came.

##### Blessing

A blessing spell infuses its target with magical energy, granting some benefit or enhancement to their capabilities.

##### Control

A control spell inflicts mental Stress, undermining the mind of its target. If a control spell causes the target to lose their last point of Resolve, the target is placed under the spellcaster's control as detailed in the spell's profile rather than suffering a Madness.

##### Curse

Curse spells infuse their targets with energies that hinder them. Each curse spell's profile describes how the curse can be broken—this might be as simple as passing a skill test, or it might require considerable time and effort.

##### Divination

Divination spells provide the spellcaster with knowledge. All spells in this category allow the spellcaster to ask questions that could not otherwise be answered, though the scope of allowable queries varies by spell.

##### Manifestation

Manifestation spells alter the fabric of reality, producing effects ranging from vivid illusions to major environmental changes.

##### Summoning

Summoning spells are powerful invocations that call objects, creatures, or phenomena to materialise out of thin air.

##### Transmutation

Transmutation spells alter a subject's physical form, transforming them into a new shape or changing their fundamental nature.

##### Ward

A ward spell provides protection or restoration, or inflicts Stress on creatures that come within range of its protective aura.

## Magical Traditions

There are myriad magical traditions. This chapter focuses on three: the Runic magic of the Germanic peoples, the Oracular magic favoured by Rome's priesthood and Germanic seiðkonur, and the Druidic magic of the druids. Some spellcasters draw from a single tradition; others may draw from any, as detailed above.

**Runic magic** draws power from runic symbols of Germanic and Norse origin. Practitioners—known as spellseers or runeweavers—use runes to focus magical energies. Though rare, they are found among the tribes of Germania Magna and wherever those tribes have settled. Runic spells are detailed in the Runic Spellbook.

**Oracular magic** allows spellcasters to perceive future events, understand things beyond the reach of ordinary senses, and interpret the fates of those around them. Whether practised by Roman augurs, Greek oracles, or Germanic seiðkonur, it is often associated with figures such as Mimir or Antevorta. Oracular spells are detailed in the Oracular Spellbook.

**Druidic magic** is rooted in animistic beliefs and draws power from both the Druidic pantheon and the forces of the natural world. Practitioners—referred to as druids, semnothei, or vates—may hail from Hibernia, Britannia, Gaul, or Germania. This tradition is strong in healing and protection, but its practitioners can also command raw forces of natural destruction. Druidic spells are detailed in the Druidic Spellbook.

## Rituals

Rituals are the province of Thaumaturgy—long, intricate workings capable of effects far beyond the reach of battlefield spells. They require significant time, preparation, and sustained concentration. A ritual that is only partially or incompletely known always fails; a complete and reliable account of the ritual's requirements is a prerequisite for any attempt.

> **Rituals: Roleplay vs. Extended Test**
> The extended test format reflects the sustained effort a ritual demands beyond a single skill test. However, when characters have ample time and circumstances are not especially dangerous, roleplaying a ritual can be equally rewarding. Reserve extended tests for rituals performed under pressure: when time is limited, danger is present, or the outcome is genuinely uncertain.
