# Pursuit

A pursuit can begin directly from a roleplaying scene, or it can follow immediately after combat if one side breaks and runs. In either case, the GM declares that a pursuit is now in progress and sets the starting range between the participants, using the normal range bands as a reference, up to a maximum of **Long** range.

Distances in a pursuit are measured only in range bands, not in exact feet or yards. Most pursuits are between two parties: one fleeing, one chasing. Larger scenes with multiple riders, footmen, or quarry can use the multiple-participant rules on the sidebar.

## Resolution

Pursuits are played out in rounds just like combat, but no initiative is drawn and no normal movement or attacks are resolved in the usual way. Instead, each participant gets one maneuver each round, and the GM generates or reveals an obstacle to shape the scene.

Each pursuit round follows these steps:

1. Each participant secretly chooses a maneuver.
2. The GM generates or reveals a pursuit obstacle.
3. The participants reveal their maneuvers.
4. Maneuvers are resolved, with the prey acting first if both sides would otherwise be tied.

A player or NPC may cancel a planned maneuver, but no other maneuver may be chosen instead; the participant simply does nothing for that round.

## Pursuit maneuvers

| Maneuver | Eligibility | Skill | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pursue / Flee** | Pursuer / prey | Athletics, Survival, or mounted movement as appropriate | Decrease or increase range by one step. Immediate hand-to-hand combat occurs if range becomes **Engaged** at the end of the round. Chasing over open ground favors the pursuer, while rough ground, crowds, and barriers may hinder either side. |
| **Hide** | Prey | Stealth vs. Observation | Break line of sight, vanish into cover, or disappear into terrain or crowd. Can only be done at **Medium** range or more. Disadvantage at **Medium** range, advantage at **Extreme**. |
| **Block** | Prey | Might, Tactics, or a fitting craft or social test | Increase range one step, or force the pursuer to overcome an obstruction before continuing. Used to close gates, topple carts, bar doors, raise a shield wall, or force a mounted chase into bad footing. |
| **Cut Off** | Pursuer | Athletics, Survival, or Tactics | Take a shortcut, cross a ford, move through a side lane, or intercept by terrain knowledge. This can trap the prey, reduce range, or force a worse route. |
| **Stand and Shoot** | Pursuer / prey | Ranged Combat, Archery, or Thrown Weapons | Make a ranged attack during the pursuit. |
| **Other** | Pursuer / prey | Varies | Any plausible pursuit action not covered above. |

## Chase obstacles

At the end of each round, after the participants have chosen their maneuvers, the GM randomly generates or selects a pursuit obstacle. Obstacles can be interpreted broadly to suit the scene: a crowded market, muddy road, narrow bridge, stable yard, castle gate, or wooded trail.

The obstacle provides color for the scene and can also affect the maneuvers chosen by the participants. The GM is free to adapt the obstacle list to the location and can create new obstacles as needed.

### Foot pursuit obstacles

| D12 | Obstacle |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Dead end and shut gate.** If the prey has chosen to Pursue / Flee, the maneuver fails automatically. |
| 2 | **Market crowd or festival throng.** The lane is packed with people, stalls, carts, and animals moving slowly in all directions. This gives an advantage to Hide, but a disadvantage to Pursue / Flee and Stand and Shoot. |
| 3 | **Timber wall, shopfront, or stable yard blocks the way.** If the prey has chosen to Block, Hide, or Cut Off, they must first make a suitable test to climb, break through, or squeeze past. If they fail, the maneuver is cancelled and the prey suffers delay or damage as appropriate. |
| 4 | **Narrow bridge over a ditch, stream, or moat.** This gives a disadvantage to Pursue / Flee and Stand and Shoot, but an advantage to Cut Off. |
| 5 | **Mud, cobbles, or ice make footing treacherous.** Any maneuver that depends on speed suffers disadvantage, while Block may gain advantage if the prey can force the chasers to stumble. |
| 6 | **Cartload of hay, barrels, or fish spills across the road.** Pursuers must test to keep pace, while the prey may gain advantage on Hide or Cut Off. |
| 7 | **Guard patrol or levy spearmen appear.** This gives a disadvantage to Pursue / Flee and Stand and Shoot unless the chasers can identify themselves, bribe the patrol, or bluff their way through. |
| 8 | **Barking dog, crowing rooster, or ringing bell breaks the silence.** The prey must remain alert or risk being revealed. Pursuers may gain advantage on Observation or Cut Off. |
| 9 | **Rubble, fallen timber, or broken fencing litters the route.** This is difficult terrain and favors Hide or Block. |
| 10 | **Hidden alley, courtyard, or herb garden offers a turn in the route.** The chase tightens and one side may gain advantage to Hide or Cut Off depending on who knows the terrain. |
| 11 | **Ladder, wall, or porch offers a vertical route.** The prey must roll Athletics or Climbing-related checks; failure means the maneuver fails automatically. |
| 12 | **Local crowd mistakes the prey for a servant, pilgrim, or retainer.** This gives advantage to Hide and disadvantage to Cut Off, unless the pursuers are known to the locals. |

### Mounted pursuit obstacles

| D12 | Obstacle |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Dead ground and a fallen rider.** If the prey has chosen to Pursue / Flee, the maneuver fails automatically. |
| 2 | **Fenced pasture or line of livestock blocks the route.** Each rider must make a suitable riding or animal-handling test to avoid being slowed. A failed test means the round fails. |
| 3 | **Ford, stream, or slick riverbank makes the horses hesitate.** The chase becomes difficult and any maneuver relying on speed suffers disadvantage. |
| 4 | **Woodline or hedge maze cuts visibility.** This gives advantage to Hide, but a disadvantage to Pursue / Flee and Stand and Shoot. |
| 5 | **Ruined wall, ditch, or trench interrupts the route.** This gives an advantage to Cut Off, but a disadvantage to Pursue / Flee and Block unless the rider can jump it. |
| 6 | **Gatehouse, toll station, or narrow bridge creates a chokepoint.** This gives an advantage to Block, but the chasers may need to slow and dismount to pass. |
| 7 | **Mounted guard patrol or caravan escort joins the route.** This gives a disadvantage to Pursue / Flee, Hide, and Cut Off unless the chasers can identify themselves or force passage. |
| 8 | **Broad field or hard-packed causeway opens the road.** This gives an advantage to Pursue / Flee and Stand and Shoot, but a disadvantage to Hide. |
| 9 | **Low wall, ditch, or hedgerow can be ridden or vaulted.** This gives an advantage to Cut Off and Hide if the riders know the land, but a disadvantage to Block. |
| 10 | **Shepherd, drover, or peasant wagon is forced into the lane.** The chasing riders must avoid collision or lose ground. |
| 11 | **Sudden slope, ravine edge, or uneven ground threatens a fall.** The prey or pursuer must make a riding or balance test to stay in the chase. |
| 12 | **Bridge, ferry, or drawbridge changes the route.** Failure to choose correctly may end the pursuit or force a dangerous crossing. |

## Mounted ranged attacks

Passengers may make ranged attacks from a mount or wagon during a pursuit, and those attacks are resolved immediately after the rider’s maneuver is resolved. However, such attacks normally suffer disadvantage if the rider performed any maneuver other than Stand and Shoot.

More disciplined cavalry, mounted archers, or a knight with a trained mount may ignore that penalty when the situation supports it. The GM decides when that is plausible.

## Ending the pursuit

A pursuit can end because of maneuver results, a successful obstacle, or because the prey chooses to stop running and stand their ground. It can also end at the beginning of a new round if the quarry reaches safety, loses the pursuers, or is cornered in a place with no reasonable escape.

If the prey chooses to stop and fight, draw initiative and commence combat as normal. If they are caught at a dead end, gatehouse, bridge, or other sealed route, the pursuit may end with immediate confrontation.

## Multiple participants

If there are more than two participants in a pursuit, there are two practical ways to resolve it. The simplest is to divide the participants into two sides and have each side perform maneuvers and rolls separately. You then track the relative distance between each participant and the person they are pursuing or protecting.

A more detailed method is to let each participant choose maneuvers independently, but that requires more tracking and is best for important scenes such as a lord’s carriage fleeing through town, a thief pursued over rooftops, or a mounted hunt across open country. In that case, keep notes of who is ahead, who is behind, and who is able to cut off another rider or footman.
