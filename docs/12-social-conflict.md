## Social Conflict

Not every confrontation is resolved with steel. Social Conflict covers any situation where one character wishes to obtain something from another through words, presence, leverage, or fear—and the other party is unwilling to simply comply. At its heart, a Social Conflict is a request: you want something, and someone either will not grant it or is standing between you and your goal.

The GM may decide that persuading a cooperative guard or inspiring a loyal follower does not warrant a full Social Conflict. In such cases, a single Skill Check using the appropriate Attribute and Skill is sufficient.

### Consent and Reasonable Requests

The base Difficulty of the active character's Check in a Social Conflict reflects how reasonable their request is—a judgment made by the GM. If a request is something the target would never do under any circumstances, the Check becomes impossible and counts as a failure. The active character cannot try again without first changing the context to make the request more agreeable. This rule also ensures that if there are lines the GM or a player does not wish to cross, outright refusal is always available.

### Resolving a Social Conflict

A Social Conflict is resolved as an Opposed Check. The GM determines the relevant Attribute and Skill for each participant. The outcome depends on both results:

- **Active character passes, reactive character fails:** The reactive character concedes and goes along with the request to a reasonable degree. If doing so would have dire consequences, their attitude shifts to acknowledge the argument's weight—they may offer a compromise or alternative.  
- **Active character fails, reactive character passes:** The request is denied. The active character cannot make the same request again without changing the context.  
- **Both characters fail:** The request is denied, but the active character may try again without changing the context.  
- **Both characters pass:** Compare Levels of Success. The character with the higher total wins. If the active character wins, the request is granted to a reasonable degree. If the reactive character wins, the request is denied and the active character cannot try again without changing the context. The winner loses Levels of Success equal to what their opponent generated; the loser discards all their Levels of Success and may not spend them. On a tie, the active character wins but generates no Levels of Success.

### Attributes and Skills

Charisma is the most common Attribute for Social Conflicts, but others may apply. Reason suits logical argument; Insight suits careful observation and reading of people; Willpower suits raw intimidation and force of personality. Manipulation is the most natural Skill, but others may fit the context—a debate between scholars might call for Academia, while a dispute over a diagnosis might involve Medicine.

### Extended Social Conflicts

Some targets are particularly defiant or the situation particularly complex. In such cases the GM may treat the Social Conflict as an Extended Check, requiring accumulated Levels of Success over multiple exchanges before the matter is resolved.

### Failure and Lasting Defeat

A reactive character who fails a Social Conflict stops being an obstacle in that regard for the rest of the scene—they agree to what was asked. However, they may reconsider if someone else intervenes; a guard who lets you pass might think twice when a superior officer appears.

A **Lasting Social Defeat** is more serious. Rather than mere failure in a single exchange, it casts a character out of a social circle or faction, cutting them off from their resources and relationships. Recovery is possible but requires meaningful effort and time.

## Social Superiority

Social Conflicts follow the same Superiority framework as combat. There are three ways to build Superiority before a Social Conflict begins.

**Know Thy Foe.** Vital or secret information about the target grants \+1 Superiority per piece of intelligence. Unlike other forms of preparation, *Know Thy Foe* stacks in Social Conflicts—each separate secret contributes independently. Useful examples include:

- The target is in serious financial difficulty and desperate for allies.  
- A rival or enemy of the target is nearby, and the target knows it.  
- A major endeavour of the target has recently collapsed, possibly without their knowledge.

**Know Thy Battlefield.** A thorough understanding of the circumstances surrounding the conflict grants +1 Superiority. Relevant intelligence might include:  
A threat the target is unaware of that will soon affect their situation directly.  
Significant unrest or upheaval about to disrupt something the target has planned.

**Know Thy Players.** Inspired preparation tailored to the specific conflict may earn \+1 Superiority at the GM's discretion. Bringing a well-armed escort to an intimidation, arriving with a conspicuously heavy purse, or carrying medicine for an ailing opponent are all examples of preparation that changes the shape of a negotiation before a word is spoken.

### Gaining and Losing Social Superiority  
Social Superiority may increase during a conflict when you win an Opposed Check using a social Skill or specialization with Levels of Success equal to or greater than the relevant Attribute of your opponent. For example, winning a Manipulation (Intimidation) check against an opponent's Resilience (Discipline) grants \+1 Superiority if your Levels of Success meet or exceed the opponent's Willpower.  
Social Superiority is lost when you fail an Opposed Check using a social Skill or specialization with negative Levels of Success equal to or greater than your own relevant Attribute.

### Spending Social Superiority  
Social Superiority works differently from Combat Superiority—it is actively spent rather than passively applied, and decreases as it is used.  
Spending 1 Superiority grants \+2 Levels of Success on a social Check. That point of Superiority is then lost.  
If you spend Superiority equal to or greater than the target's Resolve in a single Check, you also gain Advantage on that Check in addition to the bonus Levels of Success.  
Superiority must be spent in ways that make narrative sense. If leveraging knowledge that a merchant is near bankruptcy, for example, the party might spend it to:  
Use Reason \+ Manipulation (Intimidation) to threaten financial ruin unless the merchant cooperates.  
Use Charisma \+ Manipulation (Charm) to propose a mutually beneficial arrangement that puts money back in their pockets.
 
## Social Tools  
Before or during a Social Conflict, characters may employ Social Tools to shift the Difficulty of Checks in their favour, make requests seem more reasonable, or reopen avenues that have already been closed. Using one or more Social Tools effectively can reduce the Difficulty of a Check or change the context enough to allow a fresh attempt.

#### Deception  
Deception is the art of convincing someone that something false is true. It can be used alone to make a request seem more agreeable, or combined with other Social Tools to fabricate threats, invent bargains, or forge evidence.  
Deception is resolved as an Opposed Check. The Difficulty for the active character is based on the believability of the lie; for the reactive character, it is based on their suspicion. A successful deception causes the reactive character to gain a personal truth such as Trusts \[Character\]. If they later discover the lie, that truth becomes Distrusts \[Character\], increasing the Difficulty of future Social Conflicts against them and potentially motivating retaliation. Complications rolled while lying may expose flaws in the story, making the target more suspicious.

#### Evidence  
Concrete evidence is often the most persuasive tool available. Each piece of evidence is a truth—typically something like Official Documents—and reduces the Difficulty of relevant Checks in the normal manner for truths.  
If the reactive character doubts the evidence's authenticity, the active character may first need to pass a Check to explain or verify it before it takes effect. Evidence can reinforce any other Social Tool: proof of capability makes a threat more credible, documentation of wealth strengthens a negotiation, and convincing forgeries lend weight to deception.

#### Intimidation  
Intimidation uses the threat of harm or severe consequence to compel compliance. It is resolved as an Opposed Check and may use the Fighting Skill if the threat involves physical force.  
A successful intimidation makes the target more cooperative in the immediate term. Failure means the attempt has been called and cannot be repeated in that scene—a hollow threat becomes hollower the second time. The GM may permit further intimidation if a genuinely new and credible threat can be made.  
Intimidation is a hostile act. Even when successful, it leaves a residue of fear and resentment. Once the immediate effects have passed, the target remembers what was said and acts accordingly.

#### Negotiation  
Negotiation creates a transaction: you offer something in exchange for what you want. The compensation need not be coin—favours, information, expertise, and protection all have value, and finding the right offer is often the heart of the exchange.  
A successful negotiation is represented as a truth such as A Bribe of 30 Denarii or A Favour from the Magistrate. If you fail, you must sweeten the offer to try again. The obligation runs both ways—once you have negotiated something, you are expected to pay up. Failing to do so creates its own complications, particularly if what you offered was not entirely yours to give.

#### Reading a Target  
Understanding who you are dealing with is often the decisive advantage in a Social Conflict. An Insight- or Reason-based Check using Academia, Observation, or Manipulation can reveal useful information about a target either in advance or through direct interaction.  
The base Difficulty for this Check is Routine, increasing if the target is secretive or guarded. If they suspect someone is trying to read them, it may become an Opposed Check. Levels of Success are used to ask questions as per the Obtain Information option.  
Useful questions include:  
- What do they fear?  
- What do they seek to protect?  
- What do they desire?  
- What do they believe?  
Questions about background, history, and reputation may also yield actionable insight. Information gained this way reduces the Difficulty of your subsequent Checks in the Social Conflict by an amount the GM determines.
