# Social Conflict

Not every confrontation is resolved with steel. Social Conflict covers those moments when one character seeks to obtain something from another through words, presence, leverage, deception, inducement, or fear, and the other party is unwilling to simply comply. At its heart, a Social Conflict is a struggle over intent: one side wants something to change, and the other side resists that change.

This chapter presents Social Conflict as a structured exchange of pressure, positioning, and advantage. It uses the same underlying principles that govern combat—clear stakes, momentum, preparation, and collapse of resistance—but expresses them through status, leverage, and will rather than blades and blood. It retains the core procedures of the existing system: Opposed Checks, reasonable requests, Social Superiority, Resolve, and Social Tools.

Not every conversation requires a full Social Conflict. If the other party is already willing, if the matter is minor, or if the outcome is not meaningfully contested, the GM may call for a single Check or simply rule success. Use Social Conflict when the request matters, the target resists, and the consequences of success or failure are important.

## Intent and Stakes

Before any dice are rolled, the active character must state clearly what they want. A Social Conflict is not idle conversation. It is a request, demand, proposal, warning, plea, threat, bargain, or argument aimed at changing another person's behaviour, belief, or decision.

The GM should also establish what is at risk. One party might wish to gain access to a restricted archive, convince a magistrate to delay a hanging, force a gang lieutenant to reveal who sent him, secure a loan from a hostile merchant, or persuade a suspicious elder to offer shelter. The reactive character, meanwhile, may risk loss of face, money, safety, loyalty, authority, or standing if they give way.

When both sides understand what success and failure mean, the conflict becomes much easier to run. A scene in which the party seeks to "convince the duke" is vague; a scene in which they are trying to make the duke commit troops tonight, in front of his rivals, at the cost of his reputation if he appears weak, is a Social Conflict.

## Consent and Reasonable Requests

The base Difficulty of the active character's Check reflects how reasonable the request is, as judged by the GM. A target may be persuaded to do something inconvenient, dangerous, expensive, or embarrassing if the argument is strong enough or the circumstances are right. They cannot be persuaded to do something that they would never do under any circumstances. In such a case, the Check is impossible and counts as a failure.

The active character may not attempt the same request again without changing the context enough to make it meaningfully more plausible. This may require new leverage, new information, a sweeter offer, a stronger threat, different timing, or some other shift in circumstances. This rule protects the integrity of the fiction and ensures that refusal remains possible where it should.

This principle also applies at the table. If the GM or a player does not wish a subject to be negotiated through play, outright refusal remains available. Social Conflict is a system for dramatic pressure, not a tool for overriding boundaries.

## Structure of a Social Conflict

A Social Conflict is usually resolved as an Opposed Check between an active character and a reactive character. The GM determines the most relevant Attribute and Skill for each side based on what they are actually doing. Charisma and Manipulation are common, but they are far from the only options.

A precise legal argument might call for Reason and Academia. Reading a room and choosing the right emotional angle may rely on Insight and Observation. Raw force of personality, discipline, and refusal to be cowed may use Willpower or Resilience. If a physician is arguing over treatment, Medicine may matter more than charm. The system works best when the chosen mechanics reflect the actual method being used, not merely the desired outcome.

The outcome depends on both results:

- If the active character passes and the reactive character fails, the reactive character concedes and goes along with the request to a reasonable degree.
- If the active character fails and the reactive character passes, the request is denied, and the active character cannot make the same request again without changing the context.
- If both fail, the request is denied, but the active character may try again without changing the context.
- If both pass, compare Levels of Success. The higher total wins. If the active character wins, the request is granted to a reasonable degree. If the reactive character wins, the request is denied and the active character cannot try again without changing the context. On a tie, the active character wins but generates no Levels of Success.

When both characters pass, the winner loses Levels of Success equal to what their opponent generated, and the loser discards all their Levels of Success and may not spend them. This preserves the sense that even a successful exchange costs effort and that resistance still matters.

## Extended Social Conflicts

Some targets are stubborn, powerful, insulated, or deeply committed. Some situations are too complicated to resolve with a single exchange. In such cases, the GM may run the matter as an Extended Social Conflict, requiring accumulated Levels of Success over multiple exchanges before the issue is settled.

An Extended Social Conflict should be used when the scene deserves room to breathe: a debate before a council, negotiations over hostages, a blackmail attempt against a cautious noble, a trial, or the gradual breakdown of a prisoner's resistance. In such scenes, each exchange should change the fiction. New allies may enter, evidence may be introduced, tempers may rise, and failed tactics may close certain doors while opening others.

The active side should not be allowed to repeat the same argument indefinitely. Each new attempt in an extended conflict ought to represent a new angle, new pressure, or newly exploited leverage.

## Social Superiority

Social Conflicts use the same broad idea of Superiority as combat, but expressed differently. In combat, Superiority is the party's momentum on the battlefield. In Social Conflict, it represents leverage, preparation, insight, timing, and control of the situation.

Unlike Combat Superiority, Social Superiority is spent actively and decreases as it is used. It is built through secrets, preparation, and successful pressure, then converted into decisive advantage at the right moment.

### Gaining Social Superiority Before the Conflict

There are three principal ways to build Social Superiority before a Social Conflict begins.

**Know Thy Foe.** Vital or secret information about the target grants +1 Superiority per piece of meaningful intelligence. Unlike combat preparation, this stacks in Social Conflicts: each separate secret can matter independently. Knowing that a merchant is near bankruptcy, that a priest fears scandal, or that a magistrate's son is missing are all forms of leverage that change what can be said and what risks can be taken.

**Know Thy Battlefield.** A thorough understanding of the surrounding circumstances grants +1 Superiority. This includes knowing who is present, what pressures are building, which rumours are spreading, what deadlines loom, and what consequences will soon fall on the target whether they cooperate or not.

**Know Thy Players.** Inspired preparation tailored to the specific conflict may grant +1 Superiority at the GM's discretion. Arriving with a well-armed escort before an intimidation attempt, carrying ready coin to support a bribe, bringing a physician to a bedside negotiation, or ensuring that a rival witness is waiting outside are all preparations that change the social field before a word is spoken.

### Gaining and Losing Social Superiority During the Conflict

Social Superiority may increase when you win an Opposed Check using a social Skill or Specialisation with Levels of Success equal to or greater than the relevant Attribute of your opponent. For example, winning a Manipulation (Intimidation) check against an opponent's Resilience (Discipline) grants +1 Superiority if your Levels of Success meet or exceed the opponent's Willpower.

Social Superiority is lost when you fail an Opposed Check using a social Skill or Specialisation with negative Levels of Success equal to or greater than your own relevant Attribute. In play, this means overreaching, being publicly embarrassed, misreading the room, or pressing a line of argument so badly that it harms your position.

### Spending Social Superiority

Spending 1 Social Superiority grants +2 Levels of Success on a social Check. That point of Superiority is then lost.

If you spend Social Superiority equal to or greater than the target's Resolve in a single Check, you also gain Advantage on that Check. This represents the moment when accumulated pressure, leverage, and timing line up so strongly that the target begins to buckle.

Superiority must always be spent in a way that makes narrative sense. If the characters know a merchant is close to ruin, they might spend that leverage to threaten financial exposure, to offer a lifeline in exchange for cooperation, or to imply that refusal will leave the merchant isolated. They may not spend it on an unrelated line of argument that the knowledge does not support.

## Resolve in Social Conflict

Resolve in Social Conflict represents a character's willingness to hold their position, endure pressure, and refuse the requested change. It is not mere stubbornness. A person with high Resolve may still be emotional, frightened, or tempted, but they do not yield easily. A person with low Resolve may not be weak-willed so much as overextended, compromised, unsupported, or already halfway persuaded.

The GM should think of Resolve as situational as much as personal. A guildmaster with allies at her back has more Resolve in public than in private. A corrupt official has less Resolve when faced with records of his theft. A grieving parent may have little Resolve against an offer of help but much more against a threat. This makes Social Conflict feel grounded in circumstance rather than reduced to a single static number.

While Social Conflict does not normally use the Desperate condition in exactly the same formal way as combat, the same principle applies. When one side has accumulated overwhelming Superiority against a target's Resolve, the GM should begin to bring the matter to a decision. The target may concede, compromise, deflect, seek escape from the conversation, call guards, or lash out in a final act of pride or fear. Social defeat, like battlefield defeat, often arrives before a total collapse.

## Social Tools

Before or during a Social Conflict, characters may use Social Tools to make a request more reasonable, reduce Difficulty, reopen closed avenues, or shift the conflict onto more favourable ground. A Social Tool is not separate from the conflict. It is one of the main ways the context changes.

A failed request may often become possible again if the active character first uses one or more Social Tools effectively. This is how social scenes stay dynamic. The answer "no" should not always end the matter, but neither should it evaporate without cost.

### Deception

Deception is the art of convincing someone that something false is true. It may be used on its own to make a request seem more agreeable or alongside other tools to fabricate threats, forge evidence, invent opportunities, or conceal one's true aim.

Deception is resolved as an Opposed Check. The Difficulty for the active character depends on the believability of the lie, while the reactive character's side depends on their suspicion, knowledge, and attentiveness. On a success, the reactive character accepts the falsehood as true, at least for the moment, and may gain a personal truth such as Trusts [Character]. If the lie is later exposed, that truth may become Distrusts [Character], increasing the Difficulty of future Social Conflicts and creating fresh motives for reprisal.

Complications while lying should matter. A hesitation, a factual inconsistency, a witness entering at the wrong time, or visible nervousness may make the target more suspicious even if the lie is not fully uncovered.

### Evidence

Concrete evidence is often the strongest social tool available. Letters, contracts, testimony, records, forged warrants, official seals, ledgers, and visible injuries can all function as truths that reduce the Difficulty of relevant Checks.

If the reactive character doubts the authenticity of the evidence, the active character may first need to verify, interpret, or contextualise it with another Check before it takes effect. Evidence can reinforce any other social method: proof strengthens threats, documents legitimise claims, and corroboration makes even a startling accusation harder to dismiss.

### Intimidation

Intimidation uses the threat of harm, ruin, shame, or severe consequence to compel compliance. It is resolved as an Opposed Check and may use Fighting if the threat is explicitly physical.

A successful intimidation makes the target more cooperative in the immediate term. A failed intimidation, however, has consequences. Once a threat is called and found wanting, it cannot be repeated in the same scene unless the active character introduces a genuinely new and credible danger. Hollow threats become weaker each time they are repeated.

Intimidation is also hostile. Even when it works, it leaves resentment behind. The target remembers what was said once the moment of pressure passes, and the social consequences of that memory may matter far beyond the current scene.

### Negotiation

Negotiation is the art of exchange. You offer something the target wants in return for what you want from them. Coin is only one possible currency. Favour, silence, status, labour, protection, political backing, medicine, knowledge, and access may all serve.

A successful negotiation is represented as a truth such as A Bribe of 30 Denarii or A Favour from the Magistrate. If you fail, you must sweeten the offer before trying again. The obligation runs both ways. If you successfully negotiate with a promise you do not intend to keep, you have not escaped the cost—you have merely pushed the consequences into the future.

### Reading a Target

Understanding who you are dealing with is often the key to winning a Social Conflict. An Insight- or Reason-based Check using Academia, Observation, or Manipulation can reveal what a target fears, wants, believes, or seeks to protect.

The base Difficulty is usually Routine, rising if the target is secretive, disciplined, or suspicious. If the target realises they are being studied, the Check may become Opposed. Levels of Success may be spent to ask questions as with Obtain Information. Useful questions include: What do they fear? What do they seek to protect? What do they desire? What do they believe?

Information gained in this way should have practical effect. It may reduce the Difficulty of later Checks, justify Social Superiority, open new lines of argument, or reveal that a once-impossible request may now be made in a different form.

## Failure, Concession, and Lasting Defeat

A reactive character who fails a Social Conflict stops being an obstacle in that regard for the rest of the scene. They agree to what was asked, or at least to a reasonable and actionable version of it. If the requested action would bring dire consequences, they may concede in a modified way by offering a compromise, an indirect form of cooperation, or a delay that still recognises the weight of the victor's argument.

Failure does not mean mind control. The target acts in a way consistent with the outcome, their nature, and the surrounding stakes. A bribed official may look the other way; they do not instantly become a devoted ally. A cowed informer may reveal a name; they do not necessarily become trustworthy.

A **Lasting Social Defeat** is more serious. It does not merely settle one exchange. It cuts a character out of a social circle, faction, court, guild, household, or other network of support. The defeated character may lose access to resources, allies, invitations, patronage, or reputation. Recovery is possible, but it requires time, action, and often a meaningful change in circumstance.

## Running Social Conflict Well

Run Social Conflict as a struggle over pressure and leverage, not as repeated charm checks. Ask what the speaker wants, why the target resists, what each side fears, and what would make the request more reasonable. Reward preparation. Let secrets matter. Make the social environment matter as much as a battlefield matters in combat.

Do not force every conversation into a subsystem. Use full Social Conflict when the stakes justify it. Equally, do not let a scene stall because the first approach failed. Encourage players to shift the context with evidence, leverage, inducement, allies, timing, and new truths.

Above all, remember that Social Conflict should reveal character. It is where pride hardens, fear shows through, loyalties are tested, and people decide what they will pay to avoid yielding. Run it with the same seriousness and consequence as combat, even when no blade is drawn.
