## Madness

Unless otherwise noted, any madness a character develops is transient. If a character must roll on the Madness table while already afflicted with a transient madness, the new insanity is short-term. If they are suffering from a short-term insanity when they must roll again, the newly developed psychosis is long-term.

- **Transient** madness persists until the character completes the encounter that caused it. A few quiet moments of self-reassurance are sufficient to quell it — a Breather is all that is needed.
- **Short-term** madness persists until the character takes a short rest, drawing on the support of companions and rebuilding their mental defences.
- **Long-term** madness persists until the character spends downtime to address the condition — or, if downtime is not in use, until the current adventure or quest is complete. A week of rest and support is required to quell a long-term madness.
- **Indefinite** madness is permanent unless cured by exceptional means.

---

## Going Insane

The acute manifestation of a character's lunacy should be thematically congruous with the situation that caused the initial Horror check. That said, the link between the symptoms and the triggering event may not be immediately obvious. This is an opportunity for the player and the GM to be creative and develop the character's idiosyncrasies.

The tables below provide a range of options for the GM and player to explore. When relevant, the GM should roll or choose an appropriate entry.

The acute nature of these manifestations means the character can quickly shake them off. Transient madness requires only a few moments out of the immediate situation; short-term madness resolves with a short rest.

## Long-Term and Indefinite Psychoses

The effects of long-term madness persist until the character has completely recovered — mentally and physically — from the stresses of adventuring, requiring at least a week's break. Indefinite madness cannot be cured except through exceptional intervention.

After each long rest, a character suffering from long-term or indefinite madness must make a **Resilience (Discipline) check** to suppress the effects until their next long rest. The initial DC is 10. With each successful check the DC increases by 1, until the madness eventually manifests and the process resets. If the character fails their check, the madness manifests as a persistent effect and may also trigger an acute attack during high-stress situations — including combat and, at the GM's discretion, tense social encounters. To control themselves during a high-stress situation the character must make a **Resilience (Discipline) check** (DC 12), applying their Insight modifier. On a failure, they suffer the Acute effect of their madness.

*If a character is suffering from multiple forms of madness, each is handled independently.*

### Recovering Between Adventures
The character may attempt to recover her newly-lost Sanity between sessions. This attempt at treatment may only be done once, at the end of the adventure in which the character incurred 
the Sanity loss.
- Make a challenging Sanity Check.
- Regardless of roll, the character may not increase Sanity beyond maximum Sanity score.
- Critical Success: regains 2d4 Sanity
- Success: regains 1d4 Sanity
- Failure: no change in Sanity
- Critical Failure: loses 1d2 Sanity
