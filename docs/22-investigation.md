# Investigation

Investigative play is implied across Social Conflict, Skills, and the Resolution Engine, but never assembled into a procedure. This chapter provides one: a scene-based clue economy compatible with the rest of the 2d12 system.

The central premise: **the players never fail to find a critical clue.** They may fail to understand it, fail to find it cheaply, or fail to find it without consequence — but the adventure does not stall because of a single bad roll.

## Anatomy Of A Mystery

A Mystery has four components, defined by the GM in prep:

1. **The Truth.** What actually happened, and who did it.
2. **The Question.** What the players are trying to learn (it does not have to equal the Truth in scope).
3. **Leads.** A list of clues, each pointing to one or more scenes or other leads.
4. **Bystanders And Antagonists.** People with motives that may yield clues if engaged.

Prep at least **three independent paths** from the opening scene to the climactic reveal. A mystery built as a single chain breaks at the first failed roll.

## Clues, Leads, And Threads

- A **Clue** is a fact about the Truth.
- A **Lead** is a clue plus a direction — "the dagger was forged in Ostfeld" plus "Ostfeld is two days east."
- A **Thread** is a chain of leads from a starting scene to a destination scene.

A mystery should have at least one Thread that ends in the central reveal. A robust mystery has three to five Threads, some converging, some dead-ending.

## The Core Investigation Test

Use a Common or Opposed Test, depending on whether someone is actively concealing.

- **Common (Searching, Reading, Examining):** Reason + Education, Reason + Investigation, Insight + Observation, or Insight + Lore as appropriate.
- **Opposed (Interrogating, Tailing, Cracking):** Charisma + Manipulation (vs Will + Resilience), Insight + Observation (vs Agility + Stealth), or Reason + Investigation (vs Reason + Subterfuge).

Set the Difficulty by the scene's design, not by the importance of the clue.

## The Three-Outcome Convention

Replace the standard six outcomes with three for clue-related rolls (the underlying mechanics still apply to Doom and Fortune):

| Roll Result | Investigator Gets | GM Gets |
|---|---|---|
| **Pass (Total ≤ CT)** | The Core Clue, freely | Nothing |
| **Pass With Complication** | The Core Clue *and* one extra clue or lead, at a cost | 1 Doom; place a cost on the scene |
| **Fail or Fumble** | The Core Clue *at a cost* | 1–2 Doom; the cost is dramatic |

Costs are narrated, not rolled. Examples:

- A guard sees you and remembers your face.
- The witness will only speak in exchange for protection.
- You learn the truth, but lose a Connection's trust.
- The clue is incomplete and pointed; you will need a follow-up scene to confirm it.
- You take 1 Stress to Resolve from what you saw.

The players still get the clue. The price is what differs.

## Information Spends

Echoing the Social Conflict chapter, when an investigation test produces extra SL, players may spend SL as currency at the table:

| SL Spent | Information Gained |
|---|---|
| 1 | One extra peripheral detail (color, name, time) |
| 2 | One extra connected lead pointing to a different Thread |
| 3 | One key fact about an Antagonist's motive |
| 4 | The next Thread along the path, skipping a scene entirely |
| 5+ | The Antagonist's identity, location, or vulnerability outright (GM's choice) |

Players announce the spend before the GM narrates; this keeps agency on the table.

## Investigation Skills

The skills below carry investigation weight:

- **Investigation (Reason).** Examining scenes, searching for hidden things, piecing together evidence.
- **Observation (Insight).** Spotting tells, reading body language, noticing what is not present.
- **Lore (Insight).** Knowing the historical, magical, or social meaning of a clue.
- **Education (Reason).** Library research, deciphering documents, cross-referencing records.
- **Medicine (Reason).** Examining bodies and wounds.
- **Subterfuge (Agility or Charisma).** Tailing, eavesdropping, plant evidence checks (defensive).
- **Manipulation (Charisma).** Interrogations, charming witnesses, social leverage.
- **Society (Charisma).** Working the right room to ask the right person.

If multiple skills could apply, the player picks; the GM picks the difficulty.

## Scenes And The Clue Clock

Each investigation scene runs as a clock with a small number of segments (usually 3 or 4). Each meaningful action — a roll, an interview, a search of a zone, a magical sense — ticks the clock by 1, regardless of outcome.

When the clock fills, the scene ends. The party has the Core Clue and any earned extras. They may not return to this scene without burning a new Thread to re-enter (a faction asking them to revisit, a witness recalling something new, etc.).

The Clue Clock prevents the party from infinitely rerolling a scene to extract every possible piece of information. Every scene has a budget.

## Red Herrings And Dead Ends

A Mystery without dead ends feels rigged. Two rules of thumb:

- **At most one in five Threads is a red herring.** More than this and players lose faith in the clue economy.
- **Red herrings still spend the players' time and resources.** A red herring that costs nothing is a tonal beat, not a misdirection.

Mark red herrings clearly in your prep so you do not accidentally turn a dead end into a critical path.

## Antagonists And Active Mysteries

An **active mystery** is one where the Antagonist is still in motion — not a cold case. The Antagonist gets their own clock with as many segments as the mystery has phases. Each Downtime Period, each scene the players spend on the wrong Thread, and each missed lead ticks the Antagonist's clock. When it fills, the Antagonist accomplishes their goal:

- The body is buried.
- The artifact is sold.
- The accomplice is silenced.
- The next victim is taken.

This converts investigation pressure from "did you find the clue?" into "did you find it in time?"

## Interrogation

A specific subcase of Social Conflict. Use the social conflict procedure with the following overlays:

- The target's SL Required is set by their Will + Resilience plus their motivation to lie.
- A target reduced to 0 Resolve will speak the truth as they understand it. They may still be wrong.
- A target physically harmed during interrogation gives a +1d short-term advantage to the next attempt and a −2d long-term cost — testimony obtained under harm is unreliable and the target's faction is now an enemy.
- A Fortune spent on an interrogation may convert one failed attempt into a Success With Complication.

## Magical Investigation

When the party uses magic to investigate (oracular spellbook, divination rituals, true sight), apply these overlays:

- **Power is spent before the question is answered.** A failed roll consumes the spent Power and returns a true-but-narrow vision.
- **An oracle never lies, but it is rarely useful without context.** Phrase magical clues as images, riddles, or vignettes.
- **Repeated divination on the same subject in the same Downtime Period reduces the answer's reliability** by one step per attempt (clear → cryptic → contradictory → silent).
- **Cult, demon, or supernatural antagonists may roll opposed magic to muddy or implant false leads.**

A two-hour mystery rarely needs more than 4–6 scenes, 3 Threads, and 8–10 prepped costs. Overprepping a mystery is a sign you have not committed to the clue-economy rule: the players will find the clue.
