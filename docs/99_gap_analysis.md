# Game System Gap Analysis

This document maps the current rules material into three buckets: **already covered**, **partially covered**, and **missing**. It also ties the missing or incomplete areas back to the chapters that appear to be carrying that load today, so it is easier to decide where the next writing pass should go.

## Coverage matrix

| System area | Already covered | Partially covered | Missing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core resolution engine | Action rolls define common, opposed, and extended checks; difficulty, advantage/disadvantage, push, and levels of success are all present. | Outcome language could still be normalized, because degrees of success, complications, and pass/fail states sometimes overlap in wording. | A single glossary or rules reference for core terms is not visible in the current set. |
| Dice conventions | The system states that it runs on d12s and explains how d6, d4, and d3 are emulated from d12 results. | Dice conventions are brief and may need cross-references for damage, tables, and specialty rolls. | A consolidated probability-facing primer for the GM is not present. |
| Attributes and derived stats | Eight attributes, derived resources, and several key formulas are defined, including Stamina, Alacrity, Vitality, Wounds, Resolve, Sanity, and Power. | Some downstream uses of attributes appear in other chapters rather than in one unified reference, which may make onboarding slower. | A full character sheet style reference page is not visible in the current material. |
| Skills and specializations | Skill ranks, specialization rules, and a broad skill list are already present. | Skill advancement is mentioned, but broader progression pacing and interaction with campaign rewards remain thin. | No dedicated chapter on task examples, sample DC benchmarks by domain, or GM adjudication examples is visible. |
| Combat procedures | Initiative, actions, reactions, movement, attacks, damage, armor interaction, critical wounds, dying, and combat uses of Superiority are all covered. | Some combat subsystems appear to rely on material not shown here, such as full critical wound tables, equipment detail, weapon lists, and condition reference support. | A complete encounter-building chapter and enemy design guidance are not visible. |
| Social conflict | The game has a substantial social conflict engine with requests, opposed checks, social tools, reading a target, and social superiority. | Lasting social consequences, faction fallout, and reputation loops are discussed conceptually but are not yet tied to a larger campaign procedure. | A durable campaign-facing social status/faction subsystem is not yet fully present. |
| Recovery and attrition | Short Rest, Long Rest, Full Rest, first aid, ability damage recovery, and fatigue are defined. | Full Rest explicitly mentions downtime activities, but those activities are not actually spelled out in the current chapter set. | A complete downtime chapter is missing. |
| Talents and build options | Talents are extensive, include prerequisites, passive benefits, actions, reactions, and desperate actions, and clearly support varied builds. | The talent list is strong, but the broader advancement framework around when and why characters gain new talents is still only lightly framed. | A campaign-level advancement model with milestones, XP cadence, and respec/retraining guidance is not visible. |
| Superiority and momentum | Combat Superiority and Social Superiority are both defined as central momentum mechanics. | The two versions are philosophically linked, but they still read as separate modules rather than a single generalized pressure framework. | No broader framework extends Superiority into exploration, intrigue, chases, or faction conflicts. |
| Resource pressure | Stamina, Vitality, Resolve, Sanity, Power, Doom, Fortune, and fatigue all point to a strong attrition model. | These resources are spread across chapters, so the economy of play is harder to read in one pass. | A unified resource economy chapter or one-page summary is not present. |
| Exploration and travel | Survival includes navigation, woodcraft, tracking, foraging, and related wilderness-facing skills. | Travel-facing ingredients exist in Attributes, Skills, and Recovery, but they are not yet assembled into a procedure for journeys, hazards, watches, supply, or route play. | A full exploration and travel chapter is missing. |
| Investigation and discovery | Reading a target, observation, academia, scrutiny, and information-spend options support investigative play in pieces. | These tools exist, but there is no explicit investigation loop for clue discovery, leads, dead ends, and scene-based inquiry. | A dedicated investigation procedure chapter is missing. |
| Factions, reputation, and favors | A chapter appears to exist for connections, reputation, and favors, which suggests this area is at least partially designed. | The currently reviewed material does not show how that chapter fully links into advancement, downtime, or social conflict outcomes. | A fully integrated long-term faction play loop is not yet demonstrably complete from the current source set reviewed here. |
| Equipment and economy | Carrying rules and coin encumbrance are present, and negotiation/bribe logic implies money matters. | There are signs of an economy, but not a complete purchasing, maintenance, scarcity, or equipment package in the current material shown. | A dedicated equipment, wealth, and trade chapter is missing from the current source set. |
| Magic and supernatural procedures | Power and spellcaster-related hooks exist in attributes, talents, skills, and combat references. | The current material implies a magic system, but the actual casting framework, spell lists, rituals, risks, and magical opposition rules are not present here. | A full magic chapter is missing from the reviewed set. |
| Conditions and status effects | Some conditions appear, including Staggered, Irrational, Unconscious, Frightened, Restrained, and Desperate. | Conditions are referenced across multiple chapters instead of being consolidated in one rules appendix. | A complete universal conditions appendix is missing. |
| GM tools and adversary creation | The rules give the GM difficulty setting tools, consequences, rewards, and some guidance on Resolve and Desperate behavior. | These are useful building blocks, but they do not yet amount to a full GM operations chapter. | Encounter building, NPC creation, monster templates, hazard design, and campaign prep procedures are missing. |
| Long-term campaign structure | Several chapters imply campaign play through XP, social status, rest cycles, and favors. | The ingredients are there, but there is no visible chapter that explains the default campaign loop between adventure, recovery, advancement, and world reaction. | A campaign framework chapter is missing. |

## Missing pieces by chapter

### Action Rolls

**Already supporting:** the universal resolution engine, opposed checks, extended checks, consequences, rewards, and levels of success.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** a glossary of core terms, clearer adjudication examples, and tighter normalization between "degrees of success," complications, and SL-based outcomes.

**What to add next:** a short GM appendix with worked examples for stealth, research, chases, hazards, investigation scenes, and multi-step tasks.

### Attributes

**Already supporting:** core stats, derived resources, carrying capacity, initiative, injury thresholds, and magical reserves.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** a cleaner one-page reference for derived stats, a more explicit relationship between attributes and long-term advancement, and a consolidated condition cross-reference.

**What to add next:** a derived-stat summary page and an index pointing to every place each resource is spent or recovered.

### Skills

**Already supporting:** broad competency coverage for scholarship, wilderness, combat, social action, stealth, medicine, and tactics.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** an actual exploration procedure, investigation procedure, and better examples showing when a skill bypasses rolling versus when it becomes a common or extended check.

**What to add next:** short rules modules for travel, clue-finding, tracking scenes, and crafting/research projects that plug directly into existing skills.

### Combat

**Already supporting:** turn order, actions, reactions, attack flow, movement, critical wounds, and defeat pressure.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** encounter construction, full enemy-facing rules support, a conditions appendix, weapon/equipment cross-reference, and clearer hazard/trap integration.

**What to add next:** a GM combat toolkit with encounter templates, sample enemy roles, hazard rules, and encounter difficulty guidance.

### Social Conflict

**Already supporting:** social requests, opposed exchanges, social tools, reading a target, and social superiority.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** lasting consequences over multiple sessions, integration with reputation/connections, and procedures for faction-scale social play.

**What to add next:** explicit links to reputation, favors, blackmail, obligations, and faction clocks or status tracks.

### Superiority

**Already supporting:** party momentum in combat and resolve-breaking pressure on NPCs.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** a unified explanation of how Superiority should operate across the whole game rather than only combat and social scenes.

**What to add next:** either keep it intentionally pillar-specific, or promote it into a universal scene-pressure mechanic that can also run pursuits, infiltration, debates, and battlefield objectives.

### Resting and Recovery

**Already supporting:** rest cadence, healing, first aid, ability damage recovery, and fatigue removal.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** the promised downtime layer, long-term injuries, convalescence, training, crafting, lifestyle, and other between-adventure procedures.

**What to add next:** a downtime chapter that uses Full Rest as the bridge into training, projects, treatment, social maintenance, and economic decisions.

### Talents

**Already supporting:** a deep build framework with strong combat, stealth, survival, leadership, and social niches.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** advancement pacing, campaign expectations for talent acquisition, retraining rules, and guidance for balancing talent density against core skill growth.

**What to add next:** an advancement chapter that states XP awards, expected talent frequency, and whether attributes or traits can also improve later in play.

### Connections, Reputation, and Favors

**Already supporting:** evidence exists that a long-term social capital chapter exists, which is promising for campaign continuity.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** clearer integration with Social Conflict, downtime, rewards, and faction-level consequences across sessions.

**What to add next:** bring this chapter into the campaign loop by explicitly defining how favors are earned, spent, called in, lost, and escalated into obligations or enemies.

### Dice

**Already supporting:** the physical dice language of the game and d12 conversion rules.

**Likely gaps attached to this chapter:** usability support for new readers, probability intuition, and references to where each die mode appears in play.

**What to add next:** either fold Dice into Action Rolls as a short front-matter section, or expand it into a complete quickstart rules reference.

## Priority writing order

If the goal is to make the system feel publishable fastest, the next chapters to draft should be these:

1. **Downtime and campaign loop** — because Full Rest already points at downtime, but the actual procedure is absent.
2. **Exploration and travel** — because survival, fatigue, carrying, and navigation exist, but the game lacks a journey structure.
3. **GM toolkit and encounter design** — because combat and action rules are deep enough that GMs now need calibration support.
4. **Conditions appendix** — because status effects are currently scattered across chapters.
5. **Advancement chapter** — because talents and skills exist, but long-term growth cadence is still underdefined.
6. **Equipment, economy, and magic chapters** — because several current rules imply them, but they are not yet visible in the reviewed set.
