
### Purpose
Use this module for any conflict that deserves spotlight time and tactical back-and-forth:
- Combat.
- Duels.
- Chases.
- Social showdowns.
- Command contests.
- Stealth confrontations.

For minor uncertainty, use a normal opposed test instead. Burning Wheel is known for reserving its more detailed conflict procedures for major moments rather than every minor scuffle. [3][4]

### Core principles
This module is built on four ideas:
- Conflicts are about **stated stakes**.
- Each exchange has **3 passes**.
- Each side scripts a number of actions equal to **Alacrity**, up to 3.
- Resolution is simultaneous in intent, sequential in execution.

That gives tactical prediction, tempo, and mind games without requiring the full lookup complexity associated with Burning Wheel Fight!. [3][4]

## Setup

### Step 1: State stakes
Before the first exchange, each side must declare:
- **Intent**: what they want.
- **Risk**: what they stand to lose.
- **Limit**: what would make them concede.

Examples:
- “I drive him from the bridge.”
- “I force a confession in front of the court.”
- “I escape into the alleys before the alarm spreads.”

If the stakes are unclear, do not begin scripted conflict yet. Burning Wheel-style conflict works best when the objective is explicit before actions are scripted. [3][5]

### Step 2: Set starting values
Every participant records:

- **Guard** = $$3 + Body$$ in physical conflicts, or $$3 + Will$$ in social conflicts.
- **Wound Track** = Bruised, Hurt, Broken, Out.
- **Momentum** = 0.
- **Alacrity** = usually 1–4, but only 1–3 actions may be scripted.
- **Position Tags** = any starting fictional edges, such as High Ground, Hidden, In Close, Surrounded.

### Step 3: Determine Alacrity script size
Each exchange has **3 passes**. At the start of the exchange, each side scripts a number of actions equal to its **Alacrity**, to a maximum of 3.

- **Alacrity 1**: script 1 action and fill the rest with Hold.
- **Alacrity 2**: script 2 actions and fill the last with Hold.
- **Alacrity 3+**: script all 3 passes.

If Alacrity is above 3, the extra value gives a passive edge:
- gain **advantage** on one action each exchange, or
- win ties on one pass per exchange.

This preserves the idea that faster characters act more fully within the exchange, while keeping the table manageable. Burning Wheel similarly ties scripted action economy to character speed, but practical play benefits from a hard cap. [3][6]

## Dice procedure

### Roll
When an action resolves, roll **2d12** and apply modifiers.

Base formula:
- **2d12**
- + relevant **Attribute**
- + relevant **Skill**
- + gear bonus
- + spent Momentum
- + spent Advantage Tags
- - wound penalties
- - situational penalties

### Successes
Each die showing **8 or higher** counts as 1 success.

Read results like this:
- **0 successes**: miss.
- **1 success**: weak hit.
- **2 successes**: solid hit.
- **3+ successes**: strong hit.

If both sides roll, compare total successes:
- Higher successes win.
- Tie goes to the higher relevant Attribute.
- If still tied, both actions land weakly or cancel, depending on the fiction.

### Margin
**Margin** = winner’s successes minus loser’s successes.

Use Margin for:
- Guard damage.
- Number of tags created.
- Strength of repositioning.
- Recovery amount.
- Severity of concessions.

## Exchange structure

Each exchange follows this procedure:

1. Both sides secretly script actions equal to Alacrity.
2. Empty passes are filled with **Hold**.
3. Reveal scripts at the same time.
4. Resolve **Pass 1**.
5. Resolve **Pass 2**.
6. Resolve **Pass 3**.
7. Apply end-of-exchange fallout.
8. If neither side is Out or conceding, begin a new exchange.

A participant gets only one action per pass, never more. This keeps the exchange readable and preserves the volley-like rhythm. [3][7]

## Action list

These are the core actions.

| Action | Role | On success | On strong hit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Press** | Attack, pressure, direct force | Deal Guard damage equal to Margin | If target Guard is 0, mark 1 Wound |
| **Guard** | Block, evade, absorb, hold | Reduce incoming effect by Margin | Gain 1 Guard or 1 Momentum |
| **Shift** | Reposition, change range, seize footing | Gain or remove 1 Position Tag | Also gain 1 Momentum |
| **Set Up** | Feint, bait, study, create opening | Create 1 Advantage Tag | Create 2 Advantage Tags |
| **Commit** | High-risk decisive action | Gain advantage on the roll | If it hits, +1 extra effect |
| **Recover** | Rally, breathe, regain poise | Restore 1 Guard or clear 1 minor condition | Restore 2 Guard or clear 2 minor conditions |
| **Hold** | Wait, watch, conserve action | No roll unless reacting by rule | Gain advantage to next action this exchange |

This is a smaller, cleaner list than Burning Wheel’s full combat action set, but it keeps distinct tactical jobs instead of collapsing everything into the same few abstractions. Mouse Guard’s conflict system is known for a smaller universal set, while Burning Wheel’s detailed systems differentiate action types more heavily. [8][9][3]

## Action definitions

### Press
Use Press to strike, force, batter, overbear, corner, or verbally drive an opponent.

- Roll against the opposing action if contested.
- On success, deal Guard damage equal to Margin.
- If the target has **0 Guard**, a strong hit marks **1 Wound**.

Use this for:
- Sword attacks.
- Grapples to dominate position.
- Hard accusations in debate.
- Closing in during a chase.

### Guard
Use Guard to defend, parry, duck, shield, deny, or maintain footing.

- Roll when directly opposed by hostile action.
- Reduce incoming effect by Margin.
- On a strong hit, gain either +1 Guard or +1 Momentum.

Guard is reactive but not passive. It should feel like active defense, not simply “skip turn.”

### Shift
Use Shift to move, circle, withdraw, close range, seize the high ground, split formation, or redirect the conversation.

- On success, create or remove 1 **Position Tag**.
- On a strong hit, also gain 1 Momentum.

Common tags:
- In Close
- At Reach
- High Ground
- Hidden
- Cornered
- Surrounded
- In Command
- Off-Balance

### Set Up
Use Set Up to create an opening rather than cash it in immediately.

- On success, create 1 **Advantage Tag** on the target or scene.
- On a strong hit, create 2 tags.

Common Advantage Tags:
- Exposed Guard
- Read Their Rhythm
- Distracted
- Pinned
- Split Attention
- Rattled
- Broken Formation

Advantage Tags are normally spent for advantage on a relevant roll or to justify fictional leverage.

### Commit
Use Commit for a risky, forceful, all-in action.

- Gain **advantage** before rolling.
- If you win, gain +1 effect.
- If you lose to Press or Guard, suffer 1 extra Guard damage.
- If you lose while already at 0 Guard, mark 1 Wound.

This is the “bet everything on this moment” action.

### Recover
Use Recover to regain footing, morale, breath, or composure.

- On success, restore 1 Guard or clear 1 minor condition.
- On a strong hit, restore 2 Guard or clear 2 minor conditions.
- If directly beaten by Press, Recover fails.

### Hold
Use Hold when slow, cautious, waiting for the right opening, or forced to leave a pass empty due to Alacrity.

- Hold never initiates effect.
- It grants **advantage** to the next action this exchange.
- If the fiction strongly supports it, Hold may defend weakly against a direct Press; in that case, roll 2d12 with no Skill bonus unless a trait or stance says otherwise.

## Script rules

### Secret scripting
At the start of the exchange, each side writes:
- Pass 1 action.
- Pass 2 action.
- Pass 3 action.

Only as many actions as Alacrity allows may be chosen freely; remaining passes are Hold.

### Repetition limits
To prevent dull scripting:
- You may not use **Commit** more than once in an exchange.
- You may not use **Recover** twice in the same exchange.
- You may not use **Press** in all three passes unless a weapon, stance, or special ability allows it.

This kind of limitation helps produce the prediction game that makes Burning Wheel-style scripting interesting instead of repetitive. Burning Wheel itself uses weapon speed and action constraints to shape script variety. [10][6]

## Resolution rules

Use these universal pairings instead of a large action matrix.

### Press vs Press
Both roll.
- Higher result deals Guard damage equal to Margin.
- On a tie, both deal 1 Guard damage.

### Press vs Guard
Both roll.
- If Guard wins, it reduces or negates the attack.
- If Press still has Margin left after Guard reduction, that leftover deals Guard damage.

### Press vs Shift / Set Up / Recover
Both roll.
- If Press wins, it deals damage and may interrupt the enemy’s intended benefit.
- If the non-Press action wins, it resolves and avoids the pressure.

### Guard vs Shift
Both roll.
- If Shift wins, repositioning happens.
- If Guard wins, the defender holds the line and may gain 1 Momentum on a strong hit.

### Guard vs Set Up
Both roll.
- If Set Up wins, it creates tags.
- If Guard wins, it prevents the setup and may create a “Readied” or “Steady” tag.

### Guard vs Recover
Usually no conflict unless one side is actively pressuring.
- Recover generally succeeds unless the fiction says Guard is pinning or suppressing.

### Shift vs Set Up
Both roll.
- Shift changes the battlefield or conversation frame.
- Set Up creates a future edge.
- Winner’s effect applies; loser gets nothing.

### Commit vs anything
Commit follows the base action it resembles, usually Press or Shift, but with the Commit bonuses and penalties applied.

## Tags and Momentum

### Advantage Tags
These represent created openings.

Each Advantage Tag may be spent to:
- Gain **advantage** on a relevant roll.
- Cancel 1 enemy Momentum.
- Justify a fictional permission, such as disarming, cornering, or forcing disclosure.
- Upgrade a weak hit to a solid hit if the fiction fits.

Tags expire at the end of the next exchange unless used.

### Position Tags
These represent battlefield or scene state:
- In Close
- At Reach
- High Ground
- Hidden
- Cornered
- Surrounded
- Center Stage
- Commanding Voice

Position Tags persist until removed or replaced by fiction.

### Momentum
Momentum is short-term tempo and initiative.

Gain Momentum from:
- Strong hits.
- Successful Shifts.
- Certain traits or stances.
- End-of-pass dominance if the GM wants a more dynamic flow.

Spend Momentum to:
- Gain **advantage** before a roll.
- Break a tie after rolling.
- Ignore one minor penalty.
- Reveal one enemy scripted action before the exchange begins, if you have 2 Momentum.
- Seize first declaration in the next exchange.

Maximum Momentum is 3 unless special abilities raise it.

## Guard, Wounds, and conditions

### Guard
Guard measures immediate ability to stay in the conflict.

- Most attacks reduce Guard first.
- Guard can be restored with Recover.
- Guard resets between scenes unless the fiction says otherwise.

### Wounds
When damage is taken while at 0 Guard, mark a Wound:
- **Bruised**: -1 die to physical or social exertion of the appropriate type.
- **Hurt**: -1 die to all conflict rolls.
- **Broken**: -2 dice and cannot Commit.
- **Out**: removed from the conflict.

You may also suffer a Wound from:
- Losing with Commit at 0 Guard.
- Major fictional overmatch.
- A critical weapon or special move if the wider system uses them.

### Conditions
Suggested minor conditions:
- Off-Balance
- Exposed
- Rattled
- Pinned
- Winded
- Shaken
- Bleeding

A minor condition usually gives -1 die until cleared.

## Concessions and end states

A side may concede:
- At the end of any pass.
- Before a new exchange begins.
- Immediately after suffering a Wound, if the fiction allows.

If you concede, you do **not** count as Out, but you lose the stated stakes and may negotiate survival, dignity, or a lesser cost.

When a side becomes Out:
- The winner gets their intent.
- The loser suffers the stated risk, modified by the fiction.
- The GM applies follow-through consequences.

This keeps the system from becoming simple attrition and supports dramatic negotiated outcomes, one of the useful lessons from Burning Wheel’s conflict design. [3][11]
