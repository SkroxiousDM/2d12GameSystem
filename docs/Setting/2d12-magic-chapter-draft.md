# Magic

Magic is the disciplined shaping of supernatural force through will, training, and tradition. This chapter presents a magic system built on the same action roll structure as the rest of the game, while aligning its overall logic more closely with the Shadowrun model of spellcasting, Drain, traditions, adepts, conjuring, and astral travel.[file:179][web:159]

## Magical Aptitude

Only characters with the **Awakened** trait, or another equivalent feature granted by ancestry, talent, or advancement, can cast spells, conjure spirits, perceive the astral, or initiate metaplanar travel. Magical ability is measured through the same character components already used elsewhere in the system: Attributes, Skills, Traits, and narrative positioning.[file:179]

Magic does not require a separate resolution engine. All uncertain magical actions are resolved using the standard action roll: roll 2d12, with one die designated as the Complication die, and compare the final result to the Challenge Threshold (CT). Outcomes, Levels of Success, Advantage, Disadvantage, Push, Fortune, Doom, and Success at a Cost all function normally unless a rule below says otherwise.[file:179]

### Magical Skills

The following Skills are recommended for campaigns that use this chapter:

- **Sorcery**: Casting direct spells, counterspelling, and shaping raw magical force.
- **Conjuring**: Summoning, binding, dismissing, and negotiating with spirits.
- **Arcana**: Understanding magical theory, reading auras, navigating astral space, and identifying spells, traditions, and supernatural phenomena.
- **Enchanting**: Creating charms, fetishes, spell foci, wards, and other enduring magical objects.

## Spellcasting

Spellcasting follows a simple sequence:

1. Declare the spell effect.
2. Choose the spell's **Force**.
3. Build the Challenge Threshold.
4. Make the action roll.
5. Apply the outcome.
6. Resolve Drain, if any.

This keeps casting fast at the table while preserving meaningful risk and scaling power.[file:179][web:159]

### Spell Force

**Force** represents how much magical power the caster is trying to channel into a spell. A low-Force spell is subtle, contained, and easier to control. A high-Force spell is broader, stronger, or more durable, but carries greater danger to the caster.[web:159]

Force usually ranges from 1 to 6 for ordinary play, though exceptionally powerful beings, rituals, and artifacts may exceed that range. Force determines the spell's scale and also sets the baseline severity of Drain.

| Force | Typical Scale | Common Use |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Minor | Brief sensory tricks, tiny lights, subtle nudges, cosmetic effects |
| 2 | Modest | Single-target utility, weak wards, short boosts, simple attacks |
| 3 | Standard | Reliable combat spells, practical mobility, useful detection, healing magic |
| 4 | Strong | Multi-target effects, potent barriers, major transformations |
| 5 | Severe | Large areas, long durations, dangerous bindings, battlefield magic |
| 6 | Extreme | Mythic-scale effects, major summonings, drastic environmental change |

### Spell Categories

Every spell belongs to one of four broad categories. This does not limit narrative creativity; it gives the GM a framework for setting difficulty and consequences.

| Category | Typical Effects |
|---|---|
| **Combat** | Bolts, blasts, curses, barriers, battlefield control |
| **Detection** | Aura reading, divination, tracking, magical senses |
| **Illusion** | Veils, glamour, false images, emotional masking |
| **Manipulation** | Movement, telekinesis, transformation, environmental control |

A fifth category, **Health**, may be added if the campaign includes explicit healing and restoration magic as a separate discipline.

### Building the Challenge Threshold

Magical tests use the same CT logic as the core engine. The GM sets a base difficulty from Very Easy to Very Hard, then applies any situational modifiers. The relevant Attribute, Skill, and Trait are included exactly as normal.[file:179]

For spellcasting, add the following guidance:

- **Simple effect**: no additional modifier.
- **Broad, concealed, or unusually precise effect**: -2 CT.
- **Extreme range, large area, multiple targets, or hostile magical environment**: -4 CT.
- **Each point of Force above 2**: -1 CT.

This means a Force 2 spell is generally the baseline. Force 3-6 remains playable, but each step pushes the roll toward greater danger and more frequent complications.

### Casting Roll

A typical casting roll uses:

**2d12 + relevant Attribute + Sorcery + magical Trait** versus the spell's CT.[file:179]

The relevant Attribute depends on tradition and style:

- **Reason/Logic** for careful, formulaic casting.
- **Will/Resolve** for forceful projection and endurance.
- **Presence/Charisma** for spirit-led, emotional, or devotional magic.

The table should choose attribute names that match the core character sheet.

### Spell Outcomes

Use the normal result categories from the resolution engine, but interpret them through magical fiction.[file:179]

| Result | Magical Outcome |
|---|---|
| **Critical Success** | The spell succeeds powerfully. Gain 1 Fortune, and the caster may add one bonus effect without increasing Drain. |
| **Success** | The spell succeeds as intended. |
| **Success with Complication** | The spell succeeds, but causes Drain, collateral instability, attention, or another magical consequence; the GM gains 1 Doom. |
| **Marginal Failure** | The spell fails, but the caster gains some useful information, partial effect, or safe repositioning. |
| **Failure with Complication** | The spell fails and also causes Drain, exposure, backlash, or a narrative escalation; the GM gains 1 Doom. |
| **Critical Failure** | The spell fails disastrously; the caster gains 1 Stress, the GM gains 1 Doom, and a severe magical backlash occurs. |

### Levels of Success

Levels of Success may be spent exactly as normal, but magical actions can also spend them on spell-specific upgrades.[file:179]

Common magical spends include:

- Extend duration.
- Increase area modestly.
- Add one secondary target.
- Reduce collateral effects.
- Conceal the spell's signature.
- Improve damage, potency, or penetration.
- Preserve momentum in an ongoing ritual or extended magical action.

## Drain

Drain is the personal cost of channeling power beyond safe limits. In game terms, Drain is what makes magic strong without making it free.[web:159][file:179]

This draft uses a **single-roll Drain model** because it fits the existing resolution engine cleanly. Instead of making a second resistance roll after every spell, Drain is primarily triggered by complications and failures during the casting roll.[file:179]

### When Drain Happens

A caster usually suffers Drain when:

- A spell succeeds with complication.
- A spell fails with complication.
- A spell critically fails.
- The caster chooses Success at a Cost on a magical test.
- The caster attempts a spell at Force 5 or higher, even on an ordinary success, if the GM declares the effect especially unstable.[file:179]

### Drain Severity

Drain should usually inflict **Stress**, reducing either Vitality or Resolve depending on how the setting distinguishes physical and mental strain. If the engine uses separate tracks, most spell Drain should hit **Resolve** first; overflow may spill into Vitality.[file:179]

| Force | Normal Drain | Severe Drain on Critical Failure |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | 1 Stress | 2 Stress |
| 3-4 | 2 Stress | 3 Stress |
| 5 | 3 Stress | 4 Stress |
| 6+ | 4 Stress | 5+ Stress, plus narrative backlash |

The GM may swap raw Stress for fitting magical consequences, such as nosebleeds, trembling hands, visible aura burn, spirit-marking, or temporary sensory distortion.

### Buying Down Drain

A caster may reduce incoming Drain by accepting one or more of the following limits before rolling:

- Reduce the spell's duration.
- Narrow the area.
- Affect fewer targets.
- Make the effect obvious rather than subtle.
- Delay the effect into ritual timing rather than immediate release.

Each meaningful limitation should reduce expected Drain by 1 step, at the GM's discretion.

## Traditions

Traditions describe how a culture, school, lineage, or philosophy understands magic. In practical terms, traditions change how magic looks in the fiction, what tools or gestures are used, which spirits answer, and which Attribute normally supports casting or Drain-heavy effects.[web:160][web:2]

Traditions should not create entirely separate subsystems unless that is central to the campaign. A common rules chassis keeps the game easier to run.[file:179]

### Hermetic Tradition

Hermetic magic is studied, diagrammed, and systematized. It favors circles, correspondences, names, geometry, encoded texts, and prepared formulae.[web:2][web:160]

- Common casting Attribute: Logic or Reason.
- Common Drain flavor: migraines, tremors, mnemonic overload, arcane burn.
- Typical spirits: elementals, abstract servitors, bound intelligences.
- Usual tools: tomes, rods, sigils, crystal lattices, measured components.

### Shamanic Tradition

Shamanic magic is relational rather than purely technical. It is mediated through ancestors, totems, land-spirits, visions, trance, and taboo.[web:2][web:160]

- Common casting Attribute: Presence, Instinct, or Will.
- Common Drain flavor: exhaustion, spirit-marking, emotional spillover, omen visions.
- Typical spirits: beasts, ancestors, weather beings, place-spirits.
- Usual tools: fetishes, masks, drums, smoke, bone charms, song.

### Other Traditions

Additional traditions may include priestly miracle-workers, blood magicians, alchemists, animists, necromancers, court occultists, rune-workers, and elemental martial schools. Each should define:

- Its favored casting Attribute.
- Its visual and ritual language.
- Its preferred spirit types, if any.
- A short list of taboos, obligations, or narrative complications.

## Adepts

Adepts channel magic inward rather than projecting it outward as spells. Where a sorcerer shapes magic into effects, an adept uses it to sharpen the body, mind, reflexes, senses, and soul.[web:57]

Adepts are best handled as a parallel magical path rather than as weak spellcasters. They should excel at consistency, mobility, and personal excellence while sacrificing the broad utility of Sorcery.[web:57]

### Using Adept Powers

Most adept powers should be either:

- **Passive benefits**, granted by Talents or features, or
- **Activated techniques**, resolved with ordinary action rolls using Adept Discipline or another relevant Skill.[file:179]

Example activated uses include:

- Burst movement.
- Heightened perception.
- Impossible balance.
- Brief invisibility through controlled presence.
- Impact-enhanced strikes.
- Deflecting missiles.
- Short-range wall running or leaping.

### Adept Strain

Adepts normally do not suffer Drain in the same way full casters do. Instead, when an adept power is used under pressure and results in a complication or fumble, the adept suffers **Fatigue** or **physical Stress** rather than magical backlash.[file:179]

A simple rule is:

- Success: the technique works.
- Success with complication: the technique works, but the adept takes 1 Stress or suffers positional/exposure cost.
- Failure with complication: the technique fails and the adept takes 1 Stress.
- Critical failure: the technique fails and causes 2 Stress or a temporary loss of balance, stance, or focus.

## Conjuring

Conjuring is the art of contacting, summoning, bargaining with, binding, and dismissing spirits.[web:159][web:2]

A summoned spirit is not a spell effect. It is a supernatural actor with motives, nature, and limitations. Even when obedient, it should still feel like an entity rather than equipment.

### Summoning Test

Use a standard action roll:

**2d12 + relevant Attribute + Conjuring + magical Trait** versus the spirit's CT.[file:179]

A spirit's CT should reflect:

- Its scale or rank.
- The current environment.
- Whether the summoner is using a proper tradition, offering, name, or focus.
- Whether the spirit is naturally aligned, alien, hostile, or already offended.

Suggested baseline spirit difficulties:

| Spirit Rank | Typical CT Modifier |
|---|---|
| Lesser | Easy to Routine |
| Standard | Challenging |
| Greater | Difficult |
| Ancient or alien | Hard to Very Hard |

### Summoning Outcomes

- **Critical Success**: The spirit arrives favorably inclined; it may grant an extra service or useful information.
- **Success**: The spirit arrives and performs the agreed service.
- **Success with Complication**: The spirit arrives, but demands payment, leaves a mark, attracts attention, or creates unstable conditions.
- **Marginal Failure**: No summoning occurs, but the conjurer learns the spirit's nature, taboo, or location.
- **Failure with Complication**: The summoning fails and provokes offense, Drain, or a spiritual hazard.
- **Critical Failure**: The wrong entity answers, the spirit turns hostile, or the summoner's protections collapse.

### Binding and Services

Rather than tracking spirit services with a separate minigame, a simple and flexible approach is:

- A normal success yields **1 service**.
- Every 2 Levels of Success adds **1 additional service**.
- A service may be broad if the spirit is minor, or narrow if the spirit is powerful.

Example services:

- Attack or defend.
- Scout an area.
- Carry a message.
- Conceal the summoner.
- Sustain a magical effect briefly.
- Guide astral or metaplanar travel.
- Guard a location.

## Enchanting

Enchanting creates durable magical tools and preparations. Use it for charms, fetishes, potion-like workings, weapon foci, ward-stones, ritual links, and other persistent effects.[file:179]

Most enchanting should be handled as an **Extended Check**, because item creation naturally benefits from the engine's progress system.[file:179]

### Enchanting Procedure

1. Define the item's effect and Force-equivalent rating.
2. Set the total Levels of Success required.
3. Choose the roll frequency: per hour, day, week, or moon cycle.
4. Resolve each roll as an Extended Check until the item is complete or the project fails.[file:179]

Good enchanting complications include flawed resonance, obvious signatures, difficult maintenance, volatile side effects, or obligations to a spirit patron.

## Astral Perception and Projection

Astral space is the magical reflection of reality, visible only to those with the proper aptitude, training, or supernatural nature.[web:60]

### Astral Perception

Astral perception allows a character to see beyond the material surface of things into aura, emotional resonance, magical residue, and spiritual presence.[web:60]

Treat routine perception as automatic for trained Awakened characters when drama does not require a roll. When uncertainty matters, make an Arcana test using the normal action roll framework.[file:179][web:60]

Use astral perception to:

- Read emotional states broadly.
- Identify magical effects.
- Notice spirits or active spellwork.
- Examine aura damage, corruption, or possession.
- Follow magical traces in the short term.

### Astral Projection

Astral projection fully separates the traveler's awareness from the physical body, allowing movement through astral space while leaving the body vulnerable.[web:60]

Projection should usually require:

- The Awakened trait.
- A suitable Talent, initiation, or training milestone.
- A successful Arcana test when performed under pressure.[file:179][web:60]

While projecting:

- The character may move rapidly through astral space.
- Physical barriers matter differently or not at all, depending on their magical significance.[web:60]
- Active magical wards, hostile spirits, and background corruption remain serious threats.
- Damage, terror, or forced severance on the astral may impose Resolve loss, Stress, or narrative consequences on the return to the body.[file:179]

## Metaplanar Travel

Beyond ordinary astral space lie metaplanes: symbolic, mythic, elemental, ancestral, or alien realms that do not obey mundane logic.[web:83]

Metaplanar travel should remain rare, dangerous, and narratively significant. It works best when treated as a major scene, quest, or Extended Check rather than a routine movement option.[file:179][web:83]

### Entering a Metaplane

A metaplanar journey usually requires:

- Astral projection or an equivalent gateway state.
- A guide, key, ritual, true name, or conjunction.
- An Arcana or Conjuring test, often as part of an Extended Check.[file:179][web:83]

The GM should set a threshold based on:

- How distant or alien the metaplane is.
- Whether the traveler belongs there by tradition or patronage.
- Current omens, warding, and cosmic timing.

### Running Metaplanar Journeys

Metaplanar scenes should emphasize symbol over geography. A traveler may need to answer a riddle asked by a dead ancestor, cross a bridge made of vows, bargain with a storm-king, or survive a landscape shaped by memory and fear.[web:83]

Mechanically, use:

- **Common Tests** for isolated challenges.
- **Opposed Tests** against guides, guardians, and rival spirits.[file:179]
- **Extended Checks** for the journey as a whole.[file:179]

Failure should rarely mean "nothing happens." Better outcomes include delay, transformation, spirit debt, loss of gear, marked aura, Doom gain, or being returned changed.

## Counterspelling and Magical Defense

Any campaign using this chapter should define at least one defensive magical option. A simple rule is that a caster may use Sorcery reactively to defend against an incoming spell, curse, or spirit effect, either as an Opposed Test or by granting Disadvantage to the attacker.[file:179]

Useful defensive applications include:

- Breaking line of magical effect.
- Unraveling an illusion.
- Reinforcing a ward.
- Blocking possession.
- Shielding an ally from hostile aura contact.

## Design Notes for the GM

This chapter is intended to make magic feel dangerous, flexible, and integrated with the rest of the game rather than siloed into a separate minigame.[file:179]

To keep the system running smoothly:

- Keep Force meaningful by tying it to both scale and Drain.
- Let complication results do much of the balancing work.[file:179]
- Reserve metaplanar travel for major beats rather than casual scouting.[web:83]
- Let traditions shape fiction first and mechanics second.[web:160]
- Use Levels of Success to reward good rolls instead of inflating raw spell numbers.[file:179]
- Treat spirits as characters with agendas, not disposable spell slots.[web:2]
