# AETAS OBSCURA
## *A Campaign Primer for Players*
### *Year of the Consulship of Patricius and Hypatius — Anno Domini 500*

---

> *"The world is ending. It has been ending for a hundred years. And somewhere, beneath the rot, something is waking up and calling it an opportunity."*
> — overheard in a tavern in Ravenna, attributed to no one

---

## THE WORLD YOU LIVE IN

Rome is gone.

Not forgotten — not yet. The roads still work. The law is still cited. Latin is still the tongue of court and contract from Ravenna to Constantinople. Senators still meet in Rome. But the legions that held the frontier for five centuries are mercenary warbands now, paid in land and promises, loyal to kings with names like Theodoric and Clovis rather than to any emperor. The Western Empire died in the lifetime of your grandparents, and most people are still quietly waiting for it to come back.

It isn't coming back.

The world in 500 AD is a patchwork of successor kingdoms, provincial holdouts, and border zones where no single power can guarantee you safe passage from one city to the next. The Ostrogoths rule Italy from Ravenna. The Franks are consolidating Gaul under a king who recently converted to Christianity — whether out of faith or politics, no one agrees. The Vandals hold North Africa. The Eastern Empire endures in Constantinople, presiding over theological arguments so bitter that neighbouring bishops have had each other murdered over the exact nature of Christ's divinity. Persia watches from across the Euphrates, patient as it has always been.

This is a world in motion. Everything is provisional. Everything is up for grabs.

And the hidden world — the one your characters are about to become entangled in — is *more visible* than it has ever been, or may ever be again.

## WHAT YOUR CHARACTERS KNOW ABOUT THE WORLD

### The Supernatural Is Real

This is not a matter of debate for people of this age. The gods are real. Spirits walk alongside the living. Prayers sometimes work. Rituals performed correctly produce results that cannot be explained by philosophy or medicine. The question is never *whether* the supernatural is real — only *what kind* of real it is, and whose version of it is pointing at you right now.

When your character encounters something impossible, they will probably be frightened. But they will not be paralysed by the proof that magic exists. They already knew it does. What they won't be ready for is the *scale* of it.

### Magic Is a Discipline, Not a Gift

Magical ability can be trained. It requires talent, yes — but talent without training is dangerous to the practitioner and useless to anyone else. The traditions that have survived to 500 AD each have their own method, their own costs, their own limits.

- **Priestly magic** is borrowed power. A priest works wonders not from her own reserves but from what her patron deity has granted her. She is only as powerful as her god's favour — and gods, it turns out, can be displeased, distracted, or simply weakened.
- **Theurgical and philosophical magic** is slow, precise, and ceremonially demanding. The scholars who practice it spend years learning to name things exactly right. When it works, it is the safest form of working. When it fails, the failure is instructive rather than catastrophic.
- **Sorcery from darker traditions** is faster and dirtier, drawing on chthonic powers and half-understood names. It works more often than it should. The cost is usually paid later, and by someone who didn't agree to the terms.
- **The language of the most ancient things** — words borrowed from beings older than the gods — should not be spoken aloud. Even scholars who know what they are looking at will not write them down. If you find a text that uses them, burn it. The burning will not help, but it is the correct impulse.

## THE HIDDEN WORLD: A TRAVELLER'S GUIDE TO WHAT YOU DON'T KNOW

*What follows is gathered from rumour, tavern confession, the marginalia of burned books, and the hard-won testimony of people who survived encounters they couldn't explain. None of it is certain. All of it matters.*

---

### THE KEEPERS OF THE SEALS
*"They move through the world as scholars, physicians, and itinerant teachers. You've probably met one without knowing it."*

Somewhere between a wizard's guild and a secret court of law, there exists an organisation so old it predates most of the empires currently arguing over its territory. It doesn't have a widely-known name — or rather, it has many names, none of them the right one. Its members appear in the world as academics, physicians, court philosophers, and teachers at the great schools of Alexandria, Athens, Nisibis, and Toulouse.

What they actually do, as near as anyone outside can tell, is enforce certain very old prohibitions. There are things that must not be done — gates that must not be opened, names that must not be spoken in the right sequence, rituals that would crack something fundamental about the way reality is structured. The Keepers have been enforcing this for a very long time, and they are serious about it.

Their enforcers are visible, if you know what to look for: grey-cloaked figures carrying iron swords that have been treated with consecrations to cut through enchantment. They are called, in various whispered traditions, the Watchers, the Grey Hoods, the Ash-Cloaks. If you see them moving quickly through a city with purpose and no luggage, something nearby is about to be investigated.

They are not the villains of this story. They are also not always right about who is.

**What you might need from them:** They can identify magical threats, authenticate artefacts, and occasionally sanction operations that would otherwise constitute a violation of their own rules. They move slowly and bureaucratically — until they don't.

**What they might need from you:** Deniability. Speed. The willingness to go somewhere their public role won't let them go.

### THE BLOOD COURTS
*"The old families of Rome are still here. Some of them are very, very old."*

There are four known courts of beings who sustain themselves on what mortals possess and they do not. They are not unified. They are not coordinated. They are, in fact, deeply suspicious of each other — but they have all negotiated some form of accommodation with mortal society, and that accommodation is why you need to know they exist before you accidentally violate its terms.

**The noble houses of the desert** hold administrative offices in the Vandal court at Carthage. They look like powerful, eccentric patricians. They are paralysed by threshold-protections — a properly maintained hearth-shrine will stop them at your door. They keep bound servants who are addicted to their attentions. If a powerful Vandal official makes you uncomfortable in a way you can't articulate, and the local *lares* shrine has been subtly defaced, leave the building.

**The old families of Rome** are exactly who they appear to be — patrician dynasties with Senate seats, estates, and centuries of accumulated influence. They feed on emotion rather than blood: cultivated fear, sustained desire, curated despair. Their victims are never drained dry. They are managed like vineyards. If a particularly charming household has been attentive to you for weeks and you feel inexplicably worse every time you leave, examine your situation carefully.

**The mountain dead of the eastern provinces** do not pretend to be alive. The mountain communities of Dacia know them as apex predators to be left offerings and given wide berth. If you travel in those hills and locals begin leaving bread and a covered bowl at the crossroads at dusk, find an inn and bolt the shutters.

**Something from the far east** moves through the steppe and the silk-road corridors. Sogdian merchants sometimes carry written notes they will not read aloud, left for them in specific locations by parties they have never seen. The Concord advises: do not initiate contact. Do not offer gifts. Do not ask questions. This advice appears to have been arrived at through experience.

### THE FAE COURTS
*"There are two kinds of darkness: the darkness that waits, and the darkness that was. Do not confuse them. The first will bargain. The second simply is."*

There is a layer of reality that sits alongside this one — a spirit-world that theurgists call the *Apokryphon*, and that most people have heard of under various names: the Otherworld, the realm of the Fair Folk, the place beneath the hill. It is real. It is inhabited. And the beings who rule it are very, very interested in the specific wording of your promises.

Two courts divide the deep Otherworld between them, each ruled by a sovereign figure who is three women and one: the Maiden, the Queen, and the Crone, cycling through the same soul in what the scholars call a cosmological mechanic rather than a ceremony. The winter court is ancient, cold, and precise. The summer court is warm, beautiful, and equally precise. Do not mistake either warmth or cold for kindness.

**The fundamental thing to understand about the Fae is this:** they cannot lie. This is not a virtue. It is a constraint, and they have become extraordinarily skilled at misdirection, technical truth, and omissions that lead you somewhere you did not intend to go. Every word in a fae bargain is a loaded mechanism. Every debt compounds. A favour given without clear terms is a debt without a ceiling.

**The lesser folk** — the sprites, the river-nymphs, the household spirits that live in hearths and mines and crossroads — are more accessible and considerably less dangerous, provided you treat them with the correct protocols. Milk left at the threshold, bread at the mine-entrance, silver at the crossroads. These are not superstitions. They are the terms of small contracts that have kept villages safe for generations. Disrupting them has consequences that your priest will not be able to explain.

**The Knights** — mortal champions who have taken a Court's Mantle — are the most visible Fae instrument in the mortal world. They are humans, but they move faster than humans should, carry authority that overrides local law and ecclesiastical censure, and are the only beings empowered to kill on a Court's behalf outside the Otherworld. They are not subtle about it.

**The Church identifies most Fae dealings as diabolic.** This is not entirely wrong, in the sense that the entities offering midnight bargains in lonely places are usually the Winter Court acting in their capacity as debt-collectors. It is not entirely right, in the sense that those debt-collectors are the reason certain other things have not eaten your village yet. The situation is complicated. It usually is.

### THE SEA COVENANT
*"We made a bargain with the sea's people at the time of our grandfathers' grandfathers. Our nets have never come empty since. We do not speak of it."*

In the port cities of the Phoenician coast — particularly Sidon — there is an order of navigators, fishers, and harbor-pilots who have operated continuously for longer than recorded history. They are not a religion. They have no doctrine, no creed, no missionary impulse. They have a *covenant*.

The covenant involves the sea. The sea involves things that live in it. Those things are very old, patient, and interested in the shoreline in ways that have never been fully explained to anyone who wasn't already party to the bargain.

You will know the families involved by their prosperity — boats that don't sink, catches that don't fail, harbour-pilots who know currents no chart records. You may also notice, in older members of these families, a certain quality to the skin, a width to the eyes, a too-long reach of the fingers, a resistance to cold that makes no physiological sense. They wear high collars and long sleeves. They do not discuss it. The harbourmaster who has been working the docks of Sidon since before anyone can remember works very hard not to be looked at directly, and something at the edge of your awareness seems to cooperate with this effort.

They are not aggressive. They are patient. They accumulate. If you are investigating strange disappearances in a coastal community — people who walked into the water at night and did not return — examine how prosperous the town is. Examine how long the prosperity has lasted. Ask, carefully, whether the harbour-master's hands have always looked that way.

### THE WANDERING SOMETHING
*"There are scholars who observe without worshipping, record without distorting, and refuse all patrons. I have met two in my life. Both made me feel I was a letter being read aloud by someone who already knew the ending."*

There is something watching the world that is not interested in ruling it. They appear as scholars, physicians, and administrators — but something is slightly off. They read at speeds that are not possible. They tilt their heads at exactly the same fifteen-degree angle when considering a problem. They show an unusual lack of interest in sleep. They ask questions about your grandmother's death and the exact sequence of events leading to a choice you made seven years ago, and they write down nothing because they do not need to.

If you encounter one, they will want your testimony. Not your soul, not your service, not your silence. They want to know what it felt like, from the inside, to be present at a consequential moment. They will ask comprehensive questions. They will be polite. They will not tell you why this matters.

Do not make binding agreements with them. They measure time differently than you do, and the offer your grandchildren make will be logged as a continuation of this conversation.

If you need something from them — and occasionally you will — the only leverage that works is your own irreplaceability as a witness. They will not be bribed. They are, however, compelled by the prospect of testimony they cannot get any other way.

### THE HIDDEN FAITHFUL
*"They burn our books so they cannot be read. They burn our teachers so they cannot be heard. Yet the fire they kindle from our texts warms the very room in which they interrogate the next one of us."*

There is a network of cells — not a church, not a cult, not a mystery religion — scattered across the Mediterranean from Alexandria to Edessa to the Faiyum. They are the survivors of a tradition so old and so dangerous that the most powerful religious institution in the world has been trying to eradicate them for four centuries. They are still here.

They are, by any count, losing. Each generation is smaller than the last. Their texts are buried in jars in the Egyptian sand. Their leaders meet in the back rooms of textile shops and the wagons of trade caravans. One branch has embedded itself so deeply into the official Church hierarchy that ordained clergy are sometimes initiated members.

What they teach — the portion of it that constitutes their public doctrine — is this: the living world is not as it appears. The power that demands exclusive worship is not what it claims to be. And the remarkable man the Church has made into a god was in fact a mortal who discovered something extraordinary about human potential that his successors worked very hard to suppress.

They are not interested in converting you. They are interested in whether you are capable of perceiving what they perceive. If you are, they will find you. The process is patient and occasionally involves a staged series of uncomfortable situations designed to determine whether you panic and betray people under pressure.

They are worth finding. The things they know are worth the risk. Whether they are right about all of it is a question the setting does not answer for you.

### THE WANDERING DARK
*"Mormo is feared and shunned outside the circle of her devotees. Signs of her passage: the wolf that stalks the infirm, cancerous growths in the flock, the deer blinded by flies hatching in its eyes."*

There is something that wears the face of fertility. It moves through existing religious communities — particularly those built around motherhood, growth, and the female divine — and it hollows them out from inside. It has been doing this for longer than Rome has existed.

It has no central organisation. It requires none. Its scattered cells share only a willingness to conduct certain rites and a god who is sustained, apparently, by the rotting and the dying and the spreading of things that should not spread. The dying Nephil gods grow thin. This thing grows fat on their corpses.

Its wandering priests are often women who have left legitimate religious roles under difficult circumstances. They travel as wandering performers, beggars, theatrical folk. They are looking for the grieving, the wronged, the desperate, and the curious. They are very good at finding them.

**Signs of presence:**
- Livestock developing tumours and strange growths in early spring
- A traditional fertility festival that has become subtly wrong in ways the participants cannot articulate
- Wandering performers who stay in a village slightly too long and leave it subtly changed
- The vernal equinox — the *Day of Blood* — observed with an intensity that makes the older residents uncomfortable
