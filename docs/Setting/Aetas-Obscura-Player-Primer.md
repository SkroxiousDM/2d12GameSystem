# AETAS OBSCURA
## *A Campaign Primer for Players*
### *Year of the Consulship of Patricius and Hypatius — Anno Domini 500*

---

> *"The world is ending. It has been ending for a hundred years. And somewhere, beneath the rot, something is waking up and calling it an opportunity."*
> — overheard in a tavern in Ravenna, attributed to no one

---

## HOW TO READ THIS DOCUMENT

This primer is written for **players**. Everything here is knowledge your characters could plausibly have — the shape of the world they live in, the folklore they grew up with, and the rumours a well-travelled or well-connected person might have collected about the hidden powers that move beneath ordinary life.

It is deliberately incomplete. Much of what follows is *rumour, folklore, and hard-won hearsay*, presented the way your characters would actually encounter it: as fragments, warnings, and traveller's tales rather than settled fact. Some of it is wrong. Some of it is half-right in dangerous ways. The truth of these things — what they really are, what they really want, and how they really work — is the story you are going to discover at the table.

When this document says *"it is said"* or *"they whisper"* or *"the folklore claims,"* treat that literally. Nobody has handed your character a verified encyclopedia of the supernatural. They have handed you the map that superstitious, frightened, clever people have drawn in the dark.

## THE WORLD YOU LIVE IN

Rome is gone.

Not forgotten — not yet. The roads still work. The law is still cited. Latin is still the tongue of court and contract from Ravenna to Constantinople. Senators still meet in Rome. But the legions that held the frontier for five centuries are mercenary warbands now, paid in land and promises, loyal to kings with names like Theodoric and Clovis rather than to any emperor. The Western Empire died in the lifetime of your grandparents, and most people are still quietly waiting for it to come back.

It isn't coming back.

The world in 500 AD is a patchwork of successor kingdoms, provincial holdouts, and border zones where no single power can guarantee you safe passage from one city to the next. The Ostrogoths rule Italy from Ravenna, and their king Theodoric governs with a competence that makes some Romans nostalgic and others uneasy. The Franks are consolidating Gaul under a king who recently converted to Christianity — whether out of faith or politics, no one agrees. The Vandals hold North Africa and the great grain-city of Carthage. The Visigoths hold Hispania and the south of Gaul. The Eastern Empire endures in Constantinople, rich and intact, presiding over theological arguments so bitter that neighbouring bishops have had each other murdered over the exact nature of Christ's divinity. Persia watches from across the Euphrates, patient as it has always been.

This is a world in motion. Everything is provisional. Everything is up for grabs. Old borders have dissolved and new ones have not yet hardened. A family that was powerful under the Empire may be beggared now; a warband captain's grandson may hold a city. Trust is local, loyalty is negotiable, and a letter of introduction is worth more than a sword on most roads.

Three faiths contend for the soul of this world. **Nicene Christianity** — the faith of Rome, Constantinople, and the newly-converted Franks — is ascendant and increasingly intolerant of rivals. **Arian Christianity** — a different reading of Christ's nature — is the faith of most of the Germanic kings, the Goths and Vandals, and so the ruler and the ruled often pray in different churches. And beneath both, the **old gods** have not entirely died: in the countryside, in the mountains, in the harbours and the market-towns, people still leave offerings at the crossroads and the hearth, still fear the wrong festival observed the wrong way, still know the names their grandparents warned them not to say after dark.

And the hidden world — the one your characters are about to become entangled in — is *more visible* than it has ever been, or may ever be again. When the great institutions weaken, the things they used to hold at bay come closer to the surface.

## WHAT YOUR CHARACTERS KNOW ABOUT THE WORLD

### The Supernatural Is Real

This is not a matter of debate for people of this age. The gods are real. Spirits walk alongside the living. Prayers sometimes work. Rituals performed correctly produce results that cannot be explained by philosophy or medicine. The question is never *whether* the supernatural is real — only *what kind* of real it is, and whose version of it is pointing at you right now.

When your character encounters something impossible, they will probably be frightened. But they will not be paralysed by the proof that magic exists. They already knew it does. What they won't be ready for is the *scale* of it — the sense, in certain places and certain company, that the world is far larger and far stranger than any priest or philosopher has ever admitted.

### The Shape of Reality, As People Understand It

Educated people — theurgists, philosophers, the more learned clergy — speak of reality as having layers. The everyday world, the world of flesh and stone and sunlight, is the one everyone lives in. Alongside it, or beneath it, or just behind it, is a **spirit-world**: the realm the theurgists call the *Apokryphon* and that ordinary folk know as the Otherworld, the realm of the Fair Folk, the place beneath the hill, the country of the dead and the dreaming. It is real, it is inhabited, and in certain places — old barrows, deep forests, the thresholds between one thing and another — the border between it and the waking world grows thin.

And beyond even that, the folklore speaks of something further out and further down: a **beyond** that has no comfortable name. The philosophers call it the Outer Dark; the frightened call it many things and prefer not to. Most people never think about it at all, and are better for it. It is the place the oldest prohibitions exist to keep shut. You do not need to understand it. You need only understand that some of the powers in this primer are very interested in it, and that this interest is the single most dangerous thing about them.

### The Old Laws

There is a persistent belief, shared across many traditions that otherwise agree on nothing, that the world is held together by a small number of very old **laws** — prohibitions, really, rather than descriptions. There are things that must not be done. Gates that must not be opened. Names that must not be spoken in a certain order. Bindings that must not be broken. The folklore numbers these prohibitions differently depending on who is telling the story, but a recurring figure is **seven**.

Nobody agrees on where these laws came from. Some say the gods set them; some say something older than the gods. What everyone who takes them seriously agrees on is that breaking one is not like breaking a human law, where the harm is local and the punishment comes after. Breaking one of the old laws is understood to do damage to the structure of the world itself. This is why the beings who enforce them (see *The Keepers of the Seals*, below) are so grimly humourless about it.

### Magic Is a Discipline, Not a Gift

Magical ability can be trained. It requires talent, yes — but talent without training is dangerous to the practitioner and useless to anyone else. The traditions that have survived to 500 AD each have their own method, their own costs, their own limits.

- **Priestly magic** is borrowed power. A priest works wonders not from her own reserves but from what her patron deity has granted her. She is only as powerful as her god's favour — and gods, it turns out, can be displeased, distracted, or simply weakened. When a god's power wanes, so does the priest's, often without warning and never at a convenient moment.
- **Theurgical and philosophical magic** is slow, precise, and ceremonially demanding. The scholars who practice it spend years learning to name things exactly right — to know the true and proper name of a thing, and to speak it in the correct order, with the correct preparation. When it works, it is the safest form of working. When it fails, the failure is usually instructive rather than catastrophic.
- **Sorcery from darker traditions** is faster and dirtier, drawing on chthonic powers and half-understood names. It works more often than it should. The cost is usually paid later, and by someone who didn't agree to the terms. Many a village has a story of the cunning-man whose bargains came due.
- **The language of the most ancient things** — words borrowed from beings older than the gods — should not be spoken aloud. Even scholars who know what they are looking at will not write them down. If you find a text that uses them, burn it. The burning will not help, but it is the correct impulse.

### A Note on What Magic *Costs*

Every tradition agrees on one uncomfortable point: power comes from somewhere. It is not free, it is not infinite, and it is not created out of nothing by good intentions. The priest borrows and must repay in service. The theurgist spends years and hours and health. The sorcerer defers the bill and someone else pays it. Whenever a character is offered power that seems to cost nothing, the correct response is not gratitude. It is suspicion.

## THE HIDDEN WORLD: A TRAVELLER'S GUIDE TO WHAT YOU DON'T KNOW

*What follows is gathered from rumour, tavern confession, the marginalia of burned books, and the hard-won testimony of people who survived encounters they couldn't explain. None of it is certain. All of it matters. Where this guide states something as fact, understand that it is stating what people believe — and people, especially frightened people, are frequently wrong about exactly the things that matter most.*

---

### THE KEEPERS OF THE SEALS
*"They move through the world as scholars, physicians, and itinerant teachers. You've probably met one without knowing it."*

Somewhere between a wizard's guild and a secret court of law, there exists an organisation so old it predates most of the empires currently arguing over its territory. It doesn't have a widely-known name — or rather, it has many names, none of them the right one. Its members appear in the world as academics, physicians, court philosophers, and teachers at the great schools of Alexandria, Athens, Nisibis, and Toulouse.

What they actually do, as near as anyone outside can tell, is enforce the old laws — the prohibitions described above. There are things that must not be done, and the Keepers have appointed themselves (or been appointed by someone) to see that they aren't. They investigate rumours of forbidden workings. They confiscate dangerous texts. They intervene, sometimes lethally, when someone gets close to opening something that should stay shut. They have been doing this for a very long time, and they are serious about it in the way that only people who have personally seen the consequences can be.

Their enforcers are visible, if you know what to look for: grey-cloaked figures carrying iron swords that have been treated with consecrations to cut through enchantment. They are called, in various whispered traditions, the Watchers, the Grey Hoods, the Ash-Cloaks. If you see them moving quickly through a city with purpose and no luggage, something nearby is about to be investigated.

They are not the villains of this story. They are also not always right about who is. They are cautious, bureaucratic, and slow to trust, and their caution has occasionally let a real horror grow while they debated jurisdiction. But when they decide to move, they move decisively, and they do not much care whose plans they interrupt.

**What you might need from them:** They can identify magical threats, authenticate artefacts, and occasionally sanction operations that would otherwise constitute a violation of their own rules. They move slowly and bureaucratically — until they don't.

**What they might need from you:** Deniability. Speed. The willingness to go somewhere their public role won't let them go, and to do something they cannot be seen to have ordered.

### THE BLOOD COURTS
*"The old families of Rome are still here. Some of them are very, very old."*

There are, by the reckoning of those who study such things, four known courts of beings who sustain themselves on what mortals possess and they do not. They are not unified. They are not coordinated. They are, in fact, deeply suspicious of one another — old rivals with long memories and longer grudges. But they have all negotiated some form of accommodation with mortal society, and that accommodation is precisely why you need to know they exist before you accidentally violate its terms. The fastest way to die in this world is to break a rule you didn't know was there.

**The noble houses of the desert** hold administrative offices in the Vandal court at Carthage and in the old towns of Egypt. They look like powerful, eccentric patricians of the newly-arrived ruling class. The folklore about them is specific and worth heeding: they are said to be *paralysed by thresholds* — a properly maintained hearth-shrine, an intact doorway-ward, will stop them at your door as surely as a wall. They keep bound servants — sometimes called cup-slaves — who are wholly, wretchedly addicted to their masters' attentions and will do anything to remain in favour. If a powerful Vandal official makes you uncomfortable in a way you can't articulate, and the local household shrine has been subtly defaced, leave the building and do not return after dark.

**The old families of Rome** are exactly who they appear to be — patrician dynasties with Senate seats, estates, and centuries of accumulated influence. What sets them apart is what they feed on: not blood, but *emotion*. Cultivated fear. Sustained desire. Curated despair, savoured slowly. Their victims are never drained dry, because a dead victim feeds no one; they are managed like vineyards, kept alive and productive for years. If a particularly charming and well-connected household has been attentive to you for weeks, and you find yourself feeling inexplicably worse every time you leave their company — hollowed out, diminished, obscurely afraid — examine your situation very carefully, and consider who benefits from your unhappiness.

**The mountain dead of the eastern provinces** do not pretend to be alive. In the highlands of Dacia and the Carpathian passes they are known plainly as the walking dead — apex predators to be left offerings and given a wide berth. The folk remedies against them are old and consistent: they are said to be turned by running water and thresholds, repelled by garlic and by certain consecrated things, and destroyed by the sun, which they cannot endure. They are also said to be bound to their native earth, unable to travel far from the soil of their own graves. If you travel in those hills and the locals begin leaving bread and a covered bowl at the crossroads at dusk, do not mock them. Find an inn and bolt the shutters.

**Something from the far east** moves through the steppe and the silk-road corridors, and the little that is known of it is known mostly through the care people take to avoid it. Sogdian merchants sometimes carry written notes they will not read aloud, left for them in specific locations by parties they have never seen and do not wish to meet. Those who have brushed against this power and lived speak of a killing that comes from a distance, without a mark, without a touch. The Keepers' advice, arrived at (one suspects) through hard experience, is simple: do not initiate contact. Do not offer gifts. Do not ask questions. Whatever it is, it is old, it is patient, and it is best left entirely alone.

### THE TWILIGHT COURTS
*"There are two kinds of darkness: the darkness that waits, and the darkness that was. Do not confuse them. The first will bargain. The second simply is."*

The spirit-world — the Otherworld, the *Apokryphon*, the realm of the Fair Folk — is real, it is inhabited, and the beings who rule its deeper reaches are very, very interested in the specific wording of your promises.

Two courts divide the deep Otherworld between them. Each is ruled by a sovereign figure spoken of as three-women-and-one: the **Maiden**, the **Queen**, and the **Crone**, three aspects turning through a single throne like the turning of a year. One court is often called the **Winter Court** — ancient, cold, precise, and merciless. The other is the **Summer Court** — warm, beautiful, generous-seeming, and no less precise. Do not mistake either warmth or cold for kindness. The seasons are how they present themselves to mortals; the appetite beneath is the same.

**The fundamental thing to understand about the Fair Folk is this:** they cannot lie. This is not a virtue. It is a constraint — and they have become extraordinarily skilled at misdirection, technical truth, flattering ambiguity, and omissions that lead you exactly where they wanted you to go while every word they spoke was true. Every clause in a fae bargain is a loaded mechanism. Every debt compounds. A favour accepted without clear terms is a debt without a ceiling, and they are endlessly patient about collecting.

**The lesser folk** — the sprites, the river-nymphs, the household spirits that live in hearths and mines and crossroads — are more accessible and considerably less dangerous, *provided you observe the correct protocols*. Milk left at the threshold. Bread at the mine-entrance. Silver at the crossroads. A clean hearth and a civil word. These are not empty superstitions; they are the terms of small, ancient contracts that have kept villages safe for generations. Disrupting them — mocking them, skipping them, letting the offering-bowl go dry — has consequences that your priest will not be able to explain and your neighbours will blame on you.

**The Church names most fae dealings as diabolic.** This is not entirely wrong: the entities offering midnight bargains in lonely places are often the Winter Court acting as debt-collectors, and their gifts damn as surely as any devil's. It is also not entirely right, in the sense that those same debt-collectors are, by some accounts, part of the reason certain *other* and worse things have not yet eaten your village. The situation is complicated. In this world, it usually is.

### THE SEA COVENANT
*"We made a bargain with the sea's people at the time of our grandfathers' grandfathers. Our nets have never come empty since. We do not speak of it."*

In the port cities of the Phoenician coast — particularly ancient Sidon — there is an order of navigators, fishers, and harbour-pilots who have operated continuously for longer than recorded history. They are not a religion. They have no doctrine, no creed, no missionary impulse. They have a *covenant*.

The covenant involves the sea, and the sea involves things that live in it — things that are very old, very patient, and interested in the shoreline in ways that have never been fully explained to anyone who wasn't already party to the bargain. Those inside the covenant prosper. That is the whole of what outsiders reliably know, and even that they mostly infer.

You will know the families involved by their prosperity: boats that don't sink, catches that don't fail in years when everyone else goes hungry, harbour-pilots who know currents no chart records. And you may notice, in the *older* members of these families, something harder to name — a certain quality to the skin, a width and flatness to the eyes, a too-long reach of the fingers, a strange resistance to cold. They favour high collars and long sleeves. They do not discuss it, and there is something about them that makes it difficult to look directly, difficult to remember clearly, difficult to ask the obvious question.

They are not aggressive. They are patient. They accumulate — wealth, influence, and, over generations, families. If you find yourself investigating strange disappearances in a coastal community — people who walked into the water at night and did not return, always at certain seasons — examine how prosperous the town is, and for how long the prosperity has lasted, and whether the disappearances and the good fortune began in the same generation. Then ask, carefully, whether the harbour-master's hands have always looked that way.

### THE WANDERING SCHOLARS
*"There are scholars who observe without worshipping, record without distorting, and refuse all patrons. I have met two in my life. Both made me feel I was a letter being read aloud by someone who already knew the ending."*

There is something watching the world that does not appear to want to rule it. They pass as scholars, physicians, and administrators — competent, unremarkable, easy to overlook — but something is slightly *off*. They read at speeds that should not be possible. They tilt their heads at exactly the same fifteen-degree angle when considering a problem, every one of them, as though it were a shared habit learned in the same unimaginable school. They show an unusual indifference to sleep, to food, to fear. They ask questions about your grandmother's death, and about the exact sequence of small choices that led to a decision you made seven years ago — and they write nothing down, because they do not need to.

If you encounter one, they will want your *testimony*. Not your soul, not your service, not your silence, not your worship — your testimony. They want to know what it *felt like*, from the inside, to be present at a consequential moment. They will ask thorough, patient, uncomfortably precise questions. They will be unfailingly polite. And they will not tell you why any of it matters.

Do not make binding agreements with them. They measure time on a scale that makes a human lifetime an afternoon, and an offer you make today may be treated as still open when your great-grandchildren are old. What was a favour to you will be, to them, one clause of a single unbroken conversation.

If you ever need something from them — and occasionally you will, for they know things no one else has bothered to remember — the only leverage that works is your own irreplaceability as a witness. They cannot be bribed, threatened, or flattered. They can, however, be *drawn* by the prospect of testimony they cannot obtain any other way. Be careful what you trade. You will not understand the price until much later.

### THE HIDDEN FAITHFUL
*"They burn our books so they cannot be read. They burn our teachers so they cannot be heard. Yet the fire they kindle from our texts warms the very room in which they interrogate the next one of us."*

There is a network of cells — not a church, not a cult, not a mystery religion in the usual sense — scattered across the Mediterranean from Alexandria to Edessa to the villages of the Faiyum. They are the survivors of a tradition so old and, in the eyes of the dominant Church, so dangerous that the most powerful religious institution in the world has been trying to eradicate them for four centuries. They are still here. Barely.

They are, by any honest count, losing. Each generation is smaller than the last. Their sacred texts are buried in sealed jars in the Egyptian sand, hidden from the fires. Their teachers meet in the back rooms of textile shops, in the wagons of trade caravans, in cellars and catacombs. Most remarkably of all, one branch is said to have embedded itself so deeply into the official Church hierarchy that some ordained clergy — men who preach orthodoxy on Sunday — are quietly initiated members of the very thing they are meant to be stamping out.

What they teach — the portion of it that constitutes their public doctrine, the part they will share with an outsider they are testing — runs roughly thus: *the living world is not as it appears.* The power that demands exclusive worship and brooks no rivals is not what it claims to be. And the remarkable man the Church has raised into a god was, they say, in truth a *mortal* — a teacher who discovered something extraordinary about human potential, something his followers understood and his enemies worked very hard to bury.

They are not interested in converting you. They are interested in whether you are *capable of perceiving* what they perceive — whether you can look at the world and see the seams. If you can, they will find you; the approach is patient and sometimes involves a staged series of uncomfortable trials designed to learn whether you panic and betray your companions under pressure. If you fail those tests, you will simply never hear from them again.

They are worth finding. The things they know are worth the risk of knowing them. Whether they are *right* about all of it — about the world, the false power, the mortal made god — is a question the truth of your campaign will answer in its own time, and not before.

### THE WANDERING DARK
*"Mormo is feared and shunned outside the circle of her devotees. Signs of her passage: the wolf that stalks the infirm, cancerous growths in the flock, the deer blinded by flies hatching in its eyes."*

There is something that wears the face of fertility, and it is one of the most feared names in this primer.

It moves through existing religious communities — particularly those built around motherhood, growth, the harvest, and the female divine — and it hollows them out from the inside, so that a rite meant to bless the fields becomes something else while the worshippers still believe they are doing what their grandmothers did. It has been doing this, the folklore insists, for longer than Rome has existed.

It has no central organisation and requires none. Its scattered cells share only a willingness to conduct certain rites and a devotion to a power that is nourished, apparently, by *corruption* — by the rotting and the dying, the tumour and the blight, the spreading of things that should not spread. Where it passes, the folklore says, the land itself sickens in small wrong ways before anyone understands why.

Its wandering priestesses are often women who have left legitimate religious roles under difficult circumstances — the disgraced, the exiled, the bereaved who were promised something in their grief. They travel as wandering performers, beggars, and theatrical folk, and they are hunting: for the grieving, the wronged, the desperate, and the curious. They are very, very good at finding them, because those people are always, in every village, easy to find.

**Signs of presence:**
- Livestock developing tumours and strange growths, especially in early spring
- A traditional fertility festival that has become subtly *wrong* in ways the participants cannot quite articulate
- Wandering performers who linger in a village a little too long and leave it somehow changed
- The vernal equinox — a day some whisper of as the *Day of Blood* — observed with an intensity that makes the older residents deeply uncomfortable

The cults that hunt this thing wear the blue of the Madonna and count it their sworn enemy. The Keepers track it. Neither has ever managed to destroy it. It thrives wherever a civilisation rots, and the fifth century — a century of collapse, plague, famine, and despair — has given it more to feed on than any age in living memory.

### THE BROTHERHOOD OF THE OPEN GATE
*"They do not want power. They do not want gold. They do not want to rule the world that is. They want to open the door and let in the world that comes after. Pray you never meet one who is sincere."*

Of all the rumoured powers a traveller might hear of, this is the one the wise fear most — because it is the only one that does not want *anything you could bargain with.*

The other hidden powers of this world, for all their appetites, mostly want to *keep* the world: to feed on it, rule it, prosper within it, endure inside it. This one is different. The Brotherhood — it goes by no single name, and the names it does use are best not written — is said to be a scattered brotherhood of scholars, priests, and sorcerers who have concluded that the world as it is should *end*, and who labour patiently across generations to bring that ending about. They are the ones who study the old prohibitions not to enforce them, but to learn how to break them. They are the reason the Keepers of the Seals never truly rest.

They are subtle. They do not announce themselves. They work through respectable men — a learned priest, a wealthy patron of the arts, a scholar with an unusual library and an unusual patience. They collect forbidden texts. They seek out the sealed places and the buried names. They are willing to spend a lifetime setting up a single ritual their grandchildren will complete, because to them a human lifetime is merely a convenient unit of labour toward an end that does not care about time.

What, precisely, they are trying to *open* — and what they believe waits on the other side — is not something any sane person claims to know, and the few texts that describe it are exactly the texts one is advised to burn on sight. It is enough to know this: if you ever cross a conspiracy whose members seem to want nothing you can give, refuse nothing they can safely refuse, and pursue some incomprehensible goal with serene, generational patience — a goal that seems, the more you learn of it, to be *worse for everyone including themselves* — you may have found them. Take what you have learned to the Keepers of the Seals, and do not investigate further alone.

**Signs of presence:**
- A learned or wealthy man of good reputation with an inexplicable interest in sealed ruins, forbidden texts, and the oldest and worst names
- A ritual site being prepared with enormous care and no apparent purpose that benefits anyone
- The sudden, quiet interest of the grey-cloaked Keepers in a person or place you had thought entirely ordinary
- Scholars and clergy dying, or vanishing, along a line that seems to point somewhere on a map

## A FEW LAST WORDS OF ADVICE

For your character, embarking on this world, the accumulated wisdom of everyone who has survived it comes down to a handful of rules:

- **Assume the rules are real.** The offering at the threshold, the ban on the certain name, the festival done exactly so — these customs exist because people who ignored them died. You will not know which superstition is load-bearing until you break it.
- **Free power is the most expensive kind.** Everything in the hidden world runs on exchange. If a thing offers you strength, safety, wealth, or knowledge and names no price, the price is simply one you haven't been shown yet.
- **Words are weapons and words are chains.** Especially with the Fair Folk, the Wandering Scholars, and anything that offers a bargain. Say less than you want to. Promise nothing you have not read three times. And never, ever, agree to terms you do not fully understand.
- **The Church is powerful, sincere, and often wrong about the details.** It will name many different things "devil" and treat them alike. Some of those things are worse than devils. Some are, in a strange way, on your side. Learn to tell them apart, because your priest cannot.
- **When you are out of your depth, you are probably still underestimating how deep it is.** The scale of this world exceeds what any one person can safely know. The characters who last are the ones who learn to recognise the moment to stop pulling the thread — and to find someone older, wiser, or better-armed before they pull it further.

The world is ending. It has been ending for a hundred years.

What you do in the ruins is up to you.
