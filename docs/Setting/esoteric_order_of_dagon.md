# THE ESOTERIC ORDER OF DAGON

> *"Among them is no distinction of sex, and they breed in some way I cannot endure to think about. Perhaps that is why they are so unlike us — and yet so strangely near."*
>
> — Attributed to Philo of Byblos, *Phoenician History*, fragment preserved in the Concord archive at the Castrum (c. 60 AD)

> *"We made a bargain with the sea's people at the time of our grandfathers' grandfathers, and our nets have never come back empty since. If there are some among us who are changed by that bargain, we do not speak of it. We do not look upon them. We give them what they need, and they give us what we need, and we ask no questions about what is done on certain nights in the tidal caves beneath the headland."*
>
> — Deposition of a Sidonian fisherman before a Concord observer, c. 420 AD, logged in the Castrum's Eastern Archive

> *"The fish-god is not a god. I cannot make you understand what I mean by that sentence. He is something for which there is no word in Greek or Latin. The pagan gods, at least, are our cousins — they are the children of the descent on Hermon, walking in flesh, eating worship as we eat bread. Dagon is not any of these things. He is older and he is wetter and whatever you do, do not go to Sidon at the dark of the moon."*
>
> — From the private correspondence of **Iamblichos of Chalkis** to his student Sopater, c. 318 AD

> *"The sea gives and the sea takes. The sea does not ask permission."*
>
> — Inscribed in Phoenician and in an older script above the seaward gate of the Sidon chapter-house of the Order, translation by Concord observer **Yohannan the Syrian**, 490 AD


The **Esoteric Order of Dagon** (*Ordo Esotericus Dagonis*) is the oldest surviving covenant between humankind and the creatures that live in the earth's deep waters. It is not a religion in any sense that a Roman, a Christian, or even a worshipper of the old Canaanite gods would recognize. Its members do not believe in Dagon. They belong to him — by blood, by the slow weight of inherited change, and by the oldest agreement on record between the mortal world and something that long predates it. In 500 AD the Order persists in three principal centers — the ancestral temple-city of Sidon on the Lebanese coast, a Persian Gulf enclave in the shallows off the Arabian shore, and the storm-battered rump of a once-great Atlantic chapter on the Irish island of Innis Mor — and in scattered pockets of the Adriatic and the Aegean wherever a coastal village has quietly produced too many children with wide-set eyes and too-pale gills behind their ears.

It is not a cult in the way the Cult of Mormo is a cult: there is no agenda of apocalyptic paving-the-way, no recruitment of the unwilling, no orchestrated spread of infection and rot. The Order is patient in the way the sea is patient. It waits. It accumulates. Its members marry carefully and breed deliberately. They prosper in the trades and in the fisheries and in harbor-piloting and in the manufacture of the purple dye that has made the Phoenician coast famous across three millennia. They grow old, and as they age their bodies change in ways that can no longer be hidden, and eventually they walk down to the water one last night and do not come back. What continues beneath the surface — in the underwater complexes off Sidon and in the cathedral vaults of the Drowned Library where the tablets older than Sumer sit in cold darkness — is another Order entirely, one that has been running without interruption for a very long time.

## THE NATURE OF DAGON: A SPECIAL CASE

Most gods worshipped in 500 AD are **Nephilim**: the half-divine children of the Watchers who descended on Mount Hermon, born of the coupling of those angelic powers with mortal women, beautiful and long-lived and *hungry* for the worship-harvest that sustains their unnatural lives (see §3.4.A). The standard Phoenician and Canaanite pantheon — Baal-Hadad, Astarte, Moloch, El — follow this pattern precisely. The Inner Seal of the Concord tracks their declining cult-pools with the resigned satisfaction of scholars watching a predictable process.

**Dagon is not in this catalogue.** The Concord's Nephilim roster notes him as *a special case* and declines to elaborate. The elaboration is kept in the Castrum's innermost archive under three separate Seal-bindings, because the Concord has learned — at some cost — that the full truth about Dagon tends to produce one of two outcomes in the scholar who reads it: flight into madness or conversion to the Order's own service.

What can be said safely is this:

**Father Dagon** and his consort **Mother Hydra** are not Nephilim. They are not, properly speaking, gods in the Nephilim sense at all. They do not feed on worship-harvest in the way that Astarte or Baal-Hadad did. When the Order's rituals are performed and offerings made to Dagon, the worshippers' Pneuma is not being skimmed upward into a Nephil's accumulated pool. Something *else* is happening — something the Concord's finest Hermeticists and Theurgists cannot model cleanly. The best current theory, advanced privately by the shade of Kartīr and disputed by the Inner Seal, is that Dagon is a **liminal entity**: neither a Watcher, nor a Nephil, nor an Outer Power in the mold of Cthulhu or Nyarlathotep, but something akin to an avatar or *emanation* of a much deeper principle — the interface between the mortal Sphere (*Kosmos*) and whatever lies in the lightless ocean trenches that are neither truly mortal-world nor truly Outer Dark. In this reading, Dagon does not consume worship so much as *anchor* it: the Order's rites keep him present, present keeps the connection between his people and the Profundi (*Deep Ones*) intact, and that connection is the whole point.

What the Order itself believes — the theology it teaches in its innermost rites — is simpler and stranger: Dagon was in the sea before the Watchers descended on Hermon. He was in the sea before the Nephilim built their first altars. He will be in the sea long after the last Nephil's cult-pool has drained dry. The Great Reckoning, when his master rises from the sunken city in the southern oceans (*see* §3, "The Sleeper Beneath / Cthulhu"), will simply be the moment the surface world acknowledges what was always true: that the deep sea is older than the dry land, and that those who accepted Dagon's covenant have bet on the winning side.

This eschatology frightens the Inner Seal more than almost any other single piece of intelligence in its archive, because it is the one future in which the Order requires no active apocalyptic effort. It simply has to survive long enough.

## DEEP HISTORY: THE COVENANT OF THE COAST

### The Phoenician Cradle

Long before there was a Phoenicia, before the purple-merchants of Tyre and Byblos and Sidon built their famous trading empire, the people of the Lebanese coast had a relationship with the creatures in the deep water off their shore. The Concord's archive assigns the first formalized contact to approximately **3000 BC**, during the period of Phoenician urban settlement along the coast between what would become Sidon and Tyre. Fishermen working deep-water lines off the headland at Sidon began returning with more fish than their nets could explain, and with accounts — dismissed initially as sea-talk, the ordinary superstitions of salt-cured men — of pale, wide-eyed figures glimpsed underwater at depth, watching. Then the figures came ashore.

What followed was not conquest or catastrophe. The **Profundi** (*Deep Ones*) — the amphibian people of the deep trenches, barrel-chested and smooth-skinned below the waist, gill-slitted at the throat, eyes that catch lamplight like a cat's — did not descend on the coast in violence. They arrived at low tide and waited to be acknowledged. The oldest stratum of Phoenician oral tradition, preserved in the Concord's transcriptions of documents the Order does not know the Concord possesses, describes this first meeting in terms that the Concord's scholars find oddly mundane: *they wanted to trade*. They offered navigational knowledge — the locations of safe deep-water passages, the timing of currents and tides, the positions of underwater hazards along the entire Levantine coast — in exchange for something the mortal Phoenicians had and the Profundi wanted: **a persistent presence above the waterline**. The Profundi can survive on land for limited periods, but they cannot thrive there. They cannot build there. They cannot engage with mortal civilization except through intermediaries.

The agreement that emerged is what the Order calls the **Covenant of the First Tide** (*Foedus Primae Aestus*): mortal Phoenician families would accept Deep One partners among their kin, the resulting offspring would carry both bloodlines, and the Order founded to manage this covenant would provide the Profundi with a land-capable face for all the business they needed done on the surface world. In exchange, those families and their descendants would prosper by every measure the sea could provide, and would — in time, when the change came upon them fully — join the Profundi in the deep water as functional immortals. No more death from illness or age. An existence in the cold dark that the Order's theology describes, with what an outsider can only read as genuine conviction, as *better*.

This is the core bargain. It has not changed in three thousand years.

### The Mortal Face: Dagon and the Philistine Connection

The entity the Order worships as **Father Dagon** is known to mortal historians as the grain-and-sea god of the Philistines and Canaanites — a deity with temples at Ashdod and Gaza (the [historical Dagon of the Hebrew scriptures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dagon)), whose idol the captured Ark of the Covenant supposedly toppled. These traditions are not wrong, merely incomplete. The Philistines who arrived on the Levantine coast in the twelfth century BC during the broader [Sea Peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_Peoples) migrations encountered the Sidonian Order at the height of its Bronze Age power. Some Philistine communities *also* had their own agreements with local Profundi enclaves — the offshore trenches are not only off Sidon — and the resulting theological cross-pollination produced the Dagon-worshipping cult that appears in Israelite records. In Philistine hands Dagon became more grain-connected, more conventionally agrarian; this is what happens when a maritime covenant-cult gets adopted by a primarily land-farming population.

The Order at Sidon regards the Philistine-derived Dagon cults with a mixture of patronizing affection and mild exasperation. They were family, in a distant way. By the time those cults were suppressed by the expanding Israelite states in the ninth century BC, the Sidonian Order had already been running for two millennia and was not troubled.

### The Atlantic Crossing and the Fomorian Disaster (c. 2000–1100 BC)

The height of the Order's ambition was the Atlantic expansion. In the centuries following the Sumerian Breach near Eridu (*see* §3.4.C), during which the Court of Long Night closed a portal to the Outer Dark at great cost, the Profundi of the Levantine trenches had a different kind of crisis: the population of the underwater cities was growing, and the Levantine coast could not support indefinite expansion. The Profundi needed new coastal territory, and the Order — by then a sophisticated institution with considerable maritime expertise — was tasked with finding it.

The surveys took generations. The Order sent hybrid navigators along the Libyan coast, into the western Mediterranean, through the Pillars of Hercules. Eventually the reports came back: there was an island group far to the north and west, in the cold Atlantic, where the sea-floor offered what the Profundi needed. The coast of western Ireland.

Between approximately **2000 and 1500 BC**, the Order established what its records call the **Atlantic Chapter** (*Caput Atlanticum*), planting colonies along the western Irish coast at the Cliffs of Moher, Innis Mor, and Tory Island. The Profundi built deep-water cities in the Atlantic trenches off those shores. The hybrid population that resulted — the land-side face of this Atlantic covenant — are what Irish tradition remembers as the **[Fomorians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fomorians)**: the under-wave people, the warlike sea-giants, the beings who dwelt beneath the worlds of men.

The Fomori were, in fact, a Deep-One colonial polity: amphibious hybrids who built their above-ground holdings on the Atlantic shore and their real civilization underwater. For four or five centuries they prospered. They extracted tribute from Bronze Age Ireland — in grain, in cattle, in human beings taken as breeding stock to broaden the hybrid bloodlines. By the standards of the Levantine Order, this was not unusually aggressive; the Sidonian chapter's relationship with coastal Phoenician towns operated on similar economic terms, if more subtly administered.

The problem was the **Court of the Burning Crown**.

The Burning Crown — the senior Twilight Court in the western isles at this period — had its own interests in Ireland, operating through the mortal cultures under the guise of the Irish divine races that later myth would call the Tuatha Dé Danann (who are in fact the Aes Sídhe of the Burning Crown, remembered through mortal memory: *see* §5.3.B). The Fomorian extraction of tribute and breeding-stock from the same mortal population the Burning Crown used as its devotional base created a conflict that neither side was able or willing to avoid. The long war between the Fomorians and the Burning Crown's Tuatha host — recorded in Irish oral tradition as the two Battles of Mag Tuired — was real, cold-and-hot, and ended decisively in approximately **1100 BC** at the **Second Battle of Mag Tuired**, when the Burning Crown's assembled host shattered the Fomorian land-army.

The Court of the Burning Crown is the **only Mythos-aligned faction** the Sidhe have ever defeated in open pitched battle. The eldest of them — including the being called the **Eldest Gruff** — fought in that war. The blood-grudge has not cooled. The Burning Crown will *always* answer a call to fight Deep Ones, regardless of Concordat politics.

The Atlantic Chapter never recovered. The Profundi of the Irish trenches withdrew to depth. The hybrid population on Innis Mor and the Aran Islands survived as a depleted, frightened rump, hiding their nature, intermarrying with the local Irish population to dilute the visible signs of the change. By 500 AD, the Innis Mor rump has persisted for fifteen hundred years in reduced circumstances. It is, very recently, **beginning to rebuild**.

The chapter in Sidon stayed strong because the Burning Crown could not project force into the Mediterranean. The Levantine Order learned from the Atlantic catastrophe: never again would it establish a colonial chapter without a secured route of retreat to deep water, and never again would it antagonize a Twilight Court on that Court's own territorial ground.

### The 800 BC Concordat Interlude

When the **Concordat of Hidden Powers** was first drafted in 287 BC (*see* §4), the Order was not among the original signatories. The Sidonian chapter had, forty-seven years earlier in **800 BC**, signed a precursor agreement — a bilateral treaty with the Court of Long Night only, negotiated through a Sidon-based intermediary — that governed certain territorial questions about coastal mortal populations and the Order's right to recruit among them. That treaty was what the Order's representatives brought to the 287 BC process as their credential.

The Order signed the Concordat once, in 800 BC as its charter-date is reckoned within the later treaty. The treaty subsequently **lapsed** and was **never renewed**, for reasons the Concord's archive records with some bitterness: the Order's representative at the renewal negotiations in 190 BC, a senior hybrid named **Abibaal the Crowned**, attended the first three sessions, produced a set of counter-proposals the Concord found wholly unacceptable (they amounted to formal recognition of the Order's right to take breeding-stock from any coastal community within a defined radius of a chapter-house), and then walked into the harbor at Sidon and did not resurface. The Concord spent eighteen months trying to find a replacement representative empowered to negotiate. None came.

The Concord has since filed the Order under **known non-signatories**, alongside the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh, though it does not conflate the two: the Brotherhood is an active threat operating to open the Outer Dark; the Order is a persistent inconvenience operating to perpetuate itself. The Outside-Threat Clause of the Concordat does not technically apply to the Order. The Concord is uncomfortable about this interpretation and reviews it approximately once per generation.

### The Drowned Library and the Underwater Cities

Below the sea-floor off the Lebanese coast, at a depth mortal divers have never reached and modern maritime technology will not reach for many centuries, sits what the Concord's Key Location file calls the **Drowned Library** (*Bibliotheca Submersa*): a pre-Phoenician underwater complex of considerable scale, currently in active use by the Order's Profundi. It contains tablets whose language predates Sumerian, whose contents the Concord knows by indirect report only, and which the one Concord scholar who successfully translated three of them described as *not safe to read* before requesting indefinite leave and relocating to a mountain hermitage in Cappadocia.

The Library is not the only underwater complex. The Profundi maintain what the Order calls the **Three Halls**: the Drowned Library off Sidon (the oldest and largest), the **Hall of the Gulf** in the Persian Gulf enclave off the Arabian coast, and the **Sunken Rath** in the deep Atlantic off Innis Mor — the oldest surviving structure of the Atlantic Chapter, which was never destroyed but merely vacated, and which the Innis Mor rump has begun, very carefully, to revisit.

These underwater complexes are the true capitals of the Order. The land-side chapter-houses are offices. The Drowned Library is where the Crowned of the Tides — the fully-transformed elders — reside, govern, and archive.

## THE NATURE OF THE DEEP ONES AND THE TRANSFORMATION

### The *Profundi*: What They Are

The **Profundi** (*Deep Ones*, singular *Profundus*) are an amphibious people of uncertain origin. The Concord's best taxonomical assessment, developed over two centuries of observation and one harrowing direct consultation with a Crowned of the Tides in 388 AD, concludes that they are not a creation of the Outer Ones, not Watcher-derived, not Nephilim, and not a mortal race that has undergone transformation. They appear to have been in the deep ocean for as long as the ocean has been deep. They are **not** the servitors of Cthulhu, though the Order's eschatology aligns with a future in which the Sleeper rises. The relationship is better understood as parallel allegiance to the same ancient pattern, not command and obedience.

The Profundi can survive on land for perhaps three days without returning to salt water. In water they are fast, powerful, and essentially unkillable by mortal means — their tissues regenerate in ways that make a deep wound a temporary inconvenience. They are not immune to fire or iron, though both work less effectively than against mortal flesh. They do not age in any observable sense. They have been observed returning to the same underwater locations across periods of centuries.

They speak a version of the tongue the Order calls the **Tongue of the First Tide** (*Lingua Primae Aestus*), which is related to, but not quite the same as, what the Concord designates Black Speech. Scholars who have heard it uniformly describe it as *deeply wrong to the inner ear*, though they struggle to explain why. The few Order hybrids who can speak both human languages and the Profundi tongue describe the experience of code-switching between them as moving between fundamentally different ways of perceiving time.

Their society is hierarchical, patient, and almost entirely non-transparent to human observation. The Order's land-side leadership reports to the Profundi elders; what those elders report to, or whether the hierarchy extends upward past the Drowned Library, is not known outside the Order's innermost rites.

### The Change: Transfiguration and Immortality

The central theological and physical fact of membership in the Order is what the Order calls, with apparent sincerity, the **Blessed Change** (*Mutatio Beata*) — the gradual transformation of Deep One hybrids from superficially human to fully Profundi as they age.

It begins subtly and accelerates. The following is the progression as the Order's own rites describe it:

**Youth and early adulthood:** The hybrid child is largely indistinguishable from a fully human child, except for slightly wider-set eyes, an unusual pallor, and — in some cases — a tendency to hold their breath for longer than peers find comfortable. They are often unusually good swimmers from very early age. They process salt water without the distress human bodies experience. Some report a persistent sense of the sea's direction even inland.

**Middle years (approximately 25–40):** The first visible changes begin: skin becomes slightly waxy and more resistant to cold; eyes widen perceptibly and develop a characteristic nacreous quality in certain light; the throat may develop low ridges at the sides — not functional gills yet, but the structural precursor. Joints begin to show subtle alteration, particularly in the hands and feet. The hybrid begins to find long periods away from salt water uncomfortable, then distressing.

**The Threshold Years (40–70):** The Blessed Change accelerates. Skin loses its warm tone and becomes smooth, cool, and faintly iridescent. Eyes grow markedly, irises often greening or yellowing. Functional gill-slits open at the throat. Fingers and toes lengthen and develop visible webbing. The hybrid's voice changes — it remains comprehensible in human tongues, but carries a resonance that makes animals uneasy and mortal people feel, without being able to say why, that they should be somewhere else. The hybrid finds mortal food increasingly unpalatable; fresh fish and salt water become dietary necessities.

**Full Transformation:** The completion of the Change varies by individual and by lineage — some families are slower, some are faster. When it comes, it comes quickly: a final passage of weeks in which the hybrid's body completes its reorientation. At this point they are Profundi in all physical respects. They walk into the sea and they do not come back. They do not die. They become one of the **Crowned of the Tides** — the fully-transformed elders who govern the Order from beneath the waves and who are, barring violence, essentially immortal.

The Order's theology frames this as the point of the entire covenant: not salvation in the Christian sense, not reincarnation, not an afterlife of any kind, but *physical continuation in a form that was always the more durable one*. The sea has been here longer than the dry land. The Profundi will still be here when every Nephil has starved. The Change is not a loss of humanity; it is, in Order doctrine, a *graduation* from it.

The psychological experience of approaching the Threshold is, by all accounts including sympathetic ones, significant. Hybrids entering the Threshold Years typically become quieter, more deliberate, less socially bonded to mortal relationships. They know what is coming. They have watched their parents and their elders' parents undergo the same passage. The Order provides extensive ritual support for this period — the rites of the **Deep Vigil** (*Vigilia Profunda*), conducted at the chapter-house's tidal caves at each new moon, are specifically designed to ease the transition and celebrate rather than mourn it.

What they leave behind, in terms of mortal attachments and human-world relationships, is — for mortal observers, including investigators who may encounter an Order chapter in its daily operations — usually the most human-readable sign that something is unusual about this community. The old fisherwoman who sold her boat last season and whose house smells of salt. The harbor-master who never attends the Saturday market anymore. The family that is *always* at the wharf, regardless of weather, well before dawn.

## THE ORDER IN THE WIDER WORLD

### Relations with Mortal Coastal Society

The Order is **integrated** into its host communities to a degree that most Mythos-adjacent factions cannot approach. It does not sit beside mortal society; it is mortal society, at the harbor end of town. Chapter houses operate as going commercial concerns — fisheries, dye-works, ship-chandleries, navigation schools. The Order's hybrids are among the best navigators on the Mediterranean because they have access to Profundi knowledge of currents, depths, and hazards that no mortal mariner can match. A chapter's economic output is genuinely valuable to the surrounding town, and the town knows this, and prefers not to ask questions.

Communities that **accept** an Order chapter prosper. Their fish-catches run high. Their ships suffer fewer losses to storm and reef. Their trade contacts extend further, because the Order's network of chapters — Sidon to the Persian Gulf to the Adriatic — represents a courier and intelligence service of considerable range. Outbreaks of coastal illness are quietly suppressed; the Order does not explain how. Children of families in the Order's standing arrangement are healthy, tend to grow large, and often go on to become valuable people in maritime trades.

Communities that **resist** or, worse, *investigate* the Order's deeper nature tend to experience a different sequence of events. Catches fall. Storms worsen. Ships find unexpected shallows. People disappear from the night streets near the water. This is not, the Order's leadership is at pains to explain to anyone within earshot, *punishment*. It is simply what happens when the sea's favor is withdrawn. The distinction, a Concord investigator once remarked in a report to the Castrum, is difficult to observe from the outside.

### Relations with Other Hidden Powers

The Order is a **non-signatory of the Concordat**. It was briefly a treaty partner in 800 BC and has not been since (*see* §4). The Concord files it as a *persistent coastal phenomenon* rather than an active threat, a designation several senior Magisters have argued strenuously should be revised.

**The Concord of the Seven Seals** watches the Order with careful distance. The Order's activities do not, as a rule, threaten the Seven Seals themselves — the Covenant of the First Tide does not require breaching any of the architectural prohibitions the Seals maintain — but the Order's relationship with a power structure the Concord cannot observe clearly, in the deep ocean, makes the Inner Seal uncomfortable. The Drowned Library's tablets are of enormous scholarly interest. Retrieving them is, the Concord has decided three separate times, not worth the cost of trying.

**The Court of the Burning Crown** maintains a permanent blood-grudge with the Order from the Second Battle of Mag Tuired (*see* §3.4.D). The Burning Crown will *always* answer a call to fight Deep Ones, and its eldest Sidhe — those who remember the Fomorian war — regard the Order's current rebuilding on Innis Mor with a fury they have not expressed this openly since 1100 BC. The Queen of the Burning Crown, **Aigle, Queen of Lions and Olives**, has notified the Concord that any failure to address the Innis Mor re-establishment will be treated as Concord complicity. This has created an uncomfortable diplomatic situation that the Concord has so far addressed by sending a mid-tier investigator named Eudokia of Antioch (*see* §5.6, and Adventure Hook 10.3) to assess the matter rather than committing to action.

**The Court of Long Night** holds no blood-grudge with the Order and has occasionally transacted business with Sidon through the oldest diplomatic channel available: the bilateral precursor to the Concordat signed in 800 BC. Morrigna considers the Order a *manageable nuisance*, which is the closest thing to approval that the Queen of the Slow Knife expresses toward anyone who is not a signatory.

**The Court of Jade Breath** (*see* §5.2.D), based in the Indian Ocean, has ongoing dealings with the Order's Persian Gulf enclave along trade routes that neither party discusses publicly. The nature of those dealings is not fully known to the Concord.

**The Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh** has attempted to recruit the Order as an ally on two documented occasions, both times offering intelligence about Concord operations in the Levant. On both occasions the Order's response was to sink the messenger's ship. The Order does not want what the Brotherhood is selling — the Brotherhood's apocalypticism would require the surface world to end in a way that conflicts with the Order's own eschatological timetable. The Great Reckoning, in Order theology, is an *arrival*, not a *destruction*.

## REGIONAL CHAPTERS

### Sidon: The Mother Temple

Sidon (*Colonia Aurelia Pia Sidon*, to give it its Roman administrative title) is the oldest and most powerful surviving chapter of the Order. It has operated continuously, with varying degrees of concealment, for approximately three thousand years. The chapter-house sits on the southern headland of the harbor, operating publicly as a **navigation school and ship-chandlery** of excellent reputation. The school genuinely functions — Sidonian navigators trained at the house command a premium on any Mediterranean voyage — and the income finances the chapter's deeper operations.

Below the headland, accessible from the chapter-house cellar through a passage that requires three tide-timed locks to open safely, the Sidon chapter maintains the **Hall of the Tidal Crown** (*Aula Coronae Aestuariae*): a cave-complex extending beneath the sea-floor, partially above water at high tide and partially submerged at low, where the Order's most significant rituals are conducted and where the eldest Crowned of the Land convene for formal business. Below this hall, at a depth reachable only by swimming through a submerged passage no mortal could attempt, the access-tunnel to the Drowned Library begins.

The Sidon chapter is currently the largest in the world, with a membership it does not discuss openly but which Concord estimates put at one hundred forty to one hundred seventy hybrids at various stages of the Change, plus two to three hundred mortal **Lay Brothers and Sisters** serving in support roles. The Crowned of the Tides resident in the Drowned Library are an unknown quantity.

Sidon is, simultaneously, a functioning commercial hub of the Eastern Roman administration, a chapter of the Order, and — to anyone who has lived there long enough and paid attention — a place where the harbor-master's hands are slightly too long for his sleeves, where the dye-workers on the night shift never seem cold even in January, and where the annual festival of the Blessed Tides draws every family in the harbor district to the waterfront for a celebration that visitors find picturesque and residents find *correct* in a way that feels neither festive nor ominous, but simply inevitable.

### Innis Mor: The Rump Rebuilding

The island the Order calls **An Inis Mór** and the local Irish call **The Great Island** is a limestone flatland three miles off the Galway coast, battered by Atlantic swells, populated by subsistence farmers and fishers who have been there long enough that they have stopped wondering about the tidal caves on the western shore that their grandmothers told them never to enter. The caves were the access-tunnels to the Sunken Rath, the Atlantic Chapter's Profundi infrastructure, vacated in the catastrophe of 1100 BC and left in place.

For fifteen centuries the Innis Mor hybrid population declined generation by generation. The visible change became rarer; the bloodlines diluted; the knowledge of what they were passed as family rumor rather than formal teaching. By 400 AD the island held perhaps thirty families with documented hybrid ancestry, of whom perhaps a third retained enough of the bloodline to show any physical signs, and almost none retained formal knowledge of the Order's rites. The Sunken Rath sat abandoned in the cold Atlantic dark.

Then the **Sidonian emissaries came**.

In approximately **480 AD**, a Sidonian ship — registering as a trading vessel out of Carthage Nova — put in at the island for water and stayed. Its captain, a Crowned of the Land named **Bustan of Sidon** (*Bustān*, "the garden"), had been dispatched by the senior Sidonian hierarchy with a mandate to assess whether the Atlantic Chapter could be restored. His assessment, delivered back to Sidon in **482 AD**, was cautious: *the bloodlines persist, the Rath is intact, the locals have been isolated from the Burning Crown's direct attention for generations, the conditions are as favorable as they are going to be*. The decision to proceed was made. Bustan remained on the island. He is still there.

As of **500 AD**, the Innis Mor chapter is in an early but functional state of reconstruction. Bustan has identified and contacted twenty-six hybrid-descended families. Eleven of those families have been fully re-integrated into Order discipline — they know what they are, they know what is coming for them physically, and they have accepted it. The remaining fifteen are at various stages of approach and persuasion. The Sunken Rath's access-tunnel has been cleared and re-sealed with new Profundi-set locks. Three young Profundi from the Sidonian trench have relocated to the Atlantic site. The above-ground chapter-house — a converted stone farmhouse on the island's windward coast — operates as a fishing cooperative, which is what it also genuinely is.

The Burning Crown has noticed. The adventure hook the Concord calls *Eudokia's Dreams* (*see* §10.3) — in which the Yithian Recorder dreams of Innis Mor before the confrontation there has fully materialized — is a direct consequence of the chapter's renewed activity.

What the Innis Mor chapter **does not yet have** is a Crowned of the Tides. Bustan is Crowned of the Land; he is deep into his Threshold Years and will complete the Change within a decade. He is trying, with increasing urgency, to establish the chapter on a sound enough footing that his transformation does not orphan it.

### The Persian Gulf Enclave

The **Hall of the Gulf** (*Aula Sinus Persici*) is the Order's quietest and most insular chapter. It occupies a section of the Arabian coastal shallows east of Bahrain, in an area where the combined effects of Order-managed local reputation and genuine navigational hazard have kept outside intrusion low for approximately four centuries. It does not operate a land-side commercial front in the way the Sidon chapter does; instead it has cultivated relationships with two or three local Arab and Persian trading families who handle the chapter's surface-world transactions without asking questions about their patrons' nature.

The Gulf enclave is listed in the World's edge notes (*see* §2) as *a Deep One enclave in the Persian Gulf*. It is small — Concord estimates suggest no more than forty to sixty active hybrids, with a proportionately small Lay membership — but it sits astride the trade routes between the Persian Gulf and the Indian Ocean, which is why the Court of Jade Breath (*see* §5.2.D) maintains intermittent contact with it. This relationship with Jade Breath is the most politically complex element of the Gulf chapter's position; neither the Concord nor the Order's Sidonian leadership knows exactly what is being traded.

The Gulf enclave was established approximately **600 BC**, during a period of Phoenician commercial expansion into Arabian waters. It predates the 800 BC Concordat-precursor, which means it was operating before the Order had any treaty relationship with any other hidden power, and it has retained an independence from the Sidonian central chapter that is unusual in the Order's governance structure. The **Crowned of the Tides** resident in the Hall of the Gulf conduct their affairs largely without reference to Sidon.

### Scattered Presences: The Adriatic and the Aegean

The Atlantic catastrophe was not the Order's only contraction. The Bronze Age Collapse (*c.* 1200 BC), which disrupted trade networks across the entire eastern Mediterranean, damaged several smaller Order chapters that had established themselves on Aegean islands and on the Adriatic coast of what is now the Eastern Roman administrative province of Illyricum. Most of those chapters did not survive. A few left behind hybrid bloodlines in coastal communities that have been slowly diluting ever since, producing occasional isolated individuals with the characteristic wide eyes and unusual pallor who have no connection to the Order's formal structure and often no knowledge of what they are.

In **Crete**, there is a cluster of hybrid-descended fishing families in the villages west of Heraklion who are aware, in a vague generational way, that their family's history is *different*. The Sidon chapter has occasionally sent Crowned of the Land to assess these Cretan remnants. Thus far the assessment has been: the bloodline is too thin to formally reconstitute, and the Cretan political situation (the Eastern Roman administration maintains a naval presence on the island) makes chapter-formation inadvisable. This may change.

In **the Adriatic**, near the depopulated zone of the Dalmatian coast where the Order is mentioned in the bible overview, a single Crowned of the Land — **Mirna of the Still Water**, a woman who completed her Threshold crossing in approximately 350 AD and has lived alone in a cave on a small offshore island for a century and a half — represents the Order's entire Adriatic presence. She fishes, she observes, and she occasionally redirects a passing trader's course with hand-signs the sailors cannot quite remember afterward. What she is waiting for, she has never told the Sidon chapter, though they ask.

## ROLES OF THE FAITHFUL

The Order's internal structure is unlike the fluid, merit-and-violence-based hierarchy of the Cult of Mormo. It is **hereditary**, **biological**, and in large part **predetermined**: your rank within the Order is a function of how far along the Change you are, which is a function of your bloodline, your age, and factors of individual physiology that the Order's rites can influence but not fully control.

### The Crowned of the Tides (*Coronati Maris*)

The fully-transformed elders, those who have completed the Blessed Change. They live entirely in the sea now, returning to the land-side chapter-houses only for the highest rites — the annual Festival of the Blessed Tides, the Threshold Vigil for a hybrid approaching completion, and any crisis that requires their direct authority. They are functionally immortal, possessed of Profundi strength and regeneration, and communicate with the land-side membership in the Tongue of the First Tide, which the Crowned of the Land translate.

Their governance function is advisory and absolute simultaneously: they do not administer day-to-day chapter operations, but any decision the land-side leadership makes that conflicts with the Tides' expressed will is reversed, immediately and without discussion. They are the elders in the original sense of the word.

In physical appearance they are entirely Profundi: tall, smooth-skinned, wide-eyed, gill-slitted, long-fingered. They wear the **Crown of Tides** (*Corona Aestuum*): a circlet of deep-ocean shell-nacre and shaped whale-ivory that has no decorative purpose and every symbolic one. When a Crowned of the Tides surfaces to attend a rite, the land-side membership do not look at them directly. This is not a rule; it is simply what happens.

### The Crowned of the Land (*Coronati Terrae*)

The senior hybrids who still pass — with effort, with careful attention to dress and timing and light — as human. These are the chapter's actual administrators: they manage the commercial operations, conduct the regular rites, instruct the younger membership, and serve as the Order's face in any dealings with mortal authorities or (rarely) other hidden powers.

The **Crowned of the Land** bear the most visually obvious signs of the approaching Change. They are typically in their Threshold Years or approaching them. They handle this public exposure through a combination of strategic social withdrawal ("the harbor-master prefers to work at night, always has"), careful costuming (high collars, long sleeves, hats with brims), and the Order's own rite of **Veiling** (*Velatio*): a minor working in the Hieratica tradition, patron-loaned from Father Dagon himself, that makes observers disinclined to look closely. The Velatio is not invisibility; it is closer to a persistent social encouragement to not quite focus.

They bear a distinctive mark of their rank: a **nacreous seal**, a disc of deep-water shell set in silver, worn at the throat. This is not decorative. It functions as a credential within the Order and as a ward-anchor for the Velatio working.

### The Lay Brothers and Sisters (*Fratres et Sorores Laici*)

Mortal humans — ordinary Sidonians, Irishmen, Arab traders, Greek dockworkers — who serve the Order out of belief, fear, economic relationship, or long-standing family tradition. They attend the Order's public rites, provide labor for the chapter-houses' commercial operations, serve as couriers and lookouts and informants within the wider coastal community, and receive in exchange the Order's documented material favor: good catches, prosperity, protection, healing assistance that they understand no better than the Order wishes them to.

The majority of the Lay Brothers and Sisters are not fully initiated into the Order's deeper cosmology. They know the broad outlines — the covenant, the transformation, the immortality of the Change — and they have opinions about it ranging from straightforward belief and longing to quiet resigned acceptance. Full initiation, which gives access to the rites of the Deep Vigil and the right to attend the Festival of the Blessed Tides in the inner cave-halls, is offered to Lay members who have demonstrated several years of trustworthy service and shown that they understand what they are accepting.

What they are accepting is this: any child born to a fully-initiated Lay member who subsequently marries into a hybrid family may, several generations down, begin to show the Change. Once accepted, the covenant extends forward in time indefinitely. The Order does not recruit indiscriminately. It accumulates.

### The Harborers (*Stationarii*)

A specialized class within the Lay Brotherhood: those who manage the Order's legitimate commercial operations. Harbormasters, navigation-school instructors, dye-works supervisors, ship's chandlers. The Harborers are not necessarily of hybrid blood themselves — many are Lay members of full mortal lineage — but they are the Order's public interface with the mortal economy and must be able to move freely in mortal society without raising questions.

They are well-compensated, carefully selected, and thoroughly committed. An outside investigator watching a Harborer closely will find, usually, an efficient and prosperous small merchant who is perhaps slightly *too* incurious about some of their employers' activities, and who keeps unusual hours near the waterfront.

### The Watchers of the Shore (*Vigiles Litoris*)

The Order's internal security. Every chapter of substantial size maintains a cadre of Vigiles drawn from the most physically capable hybrids or Lay members. Their role is partly the obvious one — perimeter watch on chapter-house approaches, escort for senior Crowned of the Land during nighttime movement — and partly something more subtle: they are the first point of contact when someone asks questions they shouldn't, and their preferred tool of response is misdirection and discouragement rather than violence.

Violence is a last resort for the Order, not because it is philosophically opposed to it but because dead bodies produce investigations and investigations produce attention and attention is precisely what the Order has spent three thousand years avoiding. A Vigile's ideal outcome is that the person asking the wrong questions wakes up the next morning with no clear memory of why they were at the harbor so late, and a strong sense that they should look for work elsewhere.

In situations where the Order's security is genuinely threatened — an active Concord investigation, a Burning Crown incursion, a mortal authority with enough stubborn curiosity to overcome the Velatio — the Vigiles can be supplemented by active intervention from the Profundi themselves, who can approach from the water side of any coastline and whose physical capabilities in or near the sea make them extraordinarily effective at enforcement.

## COMPOSITION OF A TYPICAL ORDER CHAPTER

Order chapters vary in size from the Sidon mother-temple's hundred-plus hybrids down to isolated remnant-cells of a few families. The following represents a **mid-size established chapter** functioning in a coastal town of some commercial importance.

A chapter at this scale will have been in place for at least two or three generations, long enough to have embedded deeply in local economic and social life. Its chapter-house passes publicly as a legitimate business. Its membership knows what it is and has accepted what is coming for them. The local mortal population has reached a comfortable equilibrium of *not asking*.

### HIERARCHY

##### THE CROWNED OF THE TIDES (1–3, USUALLY UNSEEN)
FULLY TRANSFORMED ELDERS; RESIDE IN THE DEEP WATER NEARBY; ATTEND ONLY HIGH RITES AND CRISES; ABSOLUTE AUTHORITY WHEN PRESENT

##### CROWNED OF THE LAND — SENIOR VOICE (1)
THE CHAPTER ADMINISTRATOR; THE FACE OF THE ORDER IN ALL DEALINGS; DEEP IN THE THRESHOLD YEARS OR JUST PAST THEM; MASTER OF THE VELATIO WORKING

##### CROWNED OF THE LAND — WARDEN AND KEEPER (2–4)
MID-TIER LEADERSHIP; MANAGE SPECIFIC CHAPTER FUNCTIONS (RITES, COMMERCE, SECURITY, EXTERNAL CONTACTS); EARLIER THRESHOLD YEARS; STILL MOSTLY PASSABLE AS HUMAN WITH EFFORT

##### HARBORERS (4–10)
MANAGERS OF THE COMMERCIAL FRONT; MIX OF HYBRID AND MORTAL LINEAGE; THE CHAPTER'S PUBLIC ECONOMIC PRESENCE

##### VIGILES LITORIS (6–15)
SECURITY CADRE; DRAWN FROM PHYSICALLY CAPABLE HYBRIDS AND COMMITTED LAY MEMBERS; PRESENT AT THE CHAPTER-HOUSE AROUND THE CLOCK IN SHIFTS

##### INITIATED LAY BROTHERS AND SISTERS (30–80)
FULLY INITIATED MORTAL AND LOW-HYBRID MEMBERS; ATTEND ALL RITES; PROVIDE LABOR AND LOCAL INTELLIGENCE; LONG-TERM EMBEDDED IN THE COMMUNITY

##### UNINITIATED LAY MEMBERS (50–150)
ECONOMICALLY CONNECTED LOCALS WHO BENEFIT FROM THE CHAPTER'S COMMERCIAL NETWORK WITHOUT FULL INITIATION; LOYAL BUT NOT FULLY INFORMED

### OPTIONAL ASSETS

The most powerful asset any chapter can call on is the direct intervention of the **Crowned of the Tides**, but this is rarely done — it is disruptive, it is visible, and the elders are not easily summoned from the deep. A chapter that requires Tidal intervention has usually made a serious error.

More routinely, a Crowned of the Land in good standing can work the Hieratica tradition as a patron-loaned practitioner of Father Dagon's gift: the **Velatio** (as described), the **Tide-Speaking** rite (calling specific weather conditions, within limits), and the **Dreaming Current** (the ability to deliver a message or compulsion through a target's dreams by submerging and focusing through the salt water). These are not combat powers; they are the tools of a patient institution that prefers not to fight.

In genuine extremity, a chapter can request that Profundi from the nearest underwater complex take direct action. This is the Order's version of calling in the heavy assets: three or four Profundi working from the water side of any coastal engagement are a formidable force against almost any mortal opposition, and a significant challenge even for Concord-trained theurges who have not prepared specifically for amphibious opposition.

## SAMPLE CHAPTERS

### THE SIDON MOTHER-TEMPLE

The oldest and largest chapter of the Order, operating from the city of Sidon on the Lebanese coast. The following represents its current state in **500 AD** at campaign start.

#### Headquarters

The chapter-house occupies the **House of the Navigators** (*Domus Naviticorum*) on the southern headland of Sidon harbor: a complex of three connected buildings that function as a navigation school (ground floor), a ship-chandlery and nautical instrument workshop (second building), and what appears to be a prosperous merchant family's residence (third building). The residence belongs to the chapter's current Senior Voice. The three-tiered lock passage to the Hall of the Tidal Crown is accessed from the chandlery's locked cellar. An outside observer will note that the chandlery's cellar is *much* colder than it should be for June, and that the sound of water is audible from below even when the harbor outside is calm.

The Navigation School's public reputation is genuine and well-earned. Its students pay real fees and receive real instruction. Several generations of Sidonian harbor pilots, currently regarded as the best in the Eastern Mediterranean, learned to navigate here.

#### Leadership

**Senior Voice: Tamrit the Lantern-Keeper** (*Tamrît*, "date-palm") — A Crowned of the Land approaching the final stages of the Threshold. She is approximately sixty years old by the reckoning of anyone who has known her over the past decade, but looks, in careful lamplight and with the Velatio running, perhaps forty-five. Her eyes are the most obvious giveaway: enormously wide-set and palely iridescent in any direct light, though she has worn a scholar's veil-hat for years and this is accepted by Sidon as a personal eccentricity. She manages the chapter with the unhurried authority of someone who has already made her peace with what she is becoming. Her role as administrator will need to be handed off within the decade, and she is actively identifying her successor. She is among the most politically sophisticated individuals in the chapter and has maintained careful non-confrontational relationships with the Eastern Roman provincial administration through her official role as principal of the Navigation School.

**Warden of the Rites: Hanno the Slow** (*Hanno Tardus*) — The chapter's master of the ritual calendar, responsible for the annual Festival of the Blessed Tides, the monthly Deep Vigils, and the Threshold Vigils for hybrids approaching their Change. Hanno is fifty-four, early Threshold stage, and his primary visible sign is the persistent humidity of his skin regardless of season, which he explains as a skin condition and which nobody in Sidon believes but nobody in Sidon mentions. He is a slow, careful, methodical man with an extraordinary memory for rite-text and an encyclopedic knowledge of the Order's three-thousand-year liturgical archive. He is completely uninterested in politics.

**Keeper of the School: Arsinoe of the Harbor** (*Arsinoë Limenitis*, an adopted Greek name) — The Navigation School's public director and its most visible representative in Sidonian civic life. She is thirty-eight, pre-Threshold, passing entirely as human. She is the chapter's best Harborer: charming, professionally competent, comfortable at civic dinners, well-connected with the local Eastern Roman administration and the merchant families of the harbor district. She knows what she is and has no reservations about any of it, but she is also the most *strategically* aware that her chapter's continued operation depends on never giving the administration a sufficiently clear picture to act on. She is the first line of response if outside attention becomes acute.

**Head Vigile: Abubakr the Sailor** (*Abū Bakr al-Mallāḥ*) — An Arab trader of hybrid descent who came to Sidon from the Gulf enclave seven years ago and was assigned by the Crowned of the Tides to the Sidon chapter's security function. He is forty-six and in very early Threshold, barely showing. He is physically formidable, deeply experienced in maritime violence, and fundamentally pragmatic about the Order's interests. He does not use the Velatio or any other Hieratica working; he solves problems by knowing who is coming before they arrive, and by ensuring the chapter has information on every Concord-adjacent operative working in the Sidon harbor district.

#### Membership

**Crowned of the Land:** Four total, including Tamrit and Hanno; two additional Crowned manage the dye-works operation and the chapter's courier network.

**Crowned of the Tides resident in the Drowned Library:** Unknown, but the chapter operates as though three are directly accessible. Their names are not used above water.

**Harborers:** Eleven fully functioning commercial managers.

**Vigiles:** Fourteen, working in four-person shifts, with Abubakr commanding.

**Initiated Lay Brothers and Sisters:** Approximately ninety in the Sidon harbor community; several dozen more in Tyre and along the coast.

**Uninitiated Lay Members:** The Navigation School's extended network of students, former students, and trading contacts numbers in the hundreds; perhaps forty to sixty are meaningfully within the chapter's social orbit without formal initiation.

### THE INNIS MOR REBUILDING CHAPTER

The rump Atlantic chapter, currently in early reconstruction under Bustan of Sidon's management. The following represents its state in **500 AD** — specifically in the weeks immediately preceding the events of *Eudokia's Dreams* (Adventure Hook 10.3).

#### Headquarters

A converted stone farmhouse on the island's western shore, perhaps four hundred meters from the cliff-face access to the Sunken Rath tunnel. The farmhouse operates as the **Inis Mór Fishing Cooperative**, which it genuinely is: it processes, dries, and trades the island's catch through a small boat-crew of six Lay members. The farmhouse's rear storage building houses, under locked boards, the access-shaft to the tunnel passage. The tunnel is currently passable only by someone who can swim the lower submerged section, approximately forty meters in cold Atlantic water.

The farmhouse is also genuinely cold, permanently damp, and uncomfortable in the way that all stone buildings on the Atlantic face of Ireland are permanently cold, damp, and uncomfortable. The three Profundi from the Sidonian trench who have relocated to the Atlantic site live entirely below, in the Sunken Rath. They surface only after full dark, and only at the access cliff-face.

#### Leadership

**Bustan of Sidon** (*Bustān*, "the garden" — a Phoenician name that has confused every Irish person who has met him and been introduced, and which he has given up explaining) — The chapter's founder and sole Crowned of the Land. He is, by Sidonian records, approximately sixty-three years old; he looks seventy in the Atlantic light, which has not been kind to Threshold-stage skin. He is deep in the Change — his throat ridges are fully visible under his collar and his eyes require lamplight to appear approximately human — and he is aware that he has perhaps eight to twelve years before the completion of the transformation. He is driven, patient, methodical, and quietly frightened that he will run out of time before the chapter can stand without him.

He speaks Phoenician, Greek, Latin, and enough Gaelic to manage commercial transactions. He has never been comfortable in Ireland, which he finds cold and loud in ways he cannot explain to people who have never been to Sidon.

**Saoirse of the Tides** (*Saoirse*, "freedom" — a local name acquired on Innis Mor) — The chapter's most advanced hybrid from among the reconstituted local bloodlines. She is thirty-eight, the granddaughter of a woman who remembered the old stories, and she is the only member of the local lineage who sought out Bustan's contact rather than waiting to be approached. Early Threshold stage, most obvious sign a persistent webbing at the base of her fingers that she covers with heavy fishers' gloves. She has become Bustan's most capable assistant and his most likely successor as Warden of the Rites, if she can receive sufficient formal instruction before he transforms. She is the chapter's primary liaison with the three resident Profundi.

**Colmán of the Wide Eye** (*Colmán*, a common local name) — Head of the island's eight Vigiles, a wiry, wind-weathered man of forty-two who shows no hybrid blood but whose family has provided Vigile-function for the Order remnant on Innis Mor for four generations. He doesn't fully understand what he's protecting and has learned not to want to. He does his job, he trusts Bustan, and he sleeps badly on the nights when the Profundi come up from the Rath.

#### Membership

**Crowned of the Land:** Bustan only; Saoirse is approaching candidacy but not yet formally Crowned.

**Profundi in the Sunken Rath:** Three, sent from Sidon. Names not rendered in human language. They are young by Profundi reckoning — perhaps two hundred years old — and have been assigned here as a long-term posting.

**Hybrid-Descended Families in Active Contact:** Eleven families, representing approximately thirty-eight adults and their children.

**Initiated Lay Brothers and Sisters:** Twenty-two, drawn from the eleven families.

**Uninitiated Local Members:** Another sixty or seventy island residents who participate in the fishing cooperative's economic network without formal initiation.

**The Burning Crown's Awareness:** The Court of the Burning Crown knows this chapter is rebuilding. The island is close enough to the territory of certain Free Folk aligned with the Crown that word reached them within weeks of the Sunken Rath's first re-opening. The Crown has not yet acted directly. The Concord is aware of the Crown's awareness, and is the reason *Eudokia's Dreams* is an active investigation.

*The Recorder's dreams describe what will happen if no one intervenes. Whether those dreams are predictive or prescriptive is the question that makes this hook extraordinary.*
