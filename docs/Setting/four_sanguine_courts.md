## Overview

The **Hounds of Set** (*Canes Sethi*) are an Afro-Levantine vampire civilization, the oldest of the four Courts by recorded history and possibly the most ancient of all. They originate from a Watcher transformation enacted in the convulsion of the late Eighteenth Dynasty â€” the same era that produced Akhenaten's heretical monotheism â€” when the Watcher known as **Set** offered complete bodily transformation to three high priests of his own inner cult. The three survived the ritual, overpowered Set, and drained him completely: the first act of a species that has always defined itself by consuming its own divine source. The Concord's inner library designates the eldest of those three **Pheron-Kha-Seth**, a reconstruction the Hounds accept only grudgingly; they call him the **First Red**, and do not speak his name to outsiders.

In their **true form**, the Hounds are taller than mortals by a head, lean to the point of anatomical horror, with elongated dog-jackal skulls, a secondary jaw fitted inside the first, and bat-ribbed wings folded flat against the inside of their skin. When fully extended in battle, these wings do not carry them aloft â€” they are not flight-organs but threat-displays, the unfolding of which tends to end all negotiation. Their eyes in darkness carry a hot amber glow; in daylight, the oldest can suppress even this.

They wear **flesh-masks** â€” the Concord's term; the Hounds call them *personae*, in the Latin theatrical sense, though they adopted the word ironically. These are not disguise-spells but physiological masks: a slow reshaping of the outermost layer of their flesh into a plausible human face, which they can shift between known personas as a mortal changes clothes. The craft of a good *persona* is a social art among the Hounds: a poorly-fitted mask, one that slips under emotional stress or bright noon light, is as shameful as a lord appearing in public unwashed.

The very oldest, those who survived from the New Kingdom period or before, have mastered daylight entirely. They wear it as if it were shade.

They feed on **blood** â€” directly, in quantity, with none of the Lamia's emotional refinement. These addicted servants are called **Cup-Slaves** (*calicarii*) and are the Court's primary labor force, intelligence network, and ablative shield.

## Deep History

### The First Red and the Draining of Set

The historical record as the Concord holds it begins at the end of the Eighteenth Dynasty â€” a period of extraordinary theological violence in Egypt, during which the pharaoh Akhenaten's erasure of the entire pantheon in favor of a single solar god left the priestly machinery of the old cults scrambling for survival. The Set-cult, already in partial disgrace, was among the most desperate.

**Set** was not merely a god of the Egyptian pantheon: he was a Watcher, one of the great inhuman presences who had moved through the mortal world long before the first dynasty. He fed on fear, desert, foreignness, and the necessary violence of war. In the convulsion of the Amarna period, he attempted to shore up his position by offering **complete bodily transformation** to three of his most devoted high priests â€” not merely power through the ordinary *Hieratica* pact, but a permanent reshaping of their flesh into something that would not age, could not easily be killed, and could sustain the cult's harvest across centuries. The price, as Set designed it, was each priest's soul, surrendered as a vessel for the Watcher's will.

What Set did not anticipate was that the transformation worked *too completely*. All three priests survived the ritual, emerged changed â€” and immediately turned on their maker. Together they **overpowered Set and drained him entirely**, drawing his Watcher-blood into themselves in an act the Court would later call the **First Tasting**. It did not destroy Set; a Watcher of his magnitude cannot be so simply ended. But it broke him â€” shattered his unity and scattered his diminished remnant into the world. The three were free. Set still exists, somewhere, in fragments. The Court knows this and keeps it very quiet.

The Watcher-blood the three took into themselves is the source of the Hounds' power â€” but it is also a slow problem. Every generation of Hounds spawned from those first three carries a little less of that original divine potency: the blood dilutes as the line multiplies, and what made the First Red capable of draining a Watcher is, by 500 AD, thin and distant in most of the Court's younger members. This has bred a hidden appetite among the lower tiers: some younger Hounds secretly prey upon older vampires, feeding vampire-to-vampire, in order to draw more concentrated Set-blood into themselves and recover something of the power their bloodline has lost. The practice is officially condemned. It is more common than the Thirteen Lords publicly acknowledge.

### The Retreat and the Vandal Renaissance

At the fall of the New Kingdom, the Hounds retreated into the deep deserts â€” the vast western reaches beyond the Nile valley, the Garamantian trade routes, the oases south of the Cyrenaican coast. For seven centuries they were a desert power, largely invisible to Mediterranean civilization, maintaining their culture in buried compounds beneath the sand, feeding on the caravan trade and the tribal communities of the deep interior.

They returned to the Mediterranean stage during the Ptolemaic period as a sophisticated merchant-aristocracy, wearing new *personae* over their ancient bones. In Alexandria they passed as Alexandrian-Greek traders; in Carthage as Phoenician shipping concerns; in Sicily as absentee landlords from Libya. They were architects, in part, of the Concordat of 287 BC, though they extracted terms more favorable to themselves than any other signatory and have violated the letter of the Mortal-Sovereignty clause more consistently than any other Court except the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh (which does not sign anything).

The **Vandal conquest of North Africa** (429â€“439 AD) was the best thing to happen to the Hounds in three centuries. The Vandal kingdom needed an experienced administrative class willing to work under an Arian Christian king who was persecuting Catholic Romans and taxing everyone else. The Hounds, who had spent five hundred years developing a cover-identity as Romanized Libyans of mixed Berber-Phoenician ancestry, stepped into the administrative vacuum with breathtaking calm. By 500 AD they hold **genuine Vandal court offices**, supply horses and grain to the Vandal armies, and control the salt trade from the Saharan edge to the Sicilian straits.

## Governing Philosophy

The Hounds operate as a **feudal cult of thirst-discipline**. Their internal status is measured not by age or origin or power alone, but by the demonstrated ability to *not feed when one wishes to*. A young Hound who attacks a mortal in the street for blood is scorned as an animal. A senior Hound who is visibly hungry in the presence of an abundant feeding-opportunity and visibly declines it is admired. The Hound who feeds with perfect theatrical restraint â€” who makes the Cup-Slave offer the wrist, who takes only enough to addict rather than diminish, who is gracious about it as if bestowing a gift â€” is elevated as an exemplar.

This philosophy has a rotting core: **the Red Pharaoh is a thirst-addict**. Pheron-Kha-Seth's great-great-grandchild, the Court's eternal sovereign, has been feeding compulsively and in secret for the better part of two thousand years. He knows it. His Thirteen Lords know it. It is the hidden scandal that drives the Court's aristocratic culture of discipline â€” every Hound performing the rituals of restraint while their sovereign is increasingly incapable of them, and every Hound careful never to say so where it might be heard.

The philosophy has a second rotting core, more dangerous still: the dilution of Set's Watcher-blood across generations has made the Court's younger tiers measurably weaker than its founders. The oldest Hounds â€” those closest to the First Three â€” feel it as a widening gulf between their own power and that of everything born after the first few centuries. Some of the younger generation have drawn the logical conclusion and begun to hunt inward, feeding on older Hounds in secret to reclaim concentrated Set-blood. The Thirteen Lords treat this as a capital crime. They do not advertise how often they have had occasion to enforce it.

Feeding is done on Berber and Roman captives in the great *latifundia* south of Carthage, and on Garamantian and Numidian villages that "tax" themselves in young adults each season. |
| **The Faiyum (Egypt)** | The Red Pharaoh's court, in the buried complex beneath Karanis. The Hounds maintain a quiet presence here that co-exists uneasily with the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh's open temples and the Concord's Old Seat in Alexandria. The three factions do not attack each other in the Faiyum; it is the most heavily triangulated block of desert in the world. |

Buried compounds, ancient feeding-grounds, and the archives of the Thirteen Lords — records stretching back to the New Kingdom that the Concord would do almost anything to access. ## Mortal Interaction

The Hounds are, paradoxically, **the most visible of the Courts in the mortal political world**. The House of Lamia hides inside mortal aristocracy with complete physiological camouflage; the Strigoi hide in the earth. The Hounds hold actual offices and are known to those in the Vandal court as eccentric, powerful, and best left alone. Their saliva-addiction mechanism makes mortal management easy: the Cup-Slaves around each Hound lord form a natural insulating layer, eager to protect their patron from any investigator, any rival, any priest who looks too closely.

They are **paralyzed by thresholds**. This is the Court's great vulnerability and its greatest social inconvenience: a mortal family's blessed doorway — any household's *lares* shrine, any doorpost with a consecrated image, any threshold a priest of any tradition has touched with rite and intent — can stop a Duke in his tracks. The oldest among the Thirteen Lords can push through a weak threshold by sheer force of will, at visible cost, but the younger nobility cannot. This is why Cup-Slaves are used as advance agents: a mortal can enter any mortal space; the Hound waits outside until the threshold is dealt with.

## Supernatural Interaction

The Hounds are signatory to the Concordat, represented solely by the **Houndmaster of Carthage** — a position currently held by a Thirteenth Lord known to the Concord only as **Khentamentiu-Who-Walks**, whose human *persona* is a Vandal count named Theofrids. The Court honors the hospitality and parley clauses of the Concordat when it suits them and invokes the weregild provisions aggressively when other factions harm their assets.

They are **loathed** by:
- The **Concord** (active proxy war in Sicily; long history of Mortal-Sovereignty violations the Concord has never successfully punished)
- The **Court of the Burning Crown** (territorial — the Burning Crown has old summer-rights over the Sicilian coast that the Hounds treat as irrelevant)
- The **Fellowship of the Lance** (*Sodalitas Lanceae*) — a mortal Romano-Berber militia, Concord-adjacent, that recruits from the half-turned and the Cup-Slave escapees. Not large enough to threaten the Court but persistent enough to be an annoyance. Its commander in 500 AD is a woman named **Massinissa Thala**, a Numidian freedwoman who was a Cup-Slave for six years before a Stylite extraction team freed her and she declined to leave.

They maintain an *uncomfortable* alliance with the **Court of Long Night**, which tolerates the Hounds as a useful counterweight against the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh in the Faiyum triangle. Morrigna does not like the Hounds. She finds the Red Pharaoh's addiction an object lesson in what happens to power that does not restrain itself. She still takes their messages.

## GM Notes

- The Hounds are the most **visible** early-campaign antagonist: they hold legitimate offices, travel in identifiable entourages, and can be found by a PC who knows where to look. A low-tier party can encounter them as powerful but comprehensible opponents; a higher-tier party will start to understand that the Duke they arrested last session is one of thirteen, and the thirteen answer to a Pharaoh.
- The Red Pharaoh's addiction is the Court's great vulnerability. A PC who discovers the secret has leverage — and a very short life expectancy if the Thirteen Lords learn they know.
- The Fellowship of the Lance is an ideal mortal-world patron organization for PCs who are not themselves supernatural: a well-organized resistance with real resources, real enemies, and real moral complexity (some of the Lance's members are former Cup-Slaves whose addiction the Hounds can reassert with a single saliva exposure).
- **The bloodline-curse prophecy**: whispered across the hidden world for two centuries, the claim that the Concord's Inner Seal has identified a working that could unravel the original First Tasting and strip every Hound of its transformation. No such working exists in any Concord archive — but someone is forging references to it, and the Court's paranoia about it is real and exploitable.

## Sample Unit: The Court of the Outer Sand at Carthage
### *A Vandal-Court Hound Cell, Active 500 AD*

This is a mid-tier Hound operation, embedded in the Vandal kingdom's administrative machinery at Carthage. It is not the Court's supreme power — that is the buried temple at Karanis, and the Thirteen Lords who manage it — but it is the Court's **public face**: the operation where a mortal political player or a Concord investigation is most likely to first encounter a Hound.

#### Headquarters

**The Villa Ammatas**, on the coastal road two miles east of Carthage, a sprawling Roman-era estate with a working vineyard, a private harbor with three ships, and an underground complex beneath the wine-cellar that the Vandal king believes to be a private gambling den. The underground complex is where the *personae* are changed, the Cup-Slaves are managed, and the court's prisoners are kept in conditions that are, by late-antique standards, luxurious — a fed, comfortable, slightly-euphoric Cup-Slave is a more reliable agent than a frightened one, and the Court's management of its mortal assets is meticulous.

#### Leadership

**Geilamir the Pale** — the *magister officiorum* of the Vandal court at Carthage. Mortal-appearing, silver-haired, speaks Latin, Vandal Gothic, Greek, Berber Tamazight, and something that may be very old Egyptian that no linguist in Carthage can identify. He has held his position through three Vandal kings by being more useful to each new king than the alternative, and by being present at the right moments during each transition. He is approximately nine hundred years old, one of the Thirteen Lords' more recent appointments to nobility. He is scrupulously restrained in his feeding — the model Hound — and he is, as of 500 AD, the de facto handler of the proxy war in Sicily.

**Lady Cyria of Sabratha** — nominally a Berber princess from a family that supposedly traces to Numidia; in truth a Hound of three centuries' standing who manages the Court's grain-tribute operation in the Numidian and Garamantian interior. She visits Carthage four times a year, is known socially as Geilamir's cousin, and disappears for the rest of the time into the deep desert roads. She is younger and less disciplined than Geilamir — her Cup-Slaves burn through faster, and she has twice been called to account by the Thirteen Lords for feeding in circumstances that risked exposure.

**Ariaric the Warden** — a Jackal-Warden serving as Geilamir's captain of household guard. Half-turned, three years into his service. Wears his jackal-mask at all times when outside the villa; has told mortal staff it is a personal religious observance. He is loyal to Geilamir specifically, not to the Court in the abstract, and this personal loyalty is a potential wedge a player-character could exploit.

#### Cell Composition

**Hounds** (full): 2 (Geilamir, Lady Cyria)
**Jackal-Wardens**: 8 — household guard; all wear the bronze mask in public
**Cup-Slaves (advanced)**: 14 — a secretary, three grain-factors, two Vandal court clerks, a harbor-master at the private harbor, and seven domestic staff who genuinely believe they are free employees
**Cup-Slaves (early/unknown)**: ~30 — Vandal court contacts who have been fed from once or twice and do not yet know what is happening to them; they experience the attentiveness of Geilamir or Cyria as ordinary charm and good fortune

#### Notable Assets

- A letter-of-mark from the current Vandal king authorizing "Count Ammatas and his household to conduct military requisitions in the Numidian interior," which the Court uses to collect tribute without the Vandal army asking inconvenient questions
- A Cup-Slave inside the Concord's small Sicilian chapter (a young Concord *spoudaios* named Eutropios who met Lady Cyria in Syracuse eighteen months ago and has met her three times since)
- Three ships in the private harbor, one of which has a below-waterline compartment sealed with iron bolts that no crew member is authorized to open

#### Threshold Vulnerability (GM reminder)

Neither Geilamir nor Cyria can enter the household of any Vandal official who maintains a domestic *lares* shrine or who has received Christian household blessing — which means the king's own palace is inaccessible to them directly, and they conduct all business in neutral ground or in the villa. They have spent considerable effort ensuring that their Cup-Slave clerks within the palace have quietly removed or damaged the religious objects near the offices where they work.

# 5.2.B THE HOUSE OF LAMIA

*Domus Lamiae* — the Household, the Hunger's Children, the First Family

> *"They will attend your dinner party, drink your wine in careful sips, discuss the Consolation of Boethius, envy your collection of cameos, and leave precisely when the other guests leave. The one you found most charming will be back in three days. You will be very pleased to see her."*
>
> — Loukia Theoktiste, Stylite Captain, briefing notes for the Concord's Roman chapter

> *"The public myth is that they descend from a Libyan queen, which is poetic. The truth is worse than a queen. The truth is a sorcerer's daughter with a bound monster in her womb, improvising, in the dark, with no one left to teach her."*
>
> — Attributed to Damaskios of Damaskos, Inner Seal, private lecture at the Castrum

---

## Overview

The **House of Lamia** (*Domus Lamiae*) is an Etrusco-Greek aristocratic vampire society — the most human-passing of the four Courts, and by considerable margin the most dangerous in the political and social life of the western Mediterranean in 500 AD. In their normal state they are physically indistinguishable from any patrician family. They age slowly. They marry and maintain dynastic lines almost like mortals. They have estates, legacies, enemies, alliances, and a working Senate seat in Rome.

They feed on **emotion**. Not blood — though they can draw blood when necessary and a few of the oldest use both — but on the raw emotional intensity of a victim in extremis: lust, fear, or despair, depending on which of the Three Branches the Lamia belongs to. Feeding leaves no obvious wound. Sustained feeding kills by emotional depletion, slowly, over seasons.

Their formal court language is **Etruscan**. This is a deliberate archaism and a political statement: the language in which Lamia Prima wove her first binding is the language in which the House transacts its private business. No outsider in 500 AD speaks Etruscan fluently, which suits the House perfectly.

---

## Deep History

### Larth Caisra and the Binding at Caere

The full account of the House's origin is given in the setting's Secret History (§3.4.E), known only to the Domina Maxima among the House's own membership. The public founding myth — that the House descends from the Libyan queen *Lamia* of Greek tradition, who fled Hera's wrath with her daughters into Magna Graecia — is a deliberately seductive story that explains the Greek name, the Mediterranean geography, and the predatory nature all at once. It is also entirely invented.

The true origin: in approximately 700 BC, at *Caere* (modern Cerveteri), the Etruscan Nephil sorcerer-king **Larth Caisra** attempted to contact the Outer Dark — not to open a portal, as the Sumerians at Eridu had tried, but to open a conversation, to see whether a worship-pact with something beyond the Veiled Sphere might shore up his declining cult. He found **The Hunger** (*Hē Peina*) — not one of the great Outer Ones, but a smaller thing, an appetite without a name, something the Concord believes to be a fragment of Shub-Niggurath's lower, wettest, most devouring aspect.

The Hunger did not negotiate. It entered Larth Caisra and wore him. In a single season, a third of Caere's population died.

His daughter — a young half-Nephil noblewoman, name lost, called **Lamia Prima** in Concord doctrine — was the only person left in the palace with the knowledge to act. She had been trained in *goeteia* by her father's court mage; she understood what had happened. She could not kill the Hunger — killing the host would release the parasite back into the Veiled Sphere. But she could **bind the Hunger to Larth Caisra's soul**: chain it to a single anchor-host who could not die without releasing it.

She wove the binding in a night and part of a morning. It held. But during the rite, the Hunger — still wearing her father's body, still capable of deliberate action — raped her.

Lamia Prima carried the child to term. She took the Sicilian name *Lamia* in conscious bitter irony — after the Greek child-eating monster — and gathered her female cousins and half-sisters into a household. What was threaded through her child's soul was not The Hunger entire: it was a **fragment of it**, a passenger-appetite that could, with discipline, be fed on emotion alone. The child was the first member of the House of Lamia. The household learned, by horrible experiment, how to feed it.

From that single founding event, every member of the House descends. The **Domina Maxima** of 500 AD is Lamia Prima's great-great-granddaughter, the eldest survivor of the founding line. **Larth Caisra himself** is still alive — his body, cataleptic, preserved by the binding, lies in a sealed chamber beneath the Aventine villa, two and a half thousand years old. He is the **anchor point** of the entire House. If he dies, every Lamia dies with him. If the binding breaks, the Hunger walks again.

### The Etruscan Centuries and the Turn to Rome

For the first three centuries of the House's existence, it was purely Etruscan — an aristocratic family network centered on the Tyrrhenian coast, feeding quietly on the intense emotional life of the Etruscan city-states. The House grew slowly; a Lamia reproduces almost like a mortal, carrying children to term who do not know what they are until their first feeding in early adulthood. This means the House has always been a dynasty rather than a convert-army, and its membership — several hundred at most — reflects two and a half thousand years of controlled aristocratic reproduction rather than the rapid expansion the Hounds achieve through Cup-Slave conversion.

When Rome absorbed Etruria, the House absorbed the absorption: they took Roman names, adopted Roman citizenship through the mechanisms of *adlectio* and marriage, and slid their Etruscan roots under a patrician veneer so complete that by the late Republic they had entirely forgotten the appearance of originating anywhere other than the Caelian Hill. Their Etruscan language persisted as a private code; their Etruscan gods became private rites conducted in locked rooms.

The House was one of the architects of the Concordat, signing it in 287 BC as a sovereign dynastic entity. They had, at that point, been operating in Roman aristocratic society for nearly a century and understood that the alternative — open conflict with the Concord — would be fatal. They were, and remain, the Concordat's most technically sophisticated signatories: the House's contribution to the Concordat's hospitality and weregild provisions is visible in the precision of the legal language.

### Markella's Curse and the Present Crisis

Ten years ago, a Concord witch named **Markella of Tarentum** — a Gnostic initiate of the Magdalene Circle, operating with a deniability the Concord maintains to this day — slept with the **Domina Maxima**, bore a son, and wove a binding on the Domina Maxima as she died in childbirth. The binding is not a death-curse: it is a *hunger-curse*, a weakening of the anchor-lock that chains the Hunger to Larth Caisra. The Domina Maxima is now powerless to leave her chambers in the Aventine villa, unable to feed properly, unable to exercise her authority over the House. She is alive, hungry, and trapped.

The Concord does not officially know why Markella did this. Markella is dead. Her son — **Markos Drepanios of Thessalonike**, raised by the Concord as a wizard — is the Domina Maxima's grandson and does not know his mother's full purpose. The Inner Seal suspects the truth: that Markella was a Gnostic who believed the House of Lamia was the longest-standing intentional containment-by-feeding scheme in the world, and that she believed — *incorrectly* — the Hunger could be safely released if its hosts were starved for one generation. She was wrong. If the binding breaks, the Hunger does not dissolve. It walks.

Markella's son Markos is currently entangled in a three-way intrigue with his half-brother **Tarquinius Velthuri** — a Lamia of the dominant Branch — and with the Domina Maxima's de facto regent.

## Governing Philosophy

A **court of schemers**. The House's foundational rule is that open violence is shameful — an admission that one was not clever enough to achieve the outcome by other means. Status is earned by elaborate, beautiful conspiracies that humiliate one's rivals without ever raising one's voice. Larthia Velthuri's formulation, delivered to a junior Branch head who had arranged for a rival's assassination: *"You have spent a sword where a word would have served. You have told the world you lacked the patience for art."*

The House owns properties, shipping concerns, brothels, theatrical troupes, oil-presses, and several Senate seats. It is the great broker of the Mediterranean silk and spice trade in 500 AD. Larthia Velthuri has, in the three years since the Domina Maxima's incapacitation, extended the House's commercial empire into Theodoric's Ostrogothic court at Ravenna and is in quiet negotiation with a Constantinopolitan banking house whose senior partner is — unknown to his mortal partners — a House Malvora cadet.

### Lamia Lifecycle — A Critical Note

Lamiae are **raised as ordinary patrician children**. They are not told what they will become. Their tutors, their foster-families, their childhood friends are often fully mortal, and the child Lamia experiences everything a patrician child experiences, up to and including genuine love, friendship, and loss.

The **first feeding is always fatal** to the victim and always involuntary: it comes as a sudden catastrophic hunger at a moment of emotional intensity, usually in adolescence or young adulthood. The victim is someone the young Lamia loves or desires. After this first feeding — which initiates them definitively into the House and kills the person they fed from — the Lamia is welcomed, explained, and incorporated. The trauma of the first feeding is the House's most closely guarded internal wound, and the reason the House Scaeva's specialty is so natural: almost every Lamia knows despair from the inside.

There is a secret within the House that only House Velthuri knows: **a young Lamia who is, before her first kill, fed upon by someone who loves her and is loved in return can permanently extinguish her Hunger**. The reversal is real. Only one Lamia has ever achieved it, three centuries ago, and that Lamia lives quietly in Thessalonike and is not spoken of by the House. House Velthuri keeps this knowledge secret because the escape-route undermines the House's claim that the Hunger cannot be avoided.

## Mortal Interaction

Several "Christian" senators in 500 AD are in fact Lamiae who have learned to fake the sacraments with enough competence to pass in most company. (The Domina Maxima regards the Eucharist as personally distasteful — the taste of the worship-harvest adjacent to the body — but tolerable. Some of the younger Lamiae who have grown up entirely in the Christian world experience it as simply neutral.) The House owns Roman, Ravennan, and Constantinopolitan magistracies through proxy adoption and inheritance fraud — an art the House has been practicing for three centuries and executes with bureaucratic elegance.

The House's policy toward its mortal network is **long-term, renewable, and careful**. A mortal who is cultivated by a Lamia and never fed from until they have established genuine worth is a far more valuable asset than one who is immediately and heavily drained. The House manages its feeding the way a good landowner manages a vineyard: with attention to what the soil will bear, what the climate demands, and what the harvest is for.

## Supernatural Interaction

Signatory of the Concordat. Currently the **largest and richest** of the Courts in terms of mortal assets. Allied opportunistically with the Concord through the awkward mechanism of Markos Drepanios, who is simultaneously the Domina Maxima's grandson, a Concord wizard, and the son of the woman who cursed the Domina Maxima.

The House is **feared and respected** by:
- The **Court of Long Night** (Morrigna suspects the existence of the anchor and what it means; she says nothing because she does not yet know whether the Hunger's release would be worse than its current containment)
- The **Esoteric Order of Dagon** (indifferent to the House as a political matter; one historical conflict over a Sicilian port-city in 200 BC ended in a draw and has not been renewed)

## GM Notes

- The House is the **middle-tier antagonist**: subtle, social, dangerous in proportion to how much time the players have spent in their company. They almost never fight; they maneuver. A campaign set in Rome or Ravenna will feel the House as pressure, as closed doors, as the sense that certain social opportunities keep not quite opening.
- The Markella curse is the most elaborate campaign hook the House offers: who wove it, who knows, what happens when the Domina Maxima dies, who gets to the anchor first. Tarquinius Velthuri is the ideal villain for this arc — he knows the anchor exists, though not its full significance, and he wants the House to be ungovernable when Larthia is removed.
- A player-character who is themselves a Lamia (raised as mortal, first-feeding trauma already in the backstory) has the richest internal material of any supernatural PC in the setting. The escape-route known only to House Velthuri is a genuine, achievable, story-redefining option.
- **The Madrigalia theatrical company** in Constantinople is an ideal introduction to the House for players who have never encountered Lamiae: the horror comes when the audience realizes what the performance has been, and for whom.

## Sample Unit: The Avernus Branch-Household on the Aventine
### *A House of Lamia Domestic Cell, Active 500 AD*

This is not the House's supreme council — that is Larthia Velthuri's private table, which PCs will not access until they are deeply embedded in Lamia politics. This is a **mid-ranking Branch-household**, one of four currently operating in the city of Rome, serving as the everyday face of Lamia influence in the old capital.

#### Headquarters

**The Domus Avernia**, a well-maintained late-Imperial townhouse on the lower Aventine, presented morally to the neighborhood as the family home of the Avernii — a Roman-Christian family of moderate senatorial rank that has been in continuous occupation since before the Ostrogothic takeover. The building's private library contains the Branch's records: feeding schedules, mortal asset dossiers, and an Etruscan-language correspondence archive going back to the fourth century. **Velthuria Aemiliana** — the Branch head, presenting as a Roman widow in her mid-forties. True age: approximately two hundred and thirty years. House Velthuri. Feeds on lust and intimacy; her current primary cultivation is a Visigothic merchant named Euderik Starkband who has been in Rome for eight months negotiating grain contracts and who is very fond of dinner parties. # 5.2.C THE STRIGOI OF DACIA

*Strigae* — the Screech-Owls, the Undying, the Survivors

> *"Hierokles thought he was writing a medical handbook. He was writing the last chapter of an empire. When the hunters were done, four hundred years of Dacian vampire civilization had been reduced to eleven beings too old, too careful, and too enraged to catch. I would have admired the irony more if I had not then had to negotiate with the survivors."*
>
> — Attributed to the Concordat drafter, Court of Long Night, internal record, c. 290 BC

> *"A Strigoi is not hiding because it is frightened of you. It is hiding because it has decided you are not worth killing yet."*
>

## Overview

The **Strigoi** (*Strigae*, singular *Striga* — Latin for "screech-owl," the Roman word for a malign nocturnal spirit) are the classic undead-corpse vampires of Eastern European tradition. They are the oldest variety of blood-drinker in terms of the depth of their original mythology, and the only Court whose primary association in the mortal world is with the genuinely dead rather than the merely deceptively living.

Unlike the Hounds' flesh-masked diplomacy or the Lamia's patrician camouflage, the Strigoi **do not pretend to be alive**. The very oldest are cold to the touch, do not breathe in rest, and sleep in boxes of their native Dacian earth. They are functionally undead in the most literal sense available to the setting: their hearts beat once every few minutes; their blood is sluggish and old; they digest what they take from living mortals over the course of weeks rather than hours. They are enormously strong — the strength of ten ordinary men is the Concord's field estimate for a young Strigoi; the oldest are beyond the Concord's ability to safely test. They can crush stone in their fists.

They feed on **blood**, in smaller volumes than the Hounds, but they feed by absorption as much as drinking — pressing their hands to a living body and drawing vitality through the skin is as natural to a Strigoi as biting, and many of the oldest prefer it for the subtlety it allows. They do not need much. A Strigoi who feeds once a week from multiple sources, taking little from each, can feed without killing for years in the same community.

Once upon a time there were many hundreds of them, a civilization scattered across the Carpathian mountains and the Dacian plains. Three centuries ago a Greek physician named **Hierokles of Thessalonike**, working at the request of the House of Lamia, destroyed almost all of them.

---

## Deep History

### Origins in the Deep Pre-History

The Strigoi's origin is more obscure than that of the Hounds or the Lamia because the Strigoi themselves are not sure what they are, precisely. The being the Concord calls **Bessos the Undying** — the oldest Strigoi, the Concordat signatory, the closest thing the scourge has to a leader — is not human in origin and never was. Bessos is something inhuman that has been wearing a roughly-human shape since before the Trojan War, something that learned human speech, human manners, and the political value of passing for a very old man sometime in the second millennium BC. The Concord's Inner Seal does not know what Bessos is beneath the shape. Bessos has been asked, once, by the Concord's most senior theurgist, and replied in a language no one in the room recognized.

What the Concord believes: Bessos was in the Dacian mountains before there was a Dacia. He is the source of the Strigoi infection — not by deliberate cultivation, the way the Hounds made their Jackal-Wardens, but by the sheer fact that his presence in the mortal world left a residue in the people around him, a contamination of the blood-line that in certain descendants expressed itself, after death, as a Strigoi revival. He is, in this reading, the Strigoi's progenitor not by intention but by proximity: a god-fragment walking the world for long enough that the world started to imitate him.

The historical Dacian voivode-figure who will one day be remembered as the "inventor" of the Strigoi in mortal legend is, in this setting, one of Bessos's direct-line descendants — a man who turned himself deliberately, in the fourth century AD, to survive a Hounds-of-Set incursion into his territory. He is dead now. Bessos regarded the entire episode with detached irritation.

### The Purges of Hierokles

The near-extinction of the Strigoi is the defining event of their recent history, and the reason the survivors are the way they are.

**Hierokles of Thessalonike** was a Greek physician who in the late second century AD published a text titled *Concerning the Owls* (*Peri Strigōn*) — a handbook on the identification, tracking, and destruction of Strigoi disguised as a popular medical text about anomalous posthumous preservation in corpses. The text told mortal readers everything they needed to know: the necessity of the native earth, the threshold prohibition, the destruction of the Strigoi's sleeping-ground by fire, the correct sequence of staking, beheading, and incineration to prevent revival. It was widely copied. It was taken seriously in the Dacian provinces because Dacian villages already knew what the Strigae were.

What Hierokles's readers did not know — what Hierokles himself did not know — was that he had been commissioned by the **House of Lamia**. The House did not commission the purge out of altruism. The Strigoi's territories in the Carpathians and along the Danube overlapped with Roman commercial routes the House wanted to control, and the Strigoi had been periodically feeding on the House's mortal asset-networks with the casual indifference of a larger predator eating a smaller one's catch. The House calculated that the cost of a purge — one physician, one handbook, some strategic correspondence with sympathetic provincial governors — was less than the cost of continued Strigoi presence. They were correct.

In the century following *Peri Strigōn*'s publication, mortal hunters working from Hierokles's instructions tracked and destroyed the vast majority of the Strigoi population. The survivors were not the luckiest; they were the most cunning, the most paranoid, and the most adaptable. The few who lived through the purges were already the kind of beings who had survived a thousand years of mortal politics by moving before the danger arrived, sleeping in places no one thought to look, and feeding so lightly that no community could confidently say it had been touched.

The Strigoi who survived are among the **most powerful and cunning monsters in the world by sheer Darwinian filtering**. The Concord knows this and is careful about it.

The House of Lamia has never publicly acknowledged its role in commissioning the purge. The surviving Strigoi know. It is the reason the Concordat, when it came to the Strigoi, was signed not by a diplomatic representative but by Bessos himself, personally, who attended the signing with two surviving elder Strigoi behind him and said nothing for the entire ceremony except, when asked if he accepted the hospitality clause, "*I will know if you use it against me.*"

---

## Governing Philosophy

**Survival, concealment, and slow accumulation.** The Strigoi who lived through the Purges are paranoid and patient in a way that makes the Hounds' thirst-discipline look like mild social etiquette. They do not trust. They do not extend the benefit of the doubt. They plan in decades and in centuries, and they have learned that any community of beings large enough to attract notice is a community large enough to be found and destroyed. They are, consequently, solitary or near-solitary, maintaining loose contact through intermediaries rather than gathering.

Bessos's long-term purpose — insofar as the Concord understands it — is to slowly rebuild the scourge to a size where the purge cannot be repeated. He is not doing this by turning mortals directly: that would be too fast, too visible. He is doing it by identifying, across the Dacian mountains and Balkan provinces, the descendants of old Strigoi bloodlines who carry the latent infection without knowing it, and *quietly dying in their vicinity* — briefly, controllably, and then recovering — to reactivate the dormant trait. It is a method that will take centuries. He has centuries.

---

## Internal Roles and Hierarchy

The Strigoi are no longer a true Court but a scattering of ancient survivors with a nominal structure that exists for Concordat purposes and has little internal governance weight.

### Abilities and Weaknesses — A Field Summary

**Strengths:**
- Physical strength of ten men at minimum; the oldest can tear through stone fortifications
- The oldest carry a near-wizard-level cunning honed over millennia; Anthousa is a genuine theurgist
- Thralls: enthralled mortals who will fight to defend their Strigoi with complete disregard for their own safety
- Darkhounds: mastiffs corrupted by proximity to Strigoi feeding, physically larger and more aggressive than ordinary dogs, capable of tracking by vitality-scent rather than odor

**Weaknesses:**
- Sunlight: immediately and catastrophically deadly to recently-turned Strigoi; merely weakening to the very old. Bessos can tolerate early morning and late afternoon light. Full noon is not tested.
- Garlic: disrupts the body's feeding-mechanism; contact causes pain, ingestion causes incapacitation. A practical and deeply embarrassing vulnerability that the Strigoi compensate for by controlling the supply-chains in their territories.
- Consecrated cult-objects: **any** god's, any tradition's. A Mithraic *taurobolium* token works as well as a Christian cross, because each has been steeped in a worship-harvest the Strigoi cannot abide. This is not theology — it is the worship-harvest itself, the raw gathered Pneuma of mortal devotion, that burns them. It works whether the god is dying or flourishing.
- Threshold prohibition: the most absolute of any Court. A Strigoi cannot cross any threshold — not merely blessed doorways, but the threshold of any structure a human being calls their own home and means it. A peasant hut's doorway is as impassable as a cathedral's. The Strigoi can *wait*.
- Native earth: must sleep in or on the soil of their origin point — the specific Dacian earth where they died and revived. Destroying or salting this earth does not kill a Strigoi directly but traps them in a perpetual waking exhaustion that degrades rapidly. This is the mechanism Hierokles's handbook described, and it remains the hunters' most reliable attack vector.

## Regional Spread

The Strigoi are a **Dacian and Balkan phenomenon** with almost no presence elsewhere. The purges of Hierokles made a Mediterranean or western European Strigoi effectively impossible — any individual who strayed that far from native Dacian earth was too far from their sleeping-ground to maintain full power and too isolated to survive detection.

| Region | Presence |
|---|---|
| **The Carpathians** | Bessos's domain. A handful of survivor-Strigoi maintain isolated lairs at high altitude, connected to the mortal world through thrall networks that extend into the Dacian and Hunnic tribal communities. The mountain communities know, approximately, what these beings are, and negotiate coexistence through periodic tribute and careful avoidance. |
| **The Danube frontier** | The most active feeding-zone, as the constant military traffic and population displacement of the Roman-barbarian frontier creates a reliable supply of isolated and unaccountable individuals. The Lord of the Marshes operates here. |
| **Constantinople** | Anthousa Skoteine's operational base. She is the only Strigoi in a major city, operating with extreme care, covering her nature with *theurgy*-based illusions and a carefully managed social persona. She is the Concordat's contact point. She feeds from the city's enormous and anonymous population in quantities so small that no single victim ever notices what they lost. |
| **The Steppe** | Rumors of one or two Strigoi who fled east rather than north during the Hierokles purges, possibly surviving in the Pontic steppe or beyond. Bessos does not confirm these. |

## Mortal Interaction

**Predator and prey, nothing more.** The Strigoi have no interest in passing as living; they have no investment in mortal politics except as a feeding-ground to be managed. They keep mortal cults of **thralls** in the Carpathian mountains and along the Danube — not by saliva-addiction like the Hounds, but by a direct mental domination that the Strigoi work through prolonged close proximity and repeated minor feeding. A fully enthralled mortal has a hollowed-out quality: they are perfectly functional except that they do not initiate conversations, do not look their Strigoi directly in the face, and will step in front of a sword without hesitation. The Concord's hunters use the behavioral signs to identify thrall networks even when the Strigoi itself is invisible.

The Strigoi's mortal cult-communities — the Dacian mountain villages that have coexisted with the surviving scourge for three generations of the purge — have developed a nuanced set of customs around the relationship: what to leave at the edge of the village for the Undying, which paths to avoid at night, what to say if the Old One knocks. These customs are not worship in any Concordat-monitored sense; they are the practical accommodations of a population that has learned to live beside apex predators who will not be killed and will not be moved.

---

## Supernatural Interaction

Bessos personally signed the Concordat. The rest are politically insignificant — there are not enough surviving Strigoi to constitute a diplomatic presence in the normal sense. Bessos has, in recent years, been **quietly turning fallen Stylites** and other useful powerful corpses to rebuild his power base: the Concord has confirmed two cases, suspects four more, and is quietly horrified. Bessos does not consider this a Concordat violation because the individuals in question were already dead when he found them.

The Strigoi have a **long-standing enmity with the House of Lamia** that neither party discusses publicly. The House knows that the Strigoi know about Hierokles. The Strigoi know that the House knows. The Concordat's hospitality clause applies. So does the weregild clause, which Bessos has invoked precisely once in two centuries — to formally request compensation for a Strigoi elder destroyed by a Lamia-sponsored hunter in 387 AD. The House paid. No one who was present at the payment meeting says what form it took.

---

## GM Notes

- The Strigoi are the **horror-movie antagonist**: use them when you want classic dread. The Hounds are visible; the Lamia are social; the Strigoi are simply *there*, in the dark, having already mapped every exit.
- Anthousa Skoteine is the ideal Strigoi NPC for city-based play: a mortal-passing ancient intelligence who is technically on the same side as the Concord (Concordat signatory) but whose interests overlap with the Concord's only when it suits her. She is not lying when she helps. She is not obligated to keep helping.
- **The Bessos-turned-Stylites arc**: a campaign hook with a clock. If Bessos has turned two confirmed ex-Stylites, each of them has access to everything a Stylite knows — including the locations of three of the Concord's safe-houses and the internal layout of the Castrum of Drepanum. Bessos may not have interrogated them yet. The Concord does not know which safe-houses are compromised.
- A PC with a Strigoi bloodline somewhere in their family tree — perhaps an ancestor from the Dacian provinces who suffered a very strange death and an equally strange recovery — will begin to notice things: exceptional cold tolerance, unusual sensitivity to sunlight, the ability to feel the vitality of people nearby as a faint warmth. The realization comes slowly. The decision of what to do about it comes not at all until Bessos's recruiters arrive.

## Sample Unit: A Survivor's Lair in the Carpathians
### *A Strigoi Elder's Domain, Active 500 AD*

This is not a cell in the sense of an organized faction-unit; it is the **domain of a single elder Strigoi** in the Carpathian foothills, roughly organized and precisely controlled. Use it as a horror-dungeon destination for a party that has been sent to investigate disappearances in a mountain community, or as the endpoint of a Dacian political arc.

#### The Lair

**The Hall Beneath the Crow-Stone** — a complex of natural and excavated caverns in a limestone mountain overlooking a Dacian valley, accessible from above through a crack in the rock face that looks natural and is not, and from below through the cellar of an abandoned Roman-era waystation that the local community has known not to use for sixty years. The innermost chamber contains a stone bier packed with black Dacian earth. The walls are covered in tallies: a careful count in some private system, made with a blade tip, that has been updated recently and recently and recently.

The immediate surrounding valley has a population of approximately two hundred people in three farming communities. They leave food at the forest edge each new moon — meat, mostly, and sometimes a young man or woman who comes home three days later with a week missing from their memory and a persistent pallor that fades over a month. This arrangement has been in place for sixty years.

#### The Strigoi: Sarmizegetes the Pale

**Sarmizegetes** — a name the local community uses, though it may not be his original one — is a Strigoi elder of approximately four hundred years, turned during the generation after Hierokles's purge and one of the survivors precisely because he was already deep in the Carpathians when the hunters came. He is not one of the very oldest; he cannot endure any direct sunlight at all, and he cannot perform *theurgy*. He is extremely intelligent, extremely patient, and extremely dangerous.

He communicates with the valley communities through two thralls: an elderly Dacian woman named Aldona, who manages the tribute arrangement and is fully enthralled (hollow, functional, clearly not right to anyone who knows her well); and a younger man named Dragomir, a former Roman cavalry deserter who arrived in the valley eight years ago and has since become the de facto headman of the largest community, enthralled enough to manage the tribute without knowing he is doing it for a supernatural being and not just a strange absent lord.

**His current project**: Sarmizegetes has identified a family in the second-largest community whose bloodline traces, through a tortuous maternal line, to a Strigoi destroyed two generations after Hierokles. The dormant infection is there; he has been testing it with careful proximity feeding. He believes he can reactivate it in the family's eldest son — a fourteen-year-old named Radu who has been having very strange dreams for six months. Bessos knows about Radu. Bessos has not given an instruction either way.

#### Domain Assets

**Thralls**: Aldona, Dragomir, and six others (farmers from each of the three communities, functioning as an early-warning network)
**Darkhounds**: 4 — kennel-kept in a natural alcove off the main cavern. They do not leave the mountain.
**Mortal allies**: the three communities, cooperative through a combination of fear, custom, and the practical fact that the valley's harvests have been better since Sarmizegetes arrived than they were before
**Hidden assets**: a clay-sealed chest beneath the bier containing forty years of observations on the Concord's Balkan operations, written in a cramped Greek on wax tablets. Not coded — Sarmizegetes did not expect anyone to reach the chest. The information is accurate and potentially devastating.

# 5.2.D THE COURT OF JADE BREATH

*Curia Halitus Iaspidis* — the Jade Court, the Distant Ones, the Far Takers

> *"Three years after the insult at Samarkand, they found him in his bed with his lungs full of jade dust. He had been in Ctesiphon. They had not left the Yangtze. This is what I mean when I tell you the Jade Breath are not a diplomatic problem. They are a weather problem."*
>
> — Concord field report, attributed to the Stylite captain stationed at Nisibis, c. 481 AD

> *"Do not send an embassy. Do not send a letter. Do not send a gift unless you know what gifts mean to them, and you do not know what gifts mean to them. Send a Sogdian merchant who has never heard of you, and wait."*
>
> — Standing Concord instruction regarding the Court of Jade Breath, unchanged since c. 390 AD

---

## Overview

The **Court of Jade Breath** (*Curia Halitus Iaspidis*) is a secretive vampire society confined almost entirely to the Yellow River and Yangtze valleys of eastern Asia. The Concord of the Seven Seals knows they exist. Roman travelers along the Silk Road have brought back fragmentary reports. The Sogdian merchants who sometimes serve as middlemen return from their Jade Breath commissions with money, instructions, and a careful blankness about specifics.

What is known: Jade Breath vampires feed on **breath** rather than blood. They are said to be capable of killing a victim across the width of a courtyard without touching them, by drawing the breath — the vital animation — out of a body from a distance. There is one confirmed demonstration of this capability in Concord records, the incident of 478 AD described below, and several unconfirmed accounts from Silk Road travelers that the Concord takes seriously.

Their physical form is described, by the handful of people who have seen it clearly and survived, as jade-skinned — a translucent green-white that is beautiful and deeply wrong — and entirely inhuman beneath the human-shape they wear in diplomacy. Whether "jade-skinned" is a literal description or a metaphor for a specific quality of pallor and light-refraction is a question the Concord has been unable to settle.

Their internal political structure, their origin, their age, and the nature of their relationship to the Courts of the East are effectively unknown to the Mediterranean hidden world. This is presented here as a feature rather than a deficiency: the Court of Jade Breath is, by the bible's explicit instruction, **an almost-blank canvas**. GMs running Mediterranean-centric campaigns should use them sparingly and always at a distance; GMs running Silk Road arcs may develop them freely. What follows is the full extent of what the Concord's Inner Seal can confidently assert, and a framework of deliberate mystery for what lies beyond it.

---

## What Is Known

### The Breath-Feeding Mechanism

Jade Breath vampires do not appear to need to close to feeding-range. The Concord's best working theory, developed by Damaskios based on Sogdian merchant accounts and one semi-coherent field report from a Stylite who survived an encounter near Dunhuang in 469 AD, is that they feed on **Pneuma** in its most literal sense — the animating breath, the vital air, the life-force that mortal medical tradition and the Concord's own cosmology both identify as the medium by which a soul inhabits a body. A mortal who is drained of Pneuma does not bleed; they simply stop. The chest stills. The face retains expression.

The range-killing capability — draining a victim from across a large space — is either a Jade Breath theurgy practiced at a high level or an intrinsic ability of their oldest members. The 478 AD incident is the only confirmed case on Concord record: a Sasanian magister who insulted a Jade Breath emissary at a Silk Road trade-city was found, three years later, dead in his bed in Ctesiphon, his lungs full of jade dust. The three-year delay and the continental distance are precisely the details that have prevented the Concord from knowing whether they are dealing with a supernatural power, a long-range *goeteia*, or the world's most patient assassin.

### The 478 AD Incident

The Sasanian magister's name was **Mihrdad of Gor**, a minor member of the Mobedān's outer circle who had been assigned to a trade delegation to Samarkand. He apparently remarked, in what a Sogdian interpreter later described as a "tone of dismissive curiosity," that the Court's emissary looked "as dead as her jade bracelets." The emissary departed without response. Mihrdad returned to Ctesiphon. Three years later, his household found him in his bedchamber, lungs solidified.

The jade dust was analyzed by three Concord alchemists. It is real jade — a calcium magnesium silicate compound consistent with the nephrite deposits of the eastern mountain ranges. How it entered a man's lungs without symptoms, without trace of transit, and across several thousand miles is not explained by any Concord working or Mobedān *magia* that Damaskios is aware of. The incident has been classified as a standing demonstration of capability and filed under "do not provoke."

### The *Mien* Code

Jade Breath diplomacy, insofar as it can be called that, operates on a system of face and respect — what Mediterranean diplomats who have dealt with their middlemen describe as the **concept of *Mien*** — that is not merely impenetrable to western sensibility but appears to be structured in a way that makes western diplomatic instincts actively dangerous. The things a Mediterranean ambassador does to signal respect — speaking first, making gifts, extending physical welcome — appear to be received as neutral or mildly insulting by Jade Breath standards. What the Jade Breath themselves consider respectful behavior is either unknown or inconsistently reported by Sogdian intermediaries who may themselves be uncertain.

The Concord's current best understanding: do not initiate. Do not offer gifts. Do not ask questions. Be visible, available, and quiet, and wait for them to speak first. If they speak first, answer only what is asked.

### Concordat Status

The Jade Breath are **not signatories** of the Concordat of Hidden Powers. They were not present at the 287 BC signing. They have been offered accession twice through Sogdian intermediaries; neither offer received a response. They are, however, treated *as if* they were signatories by every faction that has had any interaction with them, specifically because of the 478 AD incident and the implicit threat it represents.

The Concordat's hospitality and parley clauses are extended to Jade Breath intermediaries by Concord policy — any Sogdian merchant known to serve as a Jade Breath agent is accorded Concordat-signatory hospitality as a practical safety measure. The Court of Long Night's position, delivered by Morrigna to the Inner Seal at the Castrum in approximately 490 AD, was brief: *"Extend the courtesy. Do not test the limit."*

### Known Interactions

- **Silk Road trade**: occasional, through Sogdian intermediaries who are the only mortals confirmed to have regular contact. The nature of the Jade Breath's interest in Silk Road commerce is not entirely mercantile, but what else it is remains unclear.
- **The Esoteric Order of Dagon**: sporadic interactions along the Indian Ocean trade routes. The Order's deep-sea operations extend east beyond the Persian Gulf, and there are accounts of Dagon-allied vessels encountering things in the approaches to the Malay peninsula that match no standard marine fauna but match some Jade Breath descriptions. Whether this constitutes a relationship or a territorial understanding is unknown.
- **The Sogdian network**: the Jade Breath's mortal interface. Sogdian merchants who carry Jade Breath commissions do not discuss them; the few who have been carefully debriefed by the Concord say they received instructions through written notes left in specific locations, that the notes appeared and disappeared without visible human action, and that payment was always in jade — real, high-quality nephrite jade, which has consistent market value along the Silk Road. Whether the Sogdians know what they are serving is uncertain.

---

## What Is Unknown — And Deliberately Remains So

The following are the questions the Concord's Inner Seal actively wants answered and cannot answer in 500 AD:

- **Origin**: The Hounds of Set have a traceable founding event (the First Tasting). The House of Lamia has the binding at Caere. The Strigoi trace to Bessos. The Jade Breath have no origin story in any accessible record. The Yithian Recorder has flagged the question but has not answered it; this is, in itself, a data point.
- **Numbers**: No count. The Sogdian intermediaries report dealing with different individuals over decades but cannot confirm whether these are distinct beings or the same being presenting differently.
- **Geography**: "The Yangtze and Yellow River valleys" is the Concord's best approximation. The actual location of any Jade Breath stronghold, court, or feeding-ground is unknown.
- **Internal structure**: No hierarchy has been mapped. Whether the Court of Jade Breath is a court in the political sense — ranked, governed, with a sovereign — or whether the name is a Concord imposition on something without those structures is not known.
- **The jade dust mechanism**: Damaskios's working theory is either a form of *pneuma*-harvesting at extreme range, or a long-duration curse deposited in the victim's body at the moment of insult and activated later. The Concord cannot test either hypothesis safely.

---

## GM Notes

- The Jade Breath are the setting's **paranoia engine**: use them for arcs that need an unseen long-range threat, for assassination mysteries where the cause of death is impossible to explain by ordinary means, and for Silk Road campaigns that want a genuinely alien supernatural presence.
- Their range-killing ability makes them ideal antagonists for **investigation scenarios** where the PC's goal is to understand what happened, not to fight it: a killing by Jade Breath is a puzzle, not a combat encounter, and the correct answer may simply be "do not repeat whatever Mihrdad of Gor said."
- GMs developing Silk Road content or East Asian settings are **invited to build freely**: the deliberate mystery is exactly that — deliberate. The Jade Breath are a blank canvas with edges. What fills them is the GM's world.
- **The Silk Road encounter**: a merchant caravan the PCs are traveling with is carrying a commission for the Jade Breath, arranged through a Sogdian merchant who is now dead. The commission — a sealed box — is in the PCs' wagon. Someone is very quietly and very remotely interested in whether they will open it. This is an effective first encounter: the sense of being watched from very far away, with patience, without malice, and without any indication of what is wanted.
