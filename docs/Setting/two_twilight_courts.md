## THE NATURE OF FAERIE AND THE VEILED SPHERE

The Faerie Courts are not a religion, and to approach them as one is to court catastrophe. They gather no worship-harvest. They keep no altars. They do not require belief, and they are utterly indifferent to whether mortals love them or despise them. What they require — what the deep logic of their existence demands — is the *payment of debts*.

The **Veiled Sphere** (*Apokryphon*) is the second of the three concentric shells of reality: neither the mortal world (*Kosmos*) of flesh and iron, nor the howling Outer Dark (*Exōthen*) beyond its outer wall, but the spirit-world that wraps the mortal Sphere like a second skin. Theurgists reach it by ritual ascent. Goeteians blunder into it by force. Dreamers brush it in sleep. The Faerie Courts live there, and they rule its inner reaches absolutely.

The nature of that rule is **aristocratic, not ecclesiastical**. The Courts are not mystery-cults with exoteric and esoteric tiers; they are a feudal polity of immense age, with sworn lords and sworn knights and sworn vassals, and with debts that accumulate across centuries. The Queen of the Long Night is not a goddess accepting sacrifice. She is a sovereign collecting on loans — and in 500 AD she holds paper on enough mortal souls to staff a small empire.

Fae beings — the *sidhe*, the sprites, the djinn of the desert wastes, the river-nymphs of the Gallic lowlands — feed not on worship but on **the flow of bargained obligation**. When a mortal swears to a fae and keeps the oath, a faint surplus of that mortal's **Pneuma** (willpower; magical potential) bleeds across into the Veiled Sphere and enriches the oath-holder. When a mortal breaks a fae oath, the debt redoubles and the fae draws on the penalty clause, which is always worse. This is why fae bargains are relentlessly precise: every word is a loaded mechanism. Mortal scholars who have survived to write down the experience universally note that the Courts speak *exactly* what they mean and hear *exactly* what they are told — the difficulty is that mortals do neither.

### The Three Queens and the Turning of the Year

Each Court governs itself through three sovereign figures in perpetual succession: **the Maiden** (who comes), **the Queen** (who is), and **the Crone** (who was). This is not mere ceremony. It is cosmological mechanics. The same woman occupies all three roles in sequence, progressing from one to the next as the century-scale seasons of the Veiled Sphere turn. No Queen can serve who has not been Maiden. No Crone can retire who has not been Queen. The chain is unbreakable, and the only gap in it is death — which, for a creature of the Veiled Sphere, takes extraordinary effort.

Each Court also designates a **mortal Knight** — a human champion wrapped in a *Mantle* of borrowed faerie power. The Knight is the Queens' sword-hand in the mortal world, the only entity authorized to kill on a Court's behalf outside that Court's own sworn membership. The Mantle returns to the Queen on the Knight's death. Knights are chosen for their potential, shattered by their service, and discarded when they break. Morrigna keeps hers alive just long enough to be dangerous.

The **Stone Throne** — a blood-fueled altar somewhere in the deep Veiled Sphere, its precise location known only to the Queens — passes between the two Courts at the solstices. The Court that holds it draws on a surge of raw seasonal power. Battles have been fought at the handoff. Treaties have been drafted to manage them.

### The Economy of Oaths

The Concord's scholars have long tried to systematize fae contract law, with mixed success. What can be stated precisely is the following:

- **A fae may not lie**. Not "will not" — *may not*. Falsehood in direct statement is physically painful to a sidhe of any rank and catastrophically damaging to a being of noble blood. This does not prevent misdirection, omission, technically-true framing, or every other rhetorical device short of literal untruth.
- **An oath sworn on a true name binds both parties**. Fae contract magic is, in the Concord's taxonomy, a form of Goeteia — the imposition of a true name on reality. The oath becomes load-bearing. Break it and the fabric of the binding tears through the breaker.
- **A debt unpaid compounds**. Interest, in the fae economy, is not financial. It is existential. A small favor returned late becomes a large favor owed. A large favor unreturned becomes the currency of a Knight's Mantle or a Queen's decree.
- **The Courts enforce each other's debts**. This is the structural reason the two Courts coexist rather than annihilate each other. A debt owed to Long Night that the Burning Crown holds in assignment is still a Long Night debt. Cross-court debt-trading is brisk, carefully recorded, and ruthless.

### Cold Iron and the Weakness of the Unseen

All Faerie — both Courts and the Free Folk alike — suffer what the Concord terms *ferrophobia*: an aversion to **worked iron**, the kind shaped by mortal smiths over mortal fire. Raw meteoric iron does not trigger the reaction. Bog iron occasionally smelted without proper forging likewise. But the hammer-and-anvil work of a human blacksmith carries in it something alien to Faerie flesh, and a wound from such iron resists the rapid healing the sidhe normally enjoy. A skilled Court healer can close a cold-iron wound eventually; a deep one may scar. For lesser fae, cold iron is lethal in sufficient quantity.

The Courts keep their own smiths — pale, silent beings who work in gold, electrum, and certain alloys of meteoric nickel — for their blade-work and armor. These metals are beautiful and harder than logic suggests, but they carry their own cost: a Veiled-Sphere blade that crosses into the mortal world without its bearer loses a fraction of its edge with every dawn.

## PART ONE: THE COURT OF LONG NIGHT

### *Curia Noctis Longae*

> *"The wall holds because she wills it. The wall will hold as long as she wills it. She has been willing it for four thousand years. What does that tell you about what is on the other side?"*
>
> — **Markos Drepanios**, after the Eridu-wall scare of 488 AD

### Overview

The Court of Long Night is the faerie power of cold, predation, silence, and the killing edge. It governs the winter half of the year's turning in the Veiled Sphere, the black-glass fastnesses of the northern latitudes, and the deep hours between midnight and first light. Its sovereign is **Morrigna, Queen of Hoarfrost and the Slow Knife** — one of the oldest active powers in the known world, perhaps the most politically intelligent being still operating in the mortal era, and the architect of the Concordat that has kept the hidden world's factions from annihilating one another for nearly eight centuries.

Long Night fae are called *Brumales* in scholarly Latin, "Winter Sidhe" in the common usage of Concord apprentices. They are not, as a whole, evil — but they are relentlessly self-interested, exacting, and patient in a way that makes mortal cruelty look like a child's tantrum. Morrigna does not punish for malice. She punishes because unpaid debts *weaken the wall*, and the wall is all that stands between the Tigris valley and what has been pressing against the Eridu breach since 2300 BC.

The Court's **public face** is an iron-cold feudal aristocracy of terrifying beauty and exquisite manners. Its **private reality** is a military organization permanently deployed on a supernatural warfront, governed by a Queen who considers every mortal life a potential strategic asset and every dead Knight a resource wasted.

### Deep History: Before the First Cities

The Court of Long Night is older than the Concordat, older than Rome, older than the cities of Sumer — and the oldest layers of its history are precisely what the Court prefers not to discuss.

#### The Hyperborean Inheritance

The setting bible's timeline places Hyperborea's submersion at approximately 12,000 BC, when the polar continent drowned under the advance of the world's changed seas. What survived was not a political structure but a *seed*: certain beings, too old and too deeply woven into the cold reaches of the Veiled Sphere to follow the landmass into the deep, simply remained. They are the reason the Court of Long Night's lore is Hyperborean rather than Celtic or Germanic or any other mortal tradition — because the Court's senior stratum predates all those traditions. The **Crone of Long Night**, An Chailleach Bhéara, was already old when the first Sumerian temple was raised.

These oldest beings — the Crone, her immediate circle, and certain unnamed sidhe who have served the Court since its first generation — remember Hyperborea not as legend but as lived experience. They remember what was worshipped there: the drowned god Tsathoggua, the Voormi Mother, and something they call, in an Hyperborean dialect no mortal scholar has successfully translated, *the Voice Behind the Cold*. Concord scholars who have compared these fragments with known Mythos taxonomy believe the Voice Behind the Cold may be an aspect of Azathoth — the blind daemon-sultan at the center of the Outer Dark. If this is true, it means the Crone of Long Night, who weaves at the edge of the Veiled Sphere where the Outer Dark begins, was once herself adjacent to the thing she now guards against. This is not a fact anyone has asked the Crone about directly, and no one who has tried to ask indirectly has returned with an answer they were willing to repeat.

What can be reconstructed from the Court's own records — maintained in the *Fual-Leabhar*, the Black Book of Arctistor, in a script that the Concord's finest cryptographers have only partially translated — is that the Crone spent the first three millennia after Hyperborea's drowning in what she terms *the vigil*: standing, alone, at the thinning place in the Veiled Sphere where the polar Outer Gates are weakest. She did not move. She did not speak. She wove.

What she was weaving, during those three thousand years, was the foundation of what became the standing seal on the Outer Gates — the first, lightest layer of the containment that the Eridu breach would later force the Court to reinforce with living flesh and oath-blood.

The **first-generation Long Night sidhe** — the Court's founding nobility — are not fully comprehensible as faerie beings in the way that the word is commonly understood. They are something older, crystallized around the Crone's will like ice around a stone. Some of them have not spoken a mortal language in living memory. Some of them have not fully manifested in mortal-adjacent form since the First Crusade against the Fomorians in the second millennium BC. They attend Morrigna's court in Arctistor on the solstices and are distinguishable from the younger nobility by the fact that the air near them is slightly wrongly cold — not the cold of winter, but the cold of the Outer Dark itself, which is not temperature at all but the *absence* of whatever makes warmth possible.

#### The Founding of the Court's True Mandate: The Eridu Breach (c. 2300 BC)

Long Night's identity as a faction is inseparable from the Eridu breach. In approximately 2300 BC — during the convulsions that shattered the Akkadian Empire and left the Mesopotamian plain in ruins — seven Sumerian Nephilim attempted what no being, mortal or divine, had successfully attempted before: they tried to open a *permanent, tended portal* to the Outer Dark near the buried city of **Eridu**.

Their reasoning was elegant and catastrophic. The worship-harvest from mortal cults, however large, is bounded by mortal population. The power that flows from an Outer One, offered in exchange for a feeding-channel, is effectively limitless. They had done the arithmetic; they believed they could manage the relationship. They were wrong about everything except the arithmetic.

The portal opened. Something enormous began to push through — not Azathoth or Cthulhu, both of whom are too vast for any opening smaller than the complete unraveling of reality, but something of middle rank, sufficient to dissolve the physical geography of the southern Mesopotamian plain if it manifested completely. The seven Nephilim died first, from the simple proximity of a thing that does not share their dimension. The survivors in the surrounding territory fled or were dissolved. The portal remained open.

The **Court of Long Night** — then in its first generation, the Crone barely a millennium into her vigil — felt the breach from across the Veiled Sphere and came at speed.

They could not close it. The wound was too deep, too fundamentally structural, punched through the membrane of the Veiled Sphere at an angle that healing would require more raw magical force than the Court possessed. What they could do was *contain* it. Morrigna's predecessor — the Queen who drafted none of the Concordat because she died at the breach doing something that cannot yet be described in ordinary grammar — led the first phalanx directly into the gap and began weaving the standing seal from *inside*.

The Queen died in the sealing. The Crone became the Queen. The Maiden became the Crone and then became the Queen and then became what An Chailleach Bhéara is now. The seal held.

The seal **still holds**, and the cost of holding it is permanent and non-negotiable. A constant fraction of the Court of Long Night's total military strength is at the Eridu wall at this moment, today, in 500 AD. The phalanx has not broken in four thousand years. It has been reinforced, rotated, replenished from the bodies of Knights who served out their Mantles and were not quite dead enough to be released. The seven Nephilim who opened the breach are sealed within the wall as structural material — their spirits used as load-bearing members, still aware, still screaming in the particular silence that only the near-Outer-Dark possesses.

This is the secret that the Court of Long Night has never published, for a reason that Morrigna has articulated to exactly three people outside her own hierarchy: *if the mortal world knew what stood between them and the Eridu breach, it would understand that the Court of Long Night is not a threat to fear. It is a cost to pay. And mortals, understanding that, would immediately begin looking for ways to reduce the payment.*

The Cult of Mormo — which is presently the most active force pressing on the breach in 500 AD, because every rite worked to Shub-Niggurath in the Mormo tradition pushes a feather's weight more on the Eridu seal — does not know this. The Concord's Inner Seal knows. The Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh knows and is deliberately exploiting it. Morrigna has not forgiven the Brotherhood. She never will.

#### The Drafting of the Concordat (287 BC)

After the chaos of the Etruscan vampire wars of the third century BC, when the Roman Etruscan bloodlines and the Alexandrian wizard-college came within a season of mutual annihilation in a conflict that had been escalating for forty years, Morrigna's predecessor-but-one (the Queen who served before the current Queen who serves before Morrigna — the lineage is long) called the parley.

The treaty she drafted is known to scholars as the **Concordat of Hidden Powers** (*Concordia Potestatum Occultarum*), or, in the Court's own deliberate self-deprecation, the **Pact of the Long Night**. That name is a calculated choice: Morrigna has always wanted the hidden world to remember *who* holds the wall. The Concordat's Outside-Threat Clause — which binds all signatories to defend reality against the Outer Dark — is the clause Long Night drafted with the most precision, the clause it has never once been willing to see amended, and the clause it enforces with lethal consistency.

The Courts of Long Night and the Burning Crown are both original signatories, with precedence given to the Court that drafted the treaty. In every negotiation since, Morrigna's ambassador opens by remarking that the Concordat is Long Night's document, and closes by remarking that it would be a pity if it needed to be withdrawn.

### Structure and Internal Roles

Long Night is a feudal aristocracy organized with a rigidity that would impress a Byzantine emperor and a brutality that would impress a Scythian warlord. Its tiers are not earned by mystical initiation or by spiritual development; they are held by oath, enforced by debt, and inherited by blood or compact, depending on which is more useful to the Queen.

#### The Knight of Long Night

The **Knight** (*An Ridire Dubh*, the Black Rider) is the most visible Long Night instrument in the mortal world and the most dangerous tool in Morrigna's hands. The Knight carries a **Mantle** — a binding of faerie power grafted to a mortal soul — that grants him strength, speed, a diminished need for sleep, partial resistance to mortal-world injury, and the singular ability to cross the boundary between the Mortal Sphere and the Veiled Sphere without ritual preparation or Concord oversight.

The current Knight is **Edica the Wolf-Marked**, a Pannonian mortal of middle age — though his precise age is difficult to establish because the Mantle preserves what it uses and discards what it does not need. He has been Knight for twelve years, which is long by historical standards; most Knights last four to eight years before the Mantle's demands exceed what the mortal body can sustain. Edica survives because Morrigna has developed an idiosyncratic protocol of returning him to Arctistor for periods of Court-administered recovery, during which he is kept in a state the Court calls *the useful suspension* — not quite dead, not quite functional, being repaired by the same mechanisms the Court uses to repair its wall-wardens at the Eridu seam.

He is brutal, precise, and broken in a way that has made him more effective, not less. He does not enjoy what he does. He stopped being able to enjoy things in year three. He does it because the Mantle does not offer an alternative, and Morrigna does not offer alternatives, and somewhere inside the mechanism of a Knight there is still a man who believes, abstractly, that the wall holding matters. This is what Morrigna looks for when she chooses: not courage, not skill, not loyalty, but the capacity to believe in a wall one cannot see.

#### The Noble Houses of Long Night

Below the three Queens, the Court is organized into **noble houses** (*Cúirtí*, in the Hyperborean-inflected old tongue; *Domus Brumales* in Concord Latin) sworn directly to Morrigna. Each house holds a *fief* — a territory in the Veiled Sphere, or occasionally a mortal-adjacent boundary location — and provides a sworn quota of wardens to the Eridu rotation.

The great houses of note in 500 AD:

**House of the Cat Sith** — Headquartered in the Veiled Sphere corresponding to the Alpine passes. The Cat Sith is a being of middle antiquity, a senior sidhe who manifests in the mortal world as something between a very large black cat and a very small disaster. The Cat Sith serves Morrigna as an intelligencer and message-carrier across the Alpine routes between Italy and the Germanic territories; he has a standing arrangement with three different mortal hedge-wizards in the passes who leave out cream and silver on certain feast-days and receive, in exchange, warnings of ambush and early weather forecasts of lethal accuracy.

**The Voice Grimaerex** — Not a house but an officer. Grimaerex is Morrigna's voice when she chooses not to speak directly — a tall, sexless figure in Court grey who attends every formal negotiation and speaks with Morrigna's full authority and Morrigna's exact words, phrased as Morrigna would phrase them, without ever indicating how the transmission works. Concord diplomats who have dealt with Grimaerex at length describe the experience as equivalent to arguing with a well-dressed echo.

**The Leannan-Sidhe** — A fae noblewoman of Long Night who has attached herself, in the fae tradition of patronage, to **Markos Drepanios of Thessalonike** (the Concord's Magister of the Inner Seal and bearer of *Crinis Noctis*, the Long Night gift-blade). The nature of the attachment is officially described by Morrigna as a "protective arrangement of mutual interest." Drepanios describes it as "my godmother," which is technically what the Leannan-Sidhe told him to call her when he was seventeen and she first manifested at his bedside. She has since watched, from a discreet distance, as he grew into the most politically significant mortal wizard in the western world, and she has done nothing — which is the most alarming thing she could possibly be doing. Morrigna knows the arrangement exists. She approved it forty years ago. The debt it will eventually call in has not yet been named.

**Jenna of the Green Teeth**, **the Redcap**, and **Rawhead** — Three middle-rank Long Night sidhe who serve, collectively, as Morrigna's enforcers in the mortal-adjacent border territories: the marshes, the winter forests, the fog-hollows where roads lose their names. They are not subtle, and they are not meant to be. They are the teeth of the Concordat's enforcement clause, the visible punishment for those who think hospitality law does not apply to short people and poor people and people who are far from any city that cares. They carry Morrigna's warrant, and Morrigna's warrant overrides the objections of the local Concord chapter, the local nobleman, and the local bishop, in that order.

**Hríðr-Beard** — A Free Folk lord who attaches to Long Night out of courtesy — a Germanic spirit of midwinter generosity who predates the Germanic tribes themselves and will outlast their successors. He is mentioned in the Fual-Leabhar as a *companion of the wall-court*, which means he has been providing something — resources, information, seasonal power-flow — to the Eridu rotation since before written record. He does not take oaths. He has never been asked to. Morrigna permits this.

#### The Warden-Phalanx: The Eridu Embassy in the Veiled Sphere

*Sample holding — for GM reference*

In the Veiled Sphere above the city of Eridu's correspondent location — above the marsh, below the thinning membrane, in the space that has no mortal geography but corresponds approximately to what a traveler standing in the Tigris lowlands would find if they stepped sideways through the right gate — there is a **Long Night military encampment** that has not broken camp in forty-three centuries.

It is called, in the Court's old tongue, *Dún na hÉrideach*: the Fort at the Seam. The mortals of Mesopotamia have no knowledge of it, because the gap between the mortal world and this fragment of the Veiled Sphere has been maintained by Long Night for four thousand years as a hard border. Nothing crosses that gap without Morrigna's explicit warrant. When the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh's cell at Sarra put measurable pressure on the breach in 488 AD, the camp doubled its size in eighteen days. It is currently at full war establishment.

**Commander:** *Brennain of the Unbroken Spear* — a senior Long Night sidhe, technically the third-ranking military officer in the Court after Morrigna herself and the entity that the Court calls the Wall-Marshal (name unrecorded). Brennain has been at the Eridu seam for one thousand two hundred years. He does not remember what the mortal sky looks like; he has not seen it in nine centuries. He is not bitter about this. He is so far past bitterness that the concept no longer has purchase on him.

**Establishment:** At current standing: approximately 4,000 Long Night sidhe under arms, organized into rotating twelve-year watches. At any given moment, roughly 1,200 are in active contact with the seam; 1,200 are in reserve; 1,600 are in the Court's deep-Veiled-Sphere rest-stations to the north and northwest, recovering from their contact-rotation before returning. The total Long Night military is estimated by the Concord at 50,000; this means roughly eight percent of the Court's army is committed to the Eridu wall at all times, permanently, with no prospect of rotation away.

**The Seven** — The seven Sumerian Nephilim whose summoning opened the breach are woven into the wall as structural elements. They are conscious. They feel the pressure of the thing outside. They have been feeling it for four thousand years. Brennain believes this is the most important fact about the wall: not its strength, but the fact that the seven can feel what they created, and knowing they created it, they hold with every fragment of remaining will rather than allow themselves to be used as the opening. Morrigna has never confirmed whether she designed this, or whether it is simply what happened.

**Current threat:** The Brotherhood working at Sarra in 488 AD produced what Brennain's warden-reports describe as *seven heartbeats of true pressure* — moments in which the membrane flexed inward rather than simply trembling at the outside force. The membrane is currently, two years later, measuring slightly higher ambient pressure than the 487 AD baseline. Brennain has requested reinforcement. Morrigna has authorized it and not yet delivered it. This gap — between authorization and delivery — is a political matter, and political matters follow their own timeline.

### Philosophy: Cold Reason and the Wall

Long Night is not evil. It is also not good. It is a thing that understands, with absolute precision, what the universe's ledger looks like when the wall does not hold, and it organizes every resource it has toward the permanent prevention of that outcome.

Morrigna believes in the following propositions, in approximately this order:

1. The Outer Gates must not open.
2. A closed gate requires a wall. A wall requires wardens. Wardens require resources. Resources must be secured from available sources.
3. Mortals are available sources.
4. Mortal Pneuma, channeled through oath-debt and bargain-obligation, is the cleanest and most renewable resource known to the Veiled Sphere's economy.
5. Mortals who understand this and contract willingly produce better-quality debt-flow than mortals coerced.
6. Therefore the Court of Long Night will deal honestly, precisely, and with lethal consistency in the enforcement of contracts, because an economy of voluntary willing oaths functions better than an economy of fear.
7. The cost of running such an economy is a Queen who cannot afford to let a single debt slide, because every unmaintained obligation weakens the system, and a weakened system weakens the wall.

This philosophy produces a Court that is scrupulously fair and absolutely merciless. Morrigna has never broken a bargain. She has also never failed to collect on one. She does not threaten; she does not rage; she explains, precisely, what she is about to do and why, and then she does it.

She loathes inaccurate mortal folktales about Faerie with a venom that surprises most people who encounter it for the first time. The tales in which clever mortals trick or evade the Winter Queen are, to her, a technical threat: they teach mortals that bargains can be broken with cleverness, which is not true — and mortals who believe false things about debt-management tend to construct bargains that they cannot honor, which creates messy enforcement situations that Morrigna considers a waste of everyone's time.

### Holdings

**Arctistor** ("Frostward") — The Court's capital, a fortress of black glass and bone-white pillars at the northernmost reach of the inner Veiled Sphere. Accessible through known gates in Iceland, the Carpathians, and the high Caucasus. The interior is maintained at a temperature appropriate for its Queen — cold enough that mortal visitors can see their breath, warm enough that their blood does not freeze in their veins. The Court's great hall is called the *Ionad Fuar*, the Cold Place, and its floor is a mosaic of Hyperborean script that no Concord scholar has yet translated entirely.

**The Eridu seam** (*Dún na hÉrideach*) — Described above. Not a capital; not a court holding in any ordinary sense. More like a wound the Court carries, permanently.

**Border-stations** — Long Night maintains what the Concord calls *frost-gates*: small fortified Veiled-Sphere installations adjacent to mortal-world locations in the northern latitudes, at which the Court's agents collect arriving debt-holders, process newly sworn bargains, and maintain the local information network. Active frost-gates in 500 AD include locations corresponding to the Carpathian passes, the Caledonian frontier (important for the Arthurian contacts described in the world-at-500-AD regional notes), and the Caucasus range.

### Mortal Bargains and Supernatural Interaction

Long Night does not seek converts or worshippers. It seeks *contractors*. The Court will negotiate with any mortal who comes before it in good faith, and it will honor any bargain struck in the correct form. It will also buy existing debts from lesser fae — a practice Morrigna has raised to an art form — drawing promising or useful mortals under Court oversight before those mortals have realized the magnitude of what they owe.

**Standard terms**: The Court's contracts typically run in triads: three gifts for three obligations, or three years of service for one boon, or three favors called at the Court's discretion in exchange for one named thing the petitioner desires. The number three is not mystical; it is the minimum number of exchanges required to establish a debt-relationship that the Court can sustain across calendar and season changes.

**The Knight-track**: The most direct route for a mortal to enter deep Long Night entanglement is through the Knight succession. The Court recruits potential Knights constantly, through the Leannan-Sidhe tradition (attaching a Court noblewoman to a young person of exceptional potential and watching what they become) and through the practice of *debt-purchase* (acquiring the obligations of a mortal who has already demonstrated they can carry a significant load). Knights do not apply; they are chosen. The process takes years of observation and begins before the subject knows they are being evaluated.

**Concord relationship**: Long Night is a Concordat signatory and maintains a standing diplomatic relationship with the Concord's Inner Seal. The relationship is one of mutual respect and mutual wariness: the Concord knows what the wall costs; Long Night knows that the Concord holds the Edged-Seal blades and certain other instruments that could, in extremity, close the breach more permanently if everything else failed. *Crinis Noctis* — the blade that can wound a being only partly manifested in the mortal Sphere — is a Long Night gift to Markos Drepanios, given in 421 AD as payment for a Concord service at the Eridu wall that neither party will publicly describe.

**Persian Mobedān**: Long Night maintains a long and surprisingly warm alliance with the Zoroastrian fire-priests of Persia, whose dualist theology — the war between *ahura* (truth-light) and *daēva* (lie-dark) — is the closest any mortal religion has come to correctly describing the actual strategic situation. Morrigna finds the Mobedān intellectually refreshing. The Mobedān find Long Night philosophically coherent. The alliance predates the Concordat; it has survived five dynasties, two Persian civil wars, and the Mobedān's own internal reformation.

**The Church**: Long Night has no formal relationship with the Christian Church, and the Church has no official position on Faerie Courts that does not amount to "pretend they do not exist." In practice, the Court occasionally deals with individual bishops and abbots in territories where it needs mortal-world cooperation for a specific objective. These arrangements are always precisely framed and always honored. The bishops in question tend not to mention them in their correspondence with Rome.

### GM Notes

- **Morrigna as antagonist**: She is not a monster; she is a Queen with a strategic problem the size of eternity and a limited budget. GMs should play her as someone who has already thought through whatever the PCs are planning, already priced it, and already decided whether it is worth the overhead.
- **The Niamh arc**: The *nemesion* taint in Niamh of the Pale Hand is the setting's most dangerous open secret. If the Maiden accedes to the succession, there will be an Outer-tainted Queen of Long Night. If she does not, Long Night has no Maiden, which breaks the triadic succession and creates a constitutional crisis with cosmic consequences. Morrigna has been managing this problem for three years. She has not solved it yet.
- **Edica the Knight**: A PC ally who is slowly being consumed by his Mantle. Not evil. Not free. Completely aware of both.
- **Cold iron as plot device**: A Concord-aligned PC with a worked-iron weapon is the most dangerous person in any room that contains a Long Night sidhe at or below noble rank. This should matter.

## PART TWO: THE COURT OF THE BURNING CROWN

### *Curia Coronae Ardentis*

> *"My sister does not hate the world, you understand. She simply considers its safety a debt that falls due. I am the one who loves it. This makes me the more dangerous of the two."*
>
> — **Aigle, Queen of Lions and Olives**, to the Concord ambassador Petronius Avianus, after the Concordat renewal negotiations of 412 AD (Concord archive copy)

### Overview

The Court of the Burning Crown is the faerie power of growth, heat, fertility, and the relentless insistence of life. It governs the summer half of the year's turning in the Veiled Sphere, the gold-light corridors of southern Europe's spiritual geography, the warm hours between first light and noon. Its sovereign is **Aigle, Queen of Lions and Olives, Lady of the Burning Crown, Lady of Flowers** — the twin sister of Morrigna, her eternal counterbalance, and the being who is, in some accounting, more dangerous than the Winter Queen simply because she is the one who has chosen to love what she protects.

Burning Crown fae are called *Aestivales* in scholarly Latin, "Summer Sidhe" in the common usage of the Concord's apprentices. They are warm, passionate, and capable of a ferocity that surprises those who mistake their beauty for gentleness. The Summer Sidhe do not offer cold logic in their bargains — they offer intoxicating possibility, the dream of what might be, the burning certainty that this particular summer is the best one that has ever been or will be. They are not less precise than Long Night. They are differently precise. Long Night counts debts; the Burning Crown counts passions, which are harder to calculate and easier to overpay.

The Court's **public face** is a sun-drenched aristocracy of breathtaking warmth, creativity, and fierce loyalty. Its **private reality** is a Court that keeps the Winter Queen from becoming the world's only power, that prevents the logic of the Eridu wall from gradually consuming every mortal soul in the northern hemisphere, and that has been doing this work since long before the first Sumerian city was built — since before the catastrophe that drowned the civilization the Court still privately calls *home* — without the advantage of being able to tell anyone why.

### Deep History: The Drowned Crown

#### Origins in Atlantis (before c. 12,000 BC)

The Burning Crown is far older than the Mediterranean cultures through which mortals know it, and its truest origin is one the Court guards as jealously as Long Night guards the secret of the Eridu wall. **The Court of the Burning Crown is the surviving heir of Atlantis.**

This is the deep symmetry of the two Twilight Courts, and the Concord's Inner Seal considers it among the most dangerous facts in their keeping. Long Night descends from **Hyperborea**, the polar civilization that flourished around 30,000 BC and drowned around 12,000 BC, carrying out of its death the cold seed that became the Winter Court. The Burning Crown descends, in exactly the same way, from **Atlantis** — the warm-water antediluvian thalassocracy of the deep Atlantic, an oceanic civilization of equal antiquity and opposite temperament, drowned in the same long age of catastrophe that took Hyperborea. Two elder peoples, polar and oceanic, cold and warm, sank into the sea within the same handful of millennia; and from each the Veiled Sphere drew up a Court. This is not coincidence. The Crones in the Cottage, who predate both Courts, know why. They have not said.

Atlantis in its glory was not the corrupted thing its surviving children are now remembered as. It was a civilization of extraordinary creative and Pneumatic fertility — builders, breeders of green and living things, lovers of beauty and excess and the deliberate cultivation of joy — and it is from this character, not from any later Mediterranean source, that the entire temperament of the Burning Crown descends. When Atlantis drowned, its people did not simply die. A portion of them, the part that had loved the living world most fiercely, was drawn into the Veiled Sphere and transfigured over the slow millennia into the *Aestivales*, the Summer Sidhe. The Court's oldest nobles remember the drowning the way a mortal remembers a childhood house: not as history, but as the shape of the loss that made them what they are.

**Not all of Atlantis was drawn upward.** The catastrophe offered a choice, and not everyone made the same one. Where the future Burning Crown clung to the living world and was lifted into Faerie, others reached instead for the only powers great enough to promise survival on their own terms — the Outer Dark and the entities that crawl out of it. These are the **corrupted Atlanteans**: the estranged kin of the Burning Crown, antediluvian survivors who bought their endurance from Nyarlathotep and his kind, and who have persisted, twisted and deathless, into the present age. The druid-lord **Drest** who commands the majority of Britannia's druidic circles, and **Menes** who has held power in Ægyptus for thousands of years, are both of this kindred — kept by the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh and the cults that serve the Crawling Chaos. The Burning Crown does not speak of them as enemies, exactly. It speaks of them, when it speaks of them at all, the way one speaks of a brother who chose something unforgivable. Aigle has standing instructions that no corrupted Atlantean is ever to be killed in the Court's name without her personal sanction — not out of mercy, but because they are the last living memory of what the Court was before the water, and she has not yet decided what that memory is worth.

#### The Bull-Courts of Crete and the Green Ladies of Etruria

The Mediterranean cultures through which the Court is best documented are not its origin but its **daughter-traditions** — the warm coastal civilizations the already-ancient Burning Crown adopted and shaped in the long millennia after the drowning, as it rebuilt its mortal-world engagement from the ruins of an Atlantic heritage no living mortal remembered. Chief among these were the **gold-crowned bull-kings of Crete** — the Minoan palatial civilization that flourished from approximately 2700 to 1100 BC — and the **green ladies of the Etruscan forests**, the spirits of boundary and fecundity who presided over the sacred groves of pre-Roman Italy. To a mortal scholar these look like the Court's roots. They are in fact its second flowering, the Atlantean temperament finding new soil.

The Cretan connection is not purely mythological. The Minoan cult of the sacred bull — a practice the Concord's more careful scholars have connected to the worship of a genuine pre-Olympian Burning Crown noble who manifested in bull-form during the palace ceremonies at Knossos — fed the Court a sustained Pneuma-flow for fifteen centuries. The labyrinth at Knossos was not, as later Greek legend would have it, a prison. It was a **ritual theater**: the sacred space in which the Court's mortal-world contractors renewed their bargains annually, in the presence of the noble who held their oaths, with the bull-dance as the formal witness. The Minotaur was the name mortals gave to that noble when they stopped being able to see him clearly.

The Etruscan connection runs deeper and is more complicated. Long Night and the Burning Crown both operated in pre-Roman Italy, and the **Etruscan sacred landscape** — its haruspicy, its complex theology of divine counsel, its insistence that the world is thick with presences that must be consulted before any important action — was shaped in part by two centuries of active Burning Crown engagement with Etruscan mortal culture in the ninth and eighth centuries BC. The Burning Crown's habit of direct romantic and creative engagement with mortals — the *violent love affair*, as the faction overview notes — produced a generation of Etruscan artists, augurs, and kings who worked in the Court's aesthetic tradition without being formally sworn to it.

This was, in hindsight, imprudent. The Etruscan king Larth Caisra — who in approximately 700 BC attempted to contact the Outer Dark and was possessed by the entity the Concord calls *The Hunger* — had Burning Crown cultural sensibilities, a Concord-trained magus in his court, and precisely the combination of ambition and aesthetic confidence that the Court's engagement style tends to produce. His daughter Lamia Prima was, by some accounts, herself attached to a Burning Crown noble as a young woman, through the fae equivalent of a patronage arrangement. The Burning Crown does not discuss this. The establishment of the House of Lamia is the event that ended the Court's direct engagement with Etruscan aristocratic culture.

#### The Tuatha Dé Danann and the Fomorian War (c. 2000–1100 BC)

The Burning Crown's defining military history is the long war against the Fomorians — the Deep One colonial polity that the Esoteric Order of Dagon planted in the western Atlantic between approximately 2000 and 1000 BC.

The Fomorians (the Irish tradition's name for the Deep One hybrid population of the western coast) were not, from the Burning Crown's perspective, an ideological enemy. They were a **territorial encroachment** on the Court's western holdings, combined with a humanitarian catastrophe for the Bronze Age Irish populations that the Burning Crown had been dealing with as mortal contractors for centuries.

The conflict was long and exhausting — centuries of raid, counter-raid, uneasy ceasefire, and renewed violence. The Burning Crown of the second millennium BC was in many ways a different entity from the Court of 500 AD: its Queen at the time of the Second Battle of Mag Tuired was a being the Court's records call *Áine Mór*, the Great Bright One, whose connection to solar power was strong enough that the Concord's later scholars have confused her with a minor Nephil. (She was not a Nephil. She was simply a very old, very powerful sidhe at the apex of her summer-season strength.) The conflict is remembered in Irish tradition as the war between the Tuatha Dé Danann and the Fomorians — and this tradition is accurate in outline.

The Tuatha Dé Danann *are* the Aes Sídhe of the Burning Crown. The mortal-Irish memory of them as divine beings, as a race of gods who fought the sea-monsters and won, is not wrong — they are simply wrong about the *kind* of divine. The named gods of the Irish pantheon are Burning Crown sidhe remembered through the distorting lens of several thousand years of oral tradition. Lugh, the Dagda, Brigit, Nuada, the Morrígan (whose name is a mortal conflation of both Queens' aspects, causing considerable archival difficulty): these are, in the Court's own records, Burning Crown nobles of the second and first millennia BC.

One exception: **the Dagda** himself — the All-Father of Irish tradition, the god of abundance and the sacred cauldron — was a **true Nephil**, not a sidhe. His involvement in the war at Mag Tuired was the act of a genuinely divine being choosing to fight alongside a faerie Court against a Mythos incursion, which is both rare and indicative of the severity of the Fomorian threat. In 500 AD, the Dagda is a quiet retired nobleman on the Dingle Peninsula in southwest Ireland, living under the name **Eochaid Ollathair** in a hall that is somewhat larger on the inside than on the outside, no longer collecting worship, occasionally visited by the Court's ambassador, and absolutely certain that nobody will bother him in Dingle.

He is wrong about this, but it has not happened yet.

The **Second Battle of Mag Tuired** (Concord-dated approximately 1100 BC) ended the Fomorian threat decisively. The Burning Crown's Tuatha-aligned host shattered the Fomorian land-army and drove the survivors back below the waves. The **Eldest Gruff** — the oldest of the warrior-knight gruff kindred, a being who manifests in mortal legend as the great troll of the bridge, the tested adversary of presumptuous travelers — fought at Mag Tuired as a senior military commander and has held the Burning Crown's western frontier service-obligation ever since.

The Esoteric Order of Dagon's Atlantic chapter never recovered. The rump survived on Innis Mor and the Aran islands, and is in 500 AD quietly rebuilding under a new organizational structure. The Burning Crown has been tracking this process. Aigle has already asked three independent parties to report on it. The **blood-grudge** the Court holds against the Esoteric Order is not a political position; it is a structural feature of the Court's identity. The Burning Crown fought the only Mythos faction that has ever been defeated in open battle by Faerie. It will always answer a call to fight Deep Ones.

#### The Solstice Crisis of 497 AD

The most recent crisis in the Court's history is recent enough that the blood has barely dried. In 497 AD, **Heliodora** — then the Lady of the Burning Crown, Aigle's daughter and the Maiden of the Summer succession — attempted to seize Long Night's seasonal mantle at the summer solstice.

Her reasoning, from what the Burning Crown's records indicate, was that she believed a lasting peace between the Courts required one Court to hold both seasonal powers simultaneously long enough to demonstrate that the long winter-summer polarity could be transcended. She was idealistic, brilliant, and wrong about the metaphysics in a way that would have unbalanced the entire cosmological system of the Veiled Sphere if she had succeeded.

She was killed at Arctistor by **Markos Drepanios**, bearing *Crinis Noctis*, with the Burning Crown's own Knight at his side — a sequence of events that is diplomatically extraordinary. The Knight's presence is interpreted by some Concord scholars as evidence that Aigle knew, in advance, what her daughter was planning and did not stop it; that she sent the Knight to Arctistor as a witness and a de facto blessing of the killing. Aigle has never confirmed or denied this.

What is confirmed is that Aigle holds a personal grudge against Markos Drepanios for the death of her daughter — and that this grudge is real, not diplomatic theater, even if Drepanios acted with her implicit or explicit sanction. Fae Queens are not required to be logically consistent about grief. The grudge is load-bearing, emotionally and politically, and it will eventually have to be settled.

### Structure and Internal Roles

The Burning Crown is a feudal aristocracy organized on the same maiden-queen-crone triadic structure as Long Night, but executed with a warmth and lateral communication that the Winter Court entirely lacks. Where Long Night is hierarchy enforced by debt, the Burning Crown is hierarchy maintained by affection — which is more resilient in peacetime and more volatile in crisis.

#### The Three Queens of the Burning Crown

| Title | Name | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| **The Crone** (unnamed; title only) | The Crone of the Burning Crown | Co-occupant, with the Crone of Long Night, of the Cottage at the edge of the Veiled Sphere. She weaves gold thread. The Crone of Long Night weaves black. They share a hearth, a loom, and a cauldron, and have not stepped outside in a thousand years. A third presence is sometimes felt in the cottage — a weeping figure that smells of pus and old ice, which Concord scholars hypothesize to be the **Voormi Mother** (*Iod*) of Hyperborean tradition, sister-in-blood to Mother Winter. This hypothesized sister-link implies that the two Crones' origins predate the Courts themselves and lie somewhere in the deep Hyperborean past, adjacent to the Outer-One traditions that Hyperborea worshipped. |

In 500 AD, Aigle is grieving, politically isolated within her own Court (the Heliodora crisis fractured several of the Court's noble allegiances), and more dangerous than she has been in a century precisely because grief in a Burning Crown Queen does not produce retreat — it produces intensity. She is warm, fast, and not currently interested in being reasonable. She is also the person who keeps Morrigna from letting the logic of the Eridu wall consume too much of the mortal world's available Pneuma, which makes her, structurally, a power that cannot be allowed to fail. |

#### The Knight of the Burning Crown

The current Knight is **Theodoros Sebennytos**, a former Egyptian soldier from the village of Sebennytos in the Nile Delta — a man of middle age, dark-featured, trained in the Roman cavalry tradition of the eastern provinces before his discharge, found by a Burning Crown recruiter during a period of extreme personal crisis three years after his military service ended.

His Mantle is *different* from Edica the Wolf-Marked's in character: where Long Night's Mantle is a cold tool, a thing that uses its bearer's will as fuel, the Burning Crown's Mantle is something closer to a flooding tide — it amplifies what is already there, including things the bearer might not want amplified. Theodoros is honor-driven and exhausted and in love with someone he cannot have, the last being a circumstance the Burning Crown's Mantle makes considerably more acute and considerably more painful.

He is effective. He is not happy. He will not ask to be released because he believes, correctly, that he is currently the Court's best instrument for the problems facing it in 500 AD, and he is not capable of stepping away from a problem while he can still reach it.

#### The Noble Houses of the Burning Crown

**The Eldest Gruff** — A warrior-knight of extraordinary antiquity, the patriarch of the gruff kindred (beings who appear in mortal legend as trolls, bridge-guardians, adversaries of the presumptuous). The Eldest Gruff is not the Burning Crown's most gracious social presence, but he is its most experienced military officer after the Queen herself, and his service record at Mag Tuired means the Court's other nobles treat his opinions on military matters with an attention they do not extend to anyone else. He is, in 500 AD, stationed at the Burning Crown's western frontier holdings, keeping watch on the Innis Mor situation.

**Korrikōs** — A Cretan-origin Burning Crown sidhe, male, compact and dark, with a connection to the old labyrinth-cult that expresses itself as an occasional tendency to manifest in environments as something much larger than he appears. Serves as the Court's ambassador to the Bacchic mortal traditions still surviving in rural Greece and Italy. His treaty relationships with three surviving Dionysian lodges are the primary conduit through which the Court feeds Pneuma-exchange into the mortal world through dream and fertility rites.

**Talōs** — The Burning Crown's wall. A giant of bronze, technically a sidhe of middle antiquity who found a form appropriate to his function and has not left it since. Guards the approach to the Hall of Beaten Gold from the Mediterranean side. Does not negotiate. Does not accept messages. Will, however, move aside if a properly sworn Burning Crown contractor shows the correct mark.

**Tirnos** — Young, by sidhe standards; barely four centuries old, still capable of a fascination with the mortal world that the older nobles have long since traded for strategic distance. Tirnos is the Court's primary recruiter of Bacchic cultists and rural Italian and Greek hedge-practitioners. He has a serious and partially reciprocated fixation on a mortal herbalist in Campania whose grandmother he also had a fixation on, which is the kind of situation the Burning Crown produces and Long Night has learned to simply not comment on.

#### The Hall of Beaten Gold: Summer Capital

The Burning Crown's capital is a sun-drenched palace of gold and olive-wood on a mountain that does not exist in the mortal world. It is accessible through gates in Crete, Etruria, and the Lebanon — all locations with long histories of Burning Crown mortal engagement. The interior is warm year-round; the air smells of olive blossom and something slightly electric that scholars from the Concord's Alexandrian school associate with the moment before a summer thunderstorm.

The Court's great hall is the *Tholos Chrysos*, the Golden Round, and it is large enough to hold the entire active Burning Crown nobility at full formal assembly, which occurs at both solstices and the midpoints between — the four great seasonal nodes at which the Court's formal debt-ledger is reviewed, bargains are renewed, and the Stone Throne's position is formally acknowledged.

#### Border-Holding: The Hill-Court at Lugdunum

*Sample holding — for GM reference*

In the Veiled Sphere correspondent to the hills above **Lugdunum** (modern Lyon) in central Gaul — within the Frankish kingdom since Clovis's consolidation, sitting uneasily between the Burgundian remnants to the east and the Visigothic holdings to the south — the Burning Crown maintains a **border hill-court**: a compact faerie installation equivalent to a small noble's manor-house, staffed by a dozen Aestivales and their attached Free Folk retinue.

**Lord:** *Carannus of the Green Road* — a Burning Crown noble of moderate age (roughly six hundred years), Gaulish in cultural formation, who has served as the Court's liaison to Gaulish mortal traditions since before the Frankish consolidation. He is the reason the rural Gaulish fertility cult of *Épona* (the horse-goddess, whose sanctuary is a real location in the hills) has not been entirely absorbed by the Christian church — he has been quietly providing the priests of that tradition with just enough observable result to maintain plausibility, which is the minimum necessary for a mortal cult to survive ecclesiastical pressure in this period.

**Staff:** Carannus's court includes two lesser Burning Crown sidhe, four Free Folk sprites (of the type that inhabits waterways and sacred springs), three mortal-world contractors who have Veiled-Sphere access through the hill-court gate, and one kobold who wandered in from the German mines fifteen years ago and has not yet been persuaded to leave.

**Function:** The hill-court serves as the Burning Crown's primary intelligence-gathering and debt-processing node for the central Gaulish territory, the Rhenish frontier, and the upper Italian trade routes. It also serves as the nearest Burning Crown installation to the Long Night frost-gate in the Carpathians — the two Courts maintain a standing contact protocol through which urgent cross-court communications can be relayed through neutral Free Folk runners in under a week.

**Current tension:** The Frankish Catholic consolidation is putting pressure on Carannus's mortal contractors, several of whom are prominent in communities where bishop's authority is tightening. One contractor, a herbalist-widow named **Flavia Turantia**, has recently been identified by her local priest as a potential heretical suspect. Carannus has not yet intervened. He is calculating whether intervention costs more than it saves.

### Philosophy: Passion, Balance, and the Price of Love

Where Morrigna's philosophy is the philosophy of a wall, Aigle's philosophy is the philosophy of a garden: something that must be actively cultivated, not merely defended. The Burning Crown does not believe that keeping the mortal world safe is sufficient; it believes that keeping the mortal world *alive* — fully alive, capable of joy and grief and creativity and the entire sprawling excess of human experience — is what makes the safety worth the cost.

This produces a Court that is **passionate, partial, and occasionally catastrophic in its interventions**. A Long Night bargain is precise and impersonal; a Burning Crown bargain is precise and intensely, viscerally personal. The Court's preferred mortal cult is the Bacchic tradition — not because the Court shares Mormo's interest in dissolution and madness, but because the Bacchic tradition is the tradition that most honestly acknowledges that human beings are not rational creatures and that attempting to govern one's life by pure reason is both futile and wasteful.

Aigle believes that Long Night's error — the error the Court has been making for four thousand years — is in treating mortal Pneuma purely as a resource. Mortals are not a fuel source; they are the *reason* the wall has to be held. If the wall exists only to protect people who have been reduced to components of the wall's economy, something important has been lost.

She is not wrong. She is also in a position where being right does not give her the leverage to change the situation, because the wall has to be held, the debts have to be collected, and she and her sister are the two ends of the lever that keeps the world in balance.

The recent Heliodora crisis has sharpened this tension to a point where Aigle's own Court is uncertain of her stability. She knows this. She is managing the uncertainty by being very, very busy.

### Mortal Bargains and Supernatural Interaction

The Burning Crown reaches mortals through **dreams, harvests, fertility rites, and the occasional violent love affair**. Its preferred points of contact are the sacred spaces that remain active in the popular traditions of the Mediterranean basin and Celtic western Europe: the shrine at the sacred spring, the harvest-feast where old words are still said over the grain, the boundary-night when the Veil is thin and mortals go to the crossroads for reasons they cannot quite articulate.

**Bargain structure**: Burning Crown bargains tend to be framed in the vocabulary of gift and counter-gift rather than Long Night's debt-and-penalty architecture. This does not make them less binding — it makes them *differently* binding. A Burning Crown debt is structured as an obligation to the specific *relationship*, not to an abstract ledger entry. Breaking it feels like betrayal rather than default, and the penalty is experienced accordingly.

**Bacchic cult network**: The Burning Crown maintains a working relationship with the rural Bacchic lodges still surviving in Italy and Greece. These lodges are not formal Court contractors; they are mortal communities whose practice of collective ecstasy, seasonal acknowledgment, and communal oath-making produces a Pneuma-flow that the Court collects through proximity rather than explicit bargain. The lodges know something attends their rites. They have various names for it. The Court does not correct the names.

**Concord relationship**: Burning Crown is a Concordat signatory. The relationship with the Concord is warmer than Long Night's — Aigle engages readily with Concord diplomats and scholars, and several senior Concord members have long-standing personal relationships with Burning Crown nobles. The outstanding problem is Aigle's grudge against Drepanios, which is a bilateral issue that neither the Court's internal politics nor the Concord's internal politics have yet found a resolution for.

**The Esoteric Order of Dagon**: Unconditional hostility. The blood-grudge from Mag Tuired does not expire. Any PC who needs the Burning Crown's military cooperation against a Deep One target has the nearest thing to a guaranteed alliance that Faerie offers.

### GM Notes

- **Aigle as ambiguous ally**: She is grieving, volatile, and strategically essential. She will help PCs who bring her useful things to do with her grief. She will complicate PCs who remind her too much of Heliodora or of Drepanios.
- **Theodoros**: An ideal PC ally — honorable, exhausted, carrying more than one person's weight. The person he cannot have is a Long Night contractor. This is not an accident.
- **The hill-court at Lugdunum** is a ready-to-run starting location for campaigns set in Gaul or at the Frankish-Burgundian-Visigothic borderland.
- **The Dagda in Dingle**: A retired Nephil who knows everything about the Mag Tuired war and is available for consultation if the PCs find him before someone else does. He will not volunteer information. He will answer direct questions with infuriating precision.

## PART THREE: THE FREE FOLK

### *Liberi Populi* — The Wyldfae

> *"Neither Court will claim me. I take this as evidence that I am doing something right."*
>
> — **Gwynn ap Nudd**, to a Concord diplomatic party, 451 AD

### Overview

Beyond the Courts, outside both the winter hierarchy and the summer one, wander the **Free Folk** (*Liberi Populi* in Concord Latin; *Wyldfae* in the common usage of Britannia and the northern territories). These are faerie beings — ranging from tiny household sprites to entities of considerable age and power — who have sworn to neither Court and hold their freedom as the one possession they refuse to bargain away.

The Free Folk are not a faction. They share no leadership, no philosophy, no unified agenda. What they share is the characteristic of having said no — no to Morrigna's precise ledgers, no to Aigle's warm entanglements, no to the centuries of obligation that Court membership requires. Some of them have the capacity to take Court oaths and have simply declined; some are constitutionally unsuited to the Court structure; some are powerful enough that neither Court has been willing to press the matter.

In times of war, the Free Folk polarize: the "kind" ones — the nurturing spirits, the household guardians, the dream-weavers — tend toward the Burning Crown's sphere of influence; the "cruel" ones — the bog-lights, the wailing spirits, the things that follow lonely travelers — tend toward Long Night's. But this is tendency, not obligation, and individual Free Folk will do what individual Free Folk will do.

### The Great Free Folk: Erlking and Gwynn ap Nudd

**The Erlking of the German Forests** — The sovereign of the Wild Hunt, the King of the Winds that run ahead of winter, the lord of the forest roads after dark. The Erlking is not a Long Night noble; he is attached to Long Night's honorary court *out of courtesy*, which means he turns up at Arctistor at the solstices and eats Long Night's food and does not take Long Night's orders. Morrigna permits this because the Erlking's Wild Hunt is useful as an occasional enforcement instrument and because the alternative — trying to compel the Erlking's obedience — would consume resources she cannot spare from the Eridu rotation.

The Hunt rides in the Germanic territories from Samhain to the spring equinox, gathering the restless dead and maintaining the barrier between the Veiled Sphere and the mortal world in the northern forests. It does this without a contract, without pay, and without asking anyone's permission, which is the Erlking's preferred mode of operation in all things.

**Gwynn ap Nudd** — Lord of the British Otherworld, keeper of the *Annwn* (the Welsh-British tradition's Otherworld, which is the Veiled Sphere as seen through the lens of Brittonic mortal tradition). Gwynn holds king-like status among the Free Folk of Britannia and the adjacent western sea territories. He does not consider himself subject to either Court and has said so in direct terms to both Queens on separate occasions. Aigle considers this charming. Morrigna considers it a debt of standing that has not yet been called in. Gwynn considers the Concordat a document he knows about but has not signed, which is technically accurate and practically a permanent diplomatic irritant.

In 500 AD, Gwynn is watching the Arthurian situation with close attention. The Court of Long Night has formal interests in sub-Roman Britain (Long Night is listed in the setting's regional overview as the primary hidden power in Britannia). Gwynn's relationship to this is complex: he predates any Court's interest in the island, and his assistance can be sought, by those who know the correct approach, independently of any Court affiliation.

### Regional Free Folk

#### The Djinn of Arabia (*Liberi Deserti*)

The *djinn* of the Arabian peninsula and the deep desert territories are among the oldest and most singular of the Free Folk. They are not sidhe — they do not share the Celtic-Hyperborean morphology of the Courts' membership — but they are faerie beings in the Concord's broader taxonomy: entities of the Veiled Sphere who are capable of sustained engagement with the mortal world and who operate through a contract economy.

The djinn predate Islam by millennia and will outlast it by the same measure. Their bargain-structure is, if anything, more formalized than the Courts': the *'ahd* (covenant), the *qasam* (oath-binding), and the system of *khidma* (service-debt) that governs relationships between djinn and mortal contractors constitute an independent tradition of oath-magic that the Concord has studied with respect and occasional alarm. A djinn's 'ahd is not less binding than a Court contract; it is differently structured, drawing on the fire-and-wind cosmology of the pre-Islamic Arabian tradition rather than on the Hyperborean cold-iron framework.

The djinn of 500 AD operate across the Arabian Peninsula, the Levant, and the Persian frontier territories. They have occasional dealings with the Mobedān of Persia (whose fire-magic speaks to the djinn's own cosmological vocabulary) and with the Burning Crown's eastern holdings (the Lebanon gate to the Hall of Beaten Gold is adjacent to territory where djinn of the coastal variety have long-established treaty arrangements with local mortal communities). They have no dealing with Long Night that the Concord's records can identify, and they regard the Concordat as a western document that applies to western situations.

**The King of the Eastern Djinn**, known in the Concord's records only as *al-Muzaffar* ("the Victorious") — a being of considerable age and power who maintains his principal court in the Veiled Sphere correspondent to the Najd plateau — is not a Concordat signatory. He is also not, as far as the Concord can determine, hostile to the Concordat's purposes. He simply considers the treaty irrelevant to his situation, and since his situation has not intersected badly with Concordat business in the four centuries since the treaty was extended, neither party has pressed the issue.

**For PCs**: A PC operating in the Arabian or Levantine territories who needs Free Folk assistance will find the djinn both accessible (they engage with mortals readily, by tradition and preference) and demanding (their contracts are precise and their interest rates are high). The correct approach is through the *du'a* tradition — a formal request made at a location the djinn is known to frequent, at the correct hour, with the correct offering. Getting these details wrong is not dangerous; it simply means the djinn does not come.

#### The Ancient Iberian Wyldfae

The Iberian Peninsula's faerie population is among the oldest on the western European landmass, predating both the Celts who settled the north and the Phoenician traders who colonized the south. The setting's regional overview refers to "ancient Iberian Wyldfae" without elaborating; the following is what the Concord's records contain.

The Iberian Free Folk are associated, in their oldest layers, with the cave-painting tradition of the Cantabrian region — a tradition that the Concord's most senior scholars believe reflects genuine mortal-Veiled-Sphere contact going back to the Upper Paleolithic. The cave-painters were not the Free Folk; but they were, in some cases, mortals who had access to the Veiled Sphere through specific cave-locations that served as natural gates, and the painted animals of Altamira and its sister-sites are, in the Concord's working hypothesis, portraits of Veiled-Sphere beings encountered through those gates.

In 500 AD, the ancient Iberian Wyldfae are difficult to reach and difficult to deal with. They do not use the Concord's contract frameworks. They do not use the djinn's 'ahd structure. Their bargain-tradition is older than both and operates through **witness** rather than oath: a mortal makes a request in the presence of the correct Iberian being, in the correct location, at the correct seasonal node, and what happens next is determined by what the being witnesses — not the words spoken, but the state of the person speaking. They are extraordinarily perceptive and extraordinarily difficult to deceive, because deception is a function of words and they listen to neither.

The Visigothic administration of Hispania interacts with the Iberian Wyldfae primarily by not interacting with them. The Church interacts with them by condemning them. Neither approach is incorrect; both are strategically limited.

#### Household Spirits of the Roman World

The *lares* and *lemures* of every Roman household — the guardian spirits of threshold and hearth, the restless dead of the family line — are, in the Concord's taxonomy, the smallest and most domesticated tier of the Free Folk. They operate through the oldest and most reflexive of all mortal contract traditions: the daily offering, the spoken acknowledgment, the small gift left at the household shrine.

As the Christian church extends its reach through the former Roman territories, the household spirits are experiencing what can only be described as an identity crisis. The Church explicitly prohibits the *lararium* and the daily offering. Households that comply find their lares either departing to find a more receptive mortal family or transitioning into the category of *lemures* (restless and occasionally hostile spirits of the uncared-for dead). The distinction between a lar and a lemur is, in practice, whether the mortal household is maintaining the relationship.

This is not a crisis the Courts address directly — it is below the threshold of diplomatic notice. But the accumulation of abandoned household-compact relationships across the former Roman world is producing a slow increase in unattached, uncontracted mortal-adjacent spirit entities, and Morrigna's intelligencers have flagged it as a pattern worth watching.

**The Wizard's-Wards** (*Custodes Magi*) — A specific Free Folk unit worth noting for its exemplary function: a flight of household sprites sworn to **Markos Drepanios** in exchange for offerings of milk, honeyed barley-bread, and silver coins. They guard his rooms in Constantinople. This is not exceptional or unusual — it is entirely typical of how a single mortal of sufficient Pneuma and good faith can establish a working Free Folk compact without Court involvement. The Wards are not large, not powerful by any standard, and not capable of standing against any serious threat. They are capable of waking Drepanios from a sound sleep at the correct moment, which has saved his life on at least three occasions.

#### The Kobolds of the German Mines

The *kobolds* — mine-spirits, shaft-dwellers, beings who live in the transitional space between the mortal world's mineral geography and the Veiled Sphere's underground reaches — are Free Folk of the industrial variety. They have coexisted with Germanic mining communities since before the Germanic tribes took their current forms, and the practical relationship between kobolds and mortal miners is one of the most durably successful mortal-Faerie partnerships in the Concord's records.

Kobolds operate on a simple and durable contract: the miners leave a portion of each day's yield at the shaft-bottom, acknowledge the kobold's presence in the correct form, and do not attempt to drive them out. The kobolds in return warn of unstable shafts, direct miners toward richer veins, and occasionally physically intervene to prevent collapses. This relationship has run unbroken in some locations for three hundred years.

The Erlking considers the kobolds his nominal subordinates, which they nominally accept. Long Night considers them honorarily attached to the winter-season Free Folk. The kobolds consider themselves attached to their specific shafts and have no strong opinions about anything else.

### The Wizard's-Ward Contract: A Model for Mortal PCs

The Wizard's-Wards described above are canon; they also serve as a template for what PC engagement with the Free Folk looks like at the small end of the scale. Any mortal of meaningful Pneuma who identifies a Free Folk entity in their local environment, establishes the correct approach, and makes offerings in good faith can enter into a compact with that entity. The compact will be specific to the entity's nature, will not involve a formal Court oath (which is a much more binding and consequential instrument), and will produce modest but reliable results within the entity's domain.

**Correct approach**: Identify the entity's location, the correct material offering, and the correct manner of address (which varies by entity type and must usually be determined by research, local tradition, or previous successful contact). Make the offering without bargaining — the first offering is a gift, not a payment. Wait. If the entity responds, the compact can be negotiated.

**What Free Folk cannot offer**: Mantles of power, cross-Sphere passage without ritual preparation, protection against Cold Iron, intervention against Court members. What they can offer: information about local Veiled-Sphere topology, early warning of supernatural threats in their domain, small practical assistance within their sphere of influence, and — most valuably — an introduction to a Court noble who might be persuaded to engage with the mortal more formally.

## CODA: THE PACT OF THE LONG NIGHT AND WHAT IT COSTS

> *"The two Queens came to Alexandria in the autumn of the year that the treaty was sealed. They came through the South Gate at dusk, walking side by side, one white-haired and one gold-haired, in identical robes of grey and saffron. The guards at the gate simply moved aside. No one who saw them forgot. No one who forgot was able to explain to anyone else what it was, exactly, that they had seen."*
>
> — **Preserved fragment**, possibly from the lost *Chronicle of the Alexandrian Accord*, attributed to a Concord archivist, 287 BC. Authenticity disputed; the Concord does not dispute it.

The Concordat of Hidden Powers is formally called the *Concordia Potestatum Occultarum*. In Court usage it is the Pact of the Long Night. Morrigna named it that deliberately, when she drafted it nearly eight hundred years ago, as a reminder: before the wizard-college at Alexandria and the vampire-houses of Rome and the Persian fire-priests and the rest of the hidden world started conducting their politics, there was a Court that was already holding the wall.

Both Courts are signatories. Both Courts take the Outside-Threat Clause seriously, each in its own register: Long Night because the Outer Gates are its permanent mandate; the Burning Crown because Mag Tuired taught it, in blood and fire that are still remembered in the Court's great hall, what Outer-aligned entities do to a world that does not resist them.

The Concordat's hospitality clause protects any signatory in any signatory's territory. This means that a mortal contractor properly sworn to either Court is, in principle, protected by Concordat hospitality anywhere the Concordat applies — including within the Concord's own institutions. This is not a right the Courts advertise, but it is a right they will enforce, and the Concord's Inner Seal has been the recipient of several very precise letters from Arctistor on the subject of mortal contractors who were mistreated on Concord-administered premises.

The two Courts are eternal rivals and occasional allies. They will not annihilate each other because the cosmological structure of the Veiled Sphere requires both of them — remove Long Night and the wall fails; remove the Burning Crown and the logic of the wall eventually converts every living mortal into a debt-component. They will not merge because they are constitutionally opposed in every dimension except the one that matters: both Queens, when the alternative is the Outer Dark, will fight.

They have been fighting, in their different ways, for four thousand years.

The wall holds.
