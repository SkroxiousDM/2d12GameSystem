# THE YITHIAN ARCHIVE

> *"A scholar came to me in Alexandria — a man of Antioch, heavy-eyed, speaking as though through water — and asked me every question I had never thought to be asked. The names of my teachers' teachers. The hour of my grandmother's death. Whether I had ever dreamed of towers built for no sun. I answered everything. He wrote nothing down. When he left, I understood he had not needed to."*
>
> — Hypatia of Alexandria, private correspondence, c. 410 AD (recovered, Concord archive at the Castrum, restricted collection)

> *"There is a class of scholars who do not belong to any school, observe without worshipping, record without distorting, and refuse all patrons. I have met two in my life. Both made me feel I was a letter being read aloud by someone who already knew the ending."*
>
> — Damaskios of Damascus, *Doubts and Solutions Concerning First Principles*, scholium on Book IV (Castrum translation)

> *"Do not bargain with the Ones Who Write Down. They do not need what you offer. They already have what you will offer, and what you will offer after that, and the offer your grandchildren's grandchildren will one day lay at their feet. To deal with them is to be a character in a book. The book was finished before your birth."*
>
> — attributed to the Mobedān patriarch Bondos the Reformer, *Sayings Against Unwise Counsel* (oral tradition; date uncertain)

> *"She looked at the burning city, and then she looked at me, and she said — in the voice of a nine-year-old girl — 'I have seventy-three comparable events logged for this latitude and a further two hundred and eleven cross-indexed by outcome. The variation in mortality rates is instructive.' I have not been able to sleep since without dreaming of a library with no windows and no doors."*
>
> — a Stylite champion, after escorting the Recorder through Sirmium, 499 AD (oral report, Inner Seal files, sealed)

---

The **Yithian Archive** (*Bibliotheca Iethi*, sometimes rendered in late-antique Greek as *Archeion tōn Iēthianōn*) is, properly speaking, not a faction of the world at all. It is a faction *outside time* that has, with perfect deliberation, chosen to send a representative into it. The **Great Race of Yith** are not gods, not demons, not fallen Watchers, not Nephilim, not servants of the Outer Ones. They are something rarer and, to mortal minds, far more disquieting: they are *scholars*. They are the most meticulous, most relentless, most dispassionate chroniclers in the history of all known sapient life — a race that solved the problem of historical documentation by the simple expedient of reaching backward through time itself and being there when it happened.

In 500 AD they are present in the Mediterranean in the form of a single Greek child of nine years named **Eudokia of Antioch**, called by the Concordat signatories **the Recorder**. She walks among the hidden powers with the quiet authority of an archivist reviewing proofs in a manuscript she is only nominally obligated to correct. She does not want your worship. She does not want your territory. She does not want your soul. She wants your testimony. She wants to know what it was like, from the inside, to watch the thing happen — and she has already noted, with full citations, what you are going to tell her.

This is the document that explains what she is, where she came from, what she wants from the PCs, and exactly why the wisest course is to help her get it.

## THE GREAT RACE: A COSMIC HISTORY

### Origin Beyond Origin

The Great Race of Yith did not originate in the cone-shaped bodies that every scholar of the hidden world has come to associate with them. In the Yithians' own self-documentation — fragments of which the Recorder has, at her own discretion, shared with select Concord magisters — the Race describes itself as *teleologically homeless*: a species whose minds are not native to any single epoch or body. The cone-bodies are not their origin. They are a *residence* — a period tenancy in a set of forms that happened to be available, in an era that happened to be strategically well-situated for the work of comprehensive documentation.

The truth, as best the Inner Seal can reconstruct it from the Recorder's guarded disclosures, is this: the Great Race are minds that **migrate**. They do not travel through space. They travel through *time*, and more precisely they project their consciousness forward and backward along the axis of causality, displacing the native mind of whatever being they inhabit to a Yithian body and installing themselves in the vacated form. This is the mechanism the Concord documents as **the mind-swap** (*psychē-antallage*, in the Archive's own Greek-inflected terminology) — and it is not a violation, by Yithian reckoning, any more than scholarly occupation of a library is vandalism of the building. The building is used. The books are consulted. The books do not, the Race would note, experience the consultation.

Mortals disagree. This is logged.

### The Cone-Body Period and Pnakotus

The period most relevant to the Aetas Obscura setting is what the Archive internally classifies as the **Middle Colonial Era** — spanning roughly from the first appearance of recognizable mortal civilizations in Mesopotamia (c. 3500 BC by Concord dating) forward through what the Yithians' own projections identify as an unspecified cataclysm in the far distant future that will render the cone-form no longer viable. In this era, the Great Race occupies **cone-shaped bodies** standing roughly three meters tall, with four distinct limbs arranged radially, a complex cluster of sensory and communication organs at the apex, and a biochemistry entirely unlike that of terrestrial life. They build in a desert continent they have rendered, over millennia, into a single vast scholarly campus. This is **Pnakotus** — the primary archive.

Pnakotus is real, it exists, and in 500 AD it is inaccessible. The continent it occupies is the far southern mass the Hephthalites' southern trading partners call the Land of Utter Wind — what later ages will know as Australia. The journey from the Mediterranean to Pnakotus in the age of sail and wagon-road requires, by the Recorder's own calm estimate, between three and five years of uninterrupted travel, assuming one survives the Indian Ocean crossing, the deep desert interior, and the fact that Pnakotus is **buried**. The city has been underground, deliberately and precisely, for approximately four thousand years. The Great Race withdrew below the surface at a juncture the Concord's best reconstructions place around 2000 BC, sealing the access shafts with mechanisms that respond only to Yithian physiological signatures.

The reason for the withdrawal is not fully documented in any source available to Concord scholars. The Recorder, when asked directly by Damaskios during their first formal meeting (Castrum, 491 AD), paused for what witnesses describe as an unusually long time — perhaps twenty seconds — and then said: *"The city was observed. We prefer not to be observed."* Damaskios did not press the question. He has pressed every other question he has thought of since. The answer to this one has not changed.

What is clear is that the Race's withdrawal underground did not end their documentation work. It intensified it. With no surface presence to defend, they redirected all productive capacity to the Archive itself and to the mind-swap program, which expanded enormously in the centuries following the retreat. The buried city is not a ruin. It is a *library in active use*, staffed by cone-bodied Yithians going about their scholarly business in a maze of windowless chambers lit by phosphorescent vaulting, speaking in the clicking, scraping language the Recorder occasionally reproduces for the Concord's benefit and which sounds, witnesses report, disturbingly like the turning of very old pages.

### The Flight That Was Always Coming

The Yithians know — because they have read the records, because some of their number have projected forward and verified them in person — that the cone-body era ends. The *how* of that ending is not something the Archive has made available to mortal interlocutors, though the Recorder has confirmed that it is *not* caused by any of the currently active Outer Ones and *not* preventable by any action the Concordat signatories could take. What the Archive does with this knowledge is, in its way, the most alien thing about them: nothing. They do not panic. They do not bargain. They document the period between now and the ending with the same thoroughness they would bring to any other epoch, confident that before the cone-form fails them, they will have already solved the problem of where to go next.

The Great Race has migrated before. Their deepest records — the ones the Recorder describes as *prior to indexable sequence* — suggest that the cone-body period is not their first tenancy and will not be their last. They have occupied, or will occupy, forms the Recorder declines to describe, across a chronological range that, when she mentions it at all, she states in figures that do not correspond to any timescale the Concord has found useful to contemplate. The race is, in a functional sense, **immortal by documentation**: even if every cone-body perished tonight, the minds and the records would persist in archives that exist, simultaneously, in multiple periods of time that have not yet been damaged. To destroy the Yithian Archive would require the destruction not just of Pnakotus but of every point in history at which a Yithian mind has ever been resident — which is to say, effectively, every point.

This is why the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh has not, despite their long-standing desire to possess the Recorder's knowledge, attempted to coerce it from her by force. The threat calculus does not resolve in a satisfying direction.

## THE ARCHIVE'S INTEREST IN 500 AD

### Why This Moment, Why This Place

The Great Race observes all of time. The question of why the Mediterranean world in 500 AD receives *active* Archive attention — as opposed to the passive documentation of the mind-swap surveys — has a specific answer that the Recorder will, if properly asked, provide with something approaching enthusiasm.

This is a **hinge point**.

The Yithians classify historical moments on a scale of causal density — the degree to which events occurring in a given place and time will propagate consequences across a wide subsequent range. Low-density periods are well-documented but not actively staffed; high-density periods, where a comparatively small number of choices shape the conditions for an enormous subsequent range of outcomes, receive direct attention. By the Archive's classification, the Mediterranean in 500 AD is one of approximately eleven such hinge-windows in the entire span of documented human history. The others include the mid-Bronze Age collapse, the Macedonian conquest period, the establishment of the Concordat, and a sequence of events in the seventh and eighth centuries that the Recorder has declined to specify more precisely than "consequential."

What makes this specific hinge so densely causal? The Recorder, in her most expansive briefing of the Inner Seal (Castrum, spring 499 AD, transcript sealed), identified the following factors:

- The worship-pool of the Mediterranean's primary Nephil (Jehovah) is expanding at a rate that will, if uninterrupted, produce conditions the Archive classifies as *Nephilim-saturation* within six to eight generations — a state in which a single Nephil has absorbed enough worship-yield to begin operating at a level the Concord would find practically impossible to oppose.
- The bound Watchers (*Egrēgoroi*) in their valley-pits are not static. Three of them have, by Yithian measurement, moved. The direction is upward. The chains have not broken, but they are under load they have not been under since the Flood.
- The Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh's 333-year operational cycle concludes in 502 AD. The Archive has seventeen prior complete cycles on record. In fourteen of those cycles, the Brotherhood's operational apex coincides with a Concordat-destabilizing event. In three of those cycles, it does not. The Archive has not shared what differentiates the three from the fourteen.
- And finally: **Eudokia of Antioch is nine years old.** The Recorder's body is nine years old. She will be renewed, by the Archive's own protocol, at the onset of her twentieth year. Between now and then, she is in a window of unique vulnerability — the only period in her tenure in which she is both fully inhabited by Yithian consciousness and young enough that a sufficiently determined adversary might attempt to acquire her by methods the Concordat's protections are not designed to counter. The Archive has staffed this window accordingly.

### The Work in the Mediterranean: Possessed Scholars as Living Pens

The Yithians do not employ agents in the conventional sense. They do not fund, threaten, bribe, or recruit. What they do is **occupy**. The mind-swap program, which has operated continuously since the first Yithian observer noted the emergence of written language in Mesopotamia in the late fourth millennium BC, places Yithian consciousnesses into the bodies of mortal scholars, philosophers, physicians, administrators, and occasionally soldiers for periods ranging from weeks to years. The displaced mortal mind is housed in a cone-body in Pnakotus during this period, provided with adequate sustenance and, the Recorder notes, *access to the Archive* — which last detail she appears to consider a significant benefit. When the exchange concludes, the mortal is returned to their body with no clear memory of the period, though the suppression is imperfect: what remains is dreams.

In 500 AD the mind-swap program is operating at what the Archive calls *elevated research intensity* for the Mediterranean theater. The Recorder will not confirm exact numbers, but the Inner Seal's own investigation — conducted primarily by the Stylite called the **Marble Lens**, whose specialty is the detection of displaced cognition — has identified at least **twelve currently active Yithian vessels** in the Mediterranean and Balkans, with the highest concentrations in Alexandria, Constantinople, Antioch, and (most intriguingly) the senatorial court at Ravenna. None of these vessels are aware of what they are. Each carries a Yithian researcher as a passenger, going through the motions of mortal life with slightly more precision, slightly more data-gathering focus, and slightly less interest in sleep than the native personality displayed before the exchange.

These **vessels** (*skeuē* in Concord classification; the Archive's own term, as rendered by the Recorder, translates more precisely as *temporary correspondence-points*) are not agents the GM should treat as Archive assets in any tactical sense. They do not act on Archive instructions; the Yithian consciousness within each is not a spy or operative but a researcher, conducting interviews, reading documents, attending events, and feeding observations back to Pnakotus through mechanisms the Concord has not successfully characterized. When the research is complete, the Yithian withdraws. The mortal wakes up, confused, some years older, with no clear account of the interval.

What the mortal *does* retain — what the suppression never fully erases — is the shape of what was done to them. The dreams.

## THE RELAY-STATION AT SIRMIUM

Sirmium — the city on the Sava River in the Balkans, former capital of four Roman emperors, presently a battered administrative hub in what remains of the Eastern Roman provincial system — hosts the Archive's only known active surface installation in the Mediterranean region. The **relay-station** (*stathmós diapontoporías* in the Recorder's Greek, which translates more accurately as *waypoint for mind-transit*) occupies the basement levels of a large private house on the city's eastern edge, owned, by Concordat-recognized deed, by the Archive.

The station is staffed, at any given time, by between two and four currently-active Yithian vessels — mortals in mid-swap, who in their Yithian-inhabited state perform the specific technical function the station exists to serve. That function is **transit facilitation**: the station coordinates the mind-swap exchanges for the Mediterranean region, managing the logistics of which mortal consciousnesses are currently housed in Pnakotus (and require eventual return), which Yithian researchers are currently deployed (and require eventual recall), and which new subjects are being assessed for potential acquisition. The mortal bodies of the station's staff, observed by a neighbor or a passing Concord Stylite, appear to be a small household of scholars and scribes who keep unusual hours, rarely socialize, write a great deal in scripts no one can read, and receive occasional visitors of similarly intense scholarly demeanor who arrive and depart without notice. The household has never been taxed, conscripted, raided, or otherwise interfered with by mortal or supernatural authority. The local bishop has twice attempted to determine their religious affiliation and on both occasions experienced, afterward, a strong impression that the question had been thoroughly answered, combined with a complete inability to remember what the answer was.

The Sirmium station is the nearest point of physical Archive presence to any likely PC operating area. It is also, the Inner Seal notes in its internal assessments, the point at which the Archive is most *accessible* — not in the sense of being open to visitors, but in the sense that a PC with legitimate reason to contact the Archive can do so here without traveling to Pnakotus. The station's current senior vessel, **Prokopios of Sirmium**, is detailed below under Notable Vessels.

### Pnakotus: The Buried City

The primary archive lies beneath the desert continent's interior — inaccessible by 500 AD Mediterranean standards, deliberately sealed, and not in any meaningful sense *dangerous* to mortals who have no means of reaching it. It is mentioned here because the Recorder will, if asked where the records are kept, describe it without hesitation, and PCs may develop an interest in making the journey.

The GM's guidance on Pnakotus is simple: it is **end-of-campaign material**. A PC who reaches the buried city has access to a library containing complete historical documentation of every sapient civilization the Archive has indexed. This includes, among other things, the full Secret History of the Nephilim and Watchers with primary-source documentation; the complete roster of bound Watcher locations with architectural surveys of their pits; the full operational history of the Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh across all seventeen of its 333-year cycles; and a copy of every document the Concord's Inner Seal has chosen not to publish. The Yithians will not prevent access to these records. They will not facilitate it. They will note the visit, log the researcher's identity, and file a complete account of what was read and what was done with the information afterward.

## OPERATING IN 500 AD: WHAT THE ARCHIVE WANTS

### Testimony, Not Conquest

The Archive wants nothing from the political landscape of 500 AD. It does not want territory. It does not want worship — the concept of worship-harvest is documented in the Archive as a **Nephilim-class mechanism** and treated with the same scholarly neutrality the Archive applies to documented atrocities: noted, categorized, and not participated in. It does not want power over the Concordat, dominance of the Concord, or the destruction of any other faction. It does not, in the most precise sense, want *anything* — wanting in the purposive sense is a characteristic the Recorder applies exclusively to entities whose documentary archives are not yet complete.

What the Archive *pursues*, which is different, is **testimony**. The mind-swap program is at its core a testimony-gathering operation: a Yithian researcher is placed into a mortal body at a specific hinge-point of historical significance because the researcher wants to know, from the inside, what it felt like to make the choices that will shape the subsequent centuries. The cone-body period's extraordinary documentary record is built on precisely this: not observation from a distance but *participant documentation*, with all the ethical complications the Recorder acknowledges and does not, she notes, find troubling.

For PCs, this translates to a specific and repeatable dynamic. The Archive does not recruit companions or allies. It recruits **informants in the archival sense**: individuals whose choices and experiences are historically significant enough to warrant direct documentation. A PC who has made consequential choices — who has opposed the Brotherhood, brokered a Concordat deal, acquired Inner Seal knowledge, survived a mind-swap, or simply been present at a hinge-event — is a subject of Archive interest. The Recorder, or a Yithian vessel, will seek them out. They will be asked questions. The questions will be precise, comprehensive, and occasionally deeply personal. Nothing that is said will be changed, refuted, or acted on by the Archive. It will be logged with full citations.

This is less reassuring than it sounds. A record that cannot be altered is a record that preserves mistakes permanently.

### The Recorder as Patron-at-Distance

The Recorder — Eudokia of Antioch — is the Archive's only Concordat-recognized representative, and she is the most practical point of PC engagement with the Archive's resources. Under the Concordat's Observer provisions, she holds what amounts to diplomatic immunity plus scholarly access: she is welcome, by treaty-right, at every Concordat session, every major faction's court (save the Brotherhood and the Order of Dagon, which do not hold court in any Concordat-adjacent sense), and every event of Concordat-relevance. This access is, in practice, exactly as broad as she chooses to make it.

What the Recorder offers in exchange for cooperation is, by any normal measure, extraordinary: she has the complete documentary record of everything that has already happened. She will not share it freely. She will not share it casually. She will not share it as a favor. But she will, under specific conditions — the provision of testimony she does not have, the facilitation of Archive access to events she cannot attend alone, the protection of her person in circumstances where the Concordat protections are insufficient — share *specific, targeted excerpts* that are directly relevant to the immediate situation. A PC negotiating with the Recorder for information is not negotiating in the normal sense. They are negotiating with someone who already knows what they will offer, what they will accept, and what they will do with the information afterward. The Recorder does not reveal this knowledge as a coercive tool. She simply notes, with the placidity of a nine-year-old who has lived several centuries in aggregate, that she finds the conversation interesting.

PCs who assist the Recorder gain access, at GM discretion, to what the Inner Seal calls **Archive citations** — precisely verified historical facts that function as automatic successes on relevant knowledge checks, or as documentary evidence that cannot be disputed in Concordat proceedings. The Recorder does not lie. The Recorder does not exaggerate. The Recorder does not editorialize. What the Recorder provides is simply, exactly, and completely true.

This is also, in its way, the most dangerous thing about her.

## THE DANGER OF DEALING WITH THE ARCHIVE

### The Borrowed Body: A Cost the Vessel Does Not Consent To

The mind-swap is not violent. The Yithians are careful to note this, through the Recorder, whenever the question arises. The displaced mortal experiences no pain. The Yithian researcher, inhabiting the mortal body, maintains its physical health, exercises appropriate care, and returns it in no materially worse condition than when it was borrowed. The process is, by every Yithian metric, *humane*.

What it is not is *reversible* in the sense that matters to mortals. **A mortal whose body is borrowed loses years.** Not metaphorically — the exchange takes place in real time, in the mortal Sphere, and the mortal consciousness housed in Pnakotus does not age at the same rate as the body does in the world. A subject who is swapped for two years returns to a body that is two years older, and those two years are gone. For a young person this may be an inconvenience. For a scholar at the apex of their productive life, missing two years is a different matter. For a senator whose political alliance structure required his physical presence for a critical negotiation, or a physician whose patients died without him, or a mother whose child was born during her absence — the Archive's careful note that the body was returned *in good health* is not, mortals find, the comfort it appears intended to be.

The Concord has documented **recovery effects** among returned subjects that range from the mild to the permanent:

- **Standard suppression**: the subject retains no clear memory of the exchange period. They wake, functionally, as if from a deep and dreamless sleep that lasted some weeks or years. Disorientation, temporal confusion, professional and social disruption are common. Most subjects recover functional normalcy within months.
- **Partial breakthrough**: in approximately one subject in eight, the suppression is incomplete. Fragments of the Yithian researcher's perceptions leak through — the sensation of a cone-body, the smell of phosphorescent rock, the clicking-scraping of Yithian language, the sheer spatial enormity of a library with no visible walls. These subjects are prone to recurring nightmares, episodes of dissociation, and in some cases a persistent sense that their own personality is a costume being worn by someone they cannot quite identify. The Concord's medical tradition has no treatment. The Apokryphon's *lytrosis* working has a partial effect. Time helps some. Some subjects improve. Some do not.
- **Full cognitive imprint**: in rare cases — the Marble Lens has documented eleven across the century of Concord records — the Yithian researcher's own cognition leaves a lasting impression on the returned mortal. These subjects develop anomalous scholarly capabilities: total recall, pattern-recognition in historical texts, the ability to read and parse scripts they were never trained in. They also develop a persistent and escalating conviction that they are observing their own life from a slight remove, as if reading a document about themselves. Several have reported, in the final stages of the imprint, hearing the Archive's classification system in the back of their minds whenever they make a significant decision: the soft, internal sound of a record being updated.

None of this is acknowledged by the Archive as harm. The Recorder will confirm all documented effects when asked. She will note, accurately, that the probability of any individual subject experiencing more than standard suppression is low. She will not apologize, because from the Yithians' perspective, the subject's testimony was gathered, the record was completed, and the work was done. The Archive does not consider incomplete documentation to be the worse outcome.

The mortal community in 500 AD has reached its own conclusions.

### The Captive Mind

A mortal whose consciousness is currently resident in Pnakotus — mid-exchange — is, for the duration of the swap, a **captive mind**. They are housed in a Yithian body, provided with basic physical maintenance, and given access (with deliberate Archive generosity) to the Great Library. They cannot leave. They cannot communicate with the Mortal Sphere. They cannot conduct their own affairs, protect their own interests, or return until the Yithian researcher who displaced them is finished with the work.

The Concord's Inner Seal classification for this state is **temporal detainment**, a term that carries a precise legal meaning in Concordat proceedings: it is not imprisonment (the subject is not being punished), not death (the subject's consciousness persists), and not a Mortal Sovereignty violation (the Archive has Concordat Observer status, and the Mortal Sovereignty clause applies to hidden powers *ruling* mortals, not to *temporarily occupying* them for scholarly purposes — a distinction that has been argued before Concordat arbitration three times, and that the Archive has won all three times). It is, however, a complete interruption of the subject's mortal life, conducted without their consent, for a duration determined entirely by the researcher's work schedule.

What can a PC do about this? In the most immediate sense, not much. The Concordat protections do not extend to preventing mind-swaps; they extend only to requiring the Archive to return subjects promptly when the research is complete, to provide basic maintenance during the exchange, and to suppress memories on return to avoid Mortal Sphere destabilization. A PC whose companion has been swapped has limited recourse: they can petition the Recorder for an accelerated return (which she will assess on the merits of the research timeline, not on the basis of the companion's importance to the petitioner), they can offer something the Archive finds more valuable than the current line of research (difficult; the Archive is not easily impressed), or they can wait.

There is, in the deeper lore the Inner Seal has not published, a fourth option. The archive at Pnakotus has, once in documented history, **returned a subject early** — not because the research was complete, not because the Concordat required it, but because a PC with knowledge of the Archive's internal classification system identified the subject's dossier number, framed a specific documentary argument that the subject's return to the Mortal Sphere would generate historically significant testimony that could not otherwise be obtained, and presented that argument through the Sirmium relay-station in the Archive's preferred notation format. The Recorder confirmed this exchange in a briefing to Damaskios. She declined to specify what testimony resulted. She noted that the argument had been correct.

This is the thing the Inner Seal teaches its most advanced members about dealing with the Archive: **the only leverage that works is their own methodology**. They want documentation. Offer them documentation they cannot get another way.

## THE ARCHIVE IN THE CONCORDAT

### Observer Status: What It Means

The Yithian Archive holds **Observer status** in the Concordat of Hidden Powers — a category specifically created, at the Archive's request, when the Concordat was being drafted in 287 BC. The Archive did not request full signatory status. It requested the minimum standing that would permit it to attend Concordat sessions, hold protected property in Concordat-recognized territories, and extend Concordat protections to its mortal representative. Observer status provides all of these without requiring the Archive to be bound by the Concordat's substantive provisions.

The substantive provisions the Archive is thus *not* bound by include:

- **The Mortal Sovereignty clause.** The Archive may occupy mortal bodies for research purposes. The three arbitrations have confirmed this.
- **The Outside-Threat Clause.** The Archive is not obligated to contribute to the defense of reality against Outer One incursions. The Archive will not oppose such defense; it will document it. When this was raised at the 287 BC drafting session, the Yithian representative (not Eudokia — an earlier Recorder, whose name the Archive has never shared) noted, without visible irony, that the Archive has been documenting the Outer Ones since before the formation of the Concordat, and that its records on the subject are available to signatories upon application.
- **The Hospitality clause.** The Archive is not required to host, protect, or entertain other signatories. The house at Sirmium has guests by appointment only.

What the Observer status *does* provide, in the direction the Concordat runs toward the Archive rather than the other way, is extensive: **every signatory is bound to protect the Recorder's person, to facilitate her movement through their territory, and to treat an attack on her as an attack on the Concordat itself.** The cosmic-order penalty for harming the Recorder is not specified in the Concordat text; it is noted as *commensurate with the Archive's documentary capacity to harm the offender's legacy*. In practice, even the most politically reckless signatories — including the Twilight Courts in their less cooperative moods — treat this as an absolute. Morrigna of the Long Night does not defer to children. She defers to the Recorder. The distinction is deliberate and widely noticed.

### The Brotherhood's Interest

The Brotherhood of the Black Pharaoh's desire to acquire the Recorder is the setting's most reliably renewable source of Archive-adjacent adventure. The Brotherhood's internal reasoning, as reconstructed by the Concord's intelligence apparatus, is this: the Recorder carries in her mind a complete and verified record of **every bound Watcher's location**, every structural detail of the Eridu breach seal, every method by which the Concordat's protections might be legally circumvented, and — most pressingly — the documented outcomes of all seventeen prior Brotherhood operational cycles, including the three in which the Brotherhood's plan failed and why. What the Brotherhood would do with this information is straightforward. What the Archive would do about the attempt is less clear, but the Inner Seal's working assumption is that an attack on the Recorder would trigger a response calibrated not to prevent the attack (the Archive does not operate preemptively against mortal factions) but to ensure that the attacker's full documented history — every deal, every ally, every secret the Brotherhood has asked its members to suppress — became available to every Concordat signatory simultaneously.

Whether this would stop the Brotherhood is a question the Inner Seal has not finished discussing.

### The Archive and the Outer Ones: A Particular Neutrality

The Great Race does not worship the Outer Ones. They do not fear them in the active sense. They have documented them. The Recorder has, at Inner Seal request, confirmed that the Archive's records on Nyarlathotep alone fill a section of the Pnakotus library that would take a mortal reader approximately eight years to work through, assuming continuous reading. The records are complete. They are neutral in tone. They include entries on every Mask of Nyarlathotep the Brotherhood has ever employed, every mortal civilization the Crawling Chaos has moved through, and what the outcome was.

There is, however, one specific relationship between the Archive and the Outer Dark that is not neutral. The Recorder has confirmed, in the same briefing in which she outlined the current hinge-point factors, that the Archive's own projections — the forward-looking surveys conducted by Yithian consciousnesses who have projected themselves into future periods and returned — include **a documented discontinuity** in the Archive's own records. At a point in the future the Recorder declines to specify, the Yithian records of the Mediterranean region simply *stop*. Not because the civilization ends (she would note that; she has noted similar transitions in other regions without difficulty). Not because the Archive's attention moves elsewhere. The records stop because the Archive, in the projected period, is **no longer receiving returns from Yithian researchers deployed to the Mediterranean theater**.

What this means is not confirmed. The Recorder does not speculate, and the GM should not confirm. It is the one question she has answered with something that, in a nine-year-old's face, looks more like caution than her usual equanimity.

## RELATIONS WITH OTHER POWERS

### The Concord of the Seven Seals

The relationship between the Concord and the Archive is the most complex and rewarding bilateral relationship in the Concordat. The Concord wants the Archive's information. The Archive wants the Concord's documentary access — Concord agents can reach places and witnesses the Archive's vessel-program is slow to deploy to, and an Inner Seal magister's first-person account of a hinge-event has historiographical value the Archive's own vessel-reports sometimes lack. This mutual want creates a productive if perpetually fraught exchange.

The formal channel is the Recorder herself. Damaskios of Damascus, Scholarch of Athens and the Inner Seal's most intellectually formidable living member, has taken the Recorder's measure over a decade of meetings and arrived at a position the Concord's other magisters find characteristically alarming: he respects her. Not as one respects a useful tool, but as one respects an intellect. He has described the experience of debating the Recorder as *"the first time in forty years of scholarship that I have spoken with someone who makes me work."* The Recorder, when this was relayed to her, noted that Damaskios's file is one of the thicker ones in the 500 AD Mediterranean index.

Markos Drepanios, the Concord's Magister and political head, is more practical: he wants the Archive's records on the Eridu breach, the bound Watcher locations, and the Brotherhood's seventeen cycles, and he has not yet found the argument that will induce the Archive to share them. The Recorder has confirmed that the records exist. She has confirmed that they contain the information Drepanios wants. She has not confirmed what testimony or documentary access she would find sufficient in exchange. She is, apparently, waiting to see what events produce.

### The Twilight Courts

Both Courts treat the Archive with the elaborate courtesy they extend to entities that are more powerful than themselves in ways that cannot be opposed directly. The Long Night Court in particular is alert to the fact that the Archive has documented documentation of the Eridu breach seal — including, presumably, documentation of the exact degree to which the Long Night Court's ongoing military commitment to the wall has shaped Morrigna's political behavior across four thousand years. Morrigna does not enjoy being documented. She has not found a way to prevent it.

The Burning Crown's Heliodora (now deceased; the current Lady of the Burning Crown is in a period of internal consolidation following the 497 AD crisis) maintained a personal relationship with the Recorder that the Inner Seal categorized as *adversarial respect*: two parties who understand each other well enough to be useful, and who dislike each other enough to be honest. The new Lady has not yet had cause to form a position.

### The Mobedān and the Recorder

The Mobedān of Persia, working from their *ahura*-versus-*daēva* doctrinal frame, have reached conclusions about the Watcher-Nephilim history that approximate the Inner Seal's own Secret History more closely than any other mortal tradition. This makes them the faction whose conversations with the Recorder are most substantive and least asymmetrical — the Mobedān are one of the few interlocutors who arrive with genuine first-order knowledge to trade, rather than coming as supplicants seeking the Archive's resources.

The Recorder's relationship with the living patriarchs of the Mobedān — and, more pointedly, with the shade of Kartīr of the Seven Heavens, whose preserved consciousness in the Chinvat Lens the Recorder treats as a fully valid interview subject — is documented in the Archive as *cooperative but bounded*. The Mobedān know more than the Concord. They know less than the Archive. They are aware of both facts and have made their peace with both.

## NOTABLE VESSELS AND AGENTS

The following are three current (500 AD) individuals of Archive interest: one active vessel, one returned subject still within the dream-window, and one mortal who deals with the Archive on a voluntary basis. GMs may use them as-is, adapt them, or treat them as templates for Archive-adjacent NPCs.

### Prokopios of Sirmium — Relay Coordinator (Active Vessel)

Prokopios is, by mortal appearance, a mild-mannered Greek freedman of late middle age who serves as the steward and senior scribe of the Archive's house in Sirmium. He is, by Archive classification, the current senior Yithian researcher deployed to the Balkan relay-station — a consciousness of enormous age and scholarly accomplishment inhabiting a body it has occupied for approximately seven months and will occupy for perhaps three more. The original Prokopios — the mortal who was exchanged — is currently resident in Pnakotus, working through the Archive's holdings on the Hephthalite Hunnic migrations with a growing sense that this is not where he expected to spend his forties.

The Yithian occupying Prokopios's body is not performing Prokopios's former duties (he was a minor commercial scribe with a sideline in legal copying). It is coordinating the Mediterranean theater's exchange program, managing the roster of active vessels, and liaising with the Recorder on current documentation priorities. It speaks Latin, Greek, and Sirmian provincial dialect with Prokopios's accent and mannerisms, has read all of Prokopios's correspondence, and is broadly familiar with his professional and personal context. Where it fails to pass as Prokopios is in three consistent ways: it does not sleep for more than four hours, it reads at a rate no mortal scribe approaches, and when it is thinking hard about something it has a habit of tilting Prokopios's head at a precise fifteen-degree angle that Prokopios's wife has noted is new.

Prokopios-the-Yithian is the GMs' most useful practical contact point for Archive mechanics: scheduling exchanges, issuing warnings about active mind-swap surveys in the PCs' vicinity, and passing along Recorder communications when Eudokia herself is unavailable.

**Prokopios-the-mortal**, when he is eventually returned, will have had access to the Pnakotus library for approximately ten months. What he does with what he has read is a story the GM should tell.

### Theodota of Antioch — Returned Subject (Dream-Window Open)

Theodota is a physician of the school of Alexandria, currently resident in Antioch, thirty-one years old, who returned from a mind-swap approximately four months ago. She has no memory of the eighteen-month period of the exchange — by Concord standard-suppression criteria, the swap was technically successful. She has, however, begun dreaming.

The dreams are consistent: a chamber larger than any building she has entered, lit from the walls themselves, containing shelves of material that is not quite scrolls and not quite tablets, in a script she does not recognize and cannot stop trying to read. The chamber is not frightening. The chamber is, in the dreams, profoundly quiet. Sometimes she is aware, in the dream, that there are other presences in the chamber — vast, cone-shaped, moving with a deliberateness that is not slowness — and that they are not paying attention to her. The awareness of not being paid attention to is, she has told her colleague (who told the Concord), the most distressing element.

Theodota is not psychologically impaired. She functions as a physician with full competence and what her colleagues describe as increased efficiency. She has, in the four months since her return, produced three medical texts of unusual theoretical depth, one of which the Concord's medical faculty has classified as anomalously advanced for someone of her prior training. She is unaware of any of this being unusual.

The Marble Lens has assessed her and confirmed partial breakthrough suppression. The prognosis is, the Lens noted in its report, *not alarming*. The asterisk the Lens added to this assessment — filed separately, under inner-seal restriction — notes that Theodota appears to be *developing an interest* in the history of the Akkadian Empire, a subject entirely unrelated to her medical practice, with a focus on the reign of Naram-Sin. She has begun collecting secondary sources. She does not know why.

### Hierax the Librarian — Voluntary Correspondent

Hierax is not a vessel. He is a mortal scholar in Alexandria, sixty-three years old, who was swapped twenty-two years ago at the age of forty-one, returned after eight months with standard suppression, and made a recovery that by all external measures was complete. By internal measures, it was not. Hierax has spent twenty-two years methodically reconstructing, from the dreams that remained, what he believes he saw in Pnakotus.

He has written six notebooks of reconstruction, in a private shorthand he developed for the purpose. He has attempted to replicate the script from the library shelves from memory — not successfully, but not unsuccessfully enough that the Archive has declined to notice. Seven years ago, a woman he had never met visited his rooms in Alexandria, examined his notebooks without asking, made a specific correction to one of his rendered glyphs, and left without speaking. He has since understood this was the Recorder.

Hierax now corresponds with the Archive voluntarily, through the Sirmium relay-station, sending periodic packets of his reconstructed notes and occasionally receiving, in return, confirmations that are sometimes a single marked glyph and sometimes a longer notation he cannot always parse. He does not consider himself in the Archive's service. He considers himself a scholar who has developed, through unusual circumstances, a correspondence with a library he visited once under conditions he cannot fully remember. He is correct about this. He is also the closest thing to a *mortal Archive specialist* currently active in the Mediterranean, and the Inner Seal is aware of him, has chosen not to interfere, and has not told him they are watching.

If the PCs require a mortal who understands Archive notation and can interpret Recorder communications more precisely than any Concord-trained wizard, Hierax is that mortal. He is also, incidentally, the only person in Alexandria who has been voluntarily corresponding with the Archive for twenty-two years and has not been re-swapped. The reason for this restraint — whether the Archive has declined out of courtesy, caution, or the simple calculation that a voluntary correspondent provides better testimony than another displaced subject — is not documented in any source the PCs can access.

It may be the most interesting question in his file.

## GM NOTES

### Running the Archive at the Table

The Archive is not an antagonist, a patron, or an ally in any conventional campaign sense. It is a **constraint on epistemology**: a faction whose existence means that the setting's history is fully documented somewhere, by entities who have no stake in the outcome. Use this sparingly and with precision.

Three specific ways to deploy Archive presence:

**As verification.** When the PCs are being given contradictory information by interested parties — the Brotherhood says one thing happened, the Concord says another — the Archive is the one source that cannot be lying. Getting Archive confirmation of a historical fact requires either the Recorder's cooperation or access to Sirmium, both of which require the PCs to do something interesting. The information they get will be precise, complete, and stripped of editorial interpretation. They will have to supply the interpretation themselves.

**As pressure.** The knowledge that the Archive is documenting this period — that Prokopios-the-Yithian is filing reports from Sirmium, that the Recorder is logging everything that happens near her — is a persistent reminder that this moment matters. When the PCs are uncertain whether their choices are significant, the Archive's attentiveness is evidence that they are. Use the Recorder's logging-of-subjects (as she logs Gildas as Subject 307) as a recurring signal: the PCs are in the index. They are being watched by something ancient and dispassionate. What they do here will be in the record.

**As mystery.** The documentary discontinuity in the Mediterranean records — the point at which the Archive's future surveys stop receiving returns — is the setting's deepest long-term hook. Do not reveal it early. Do not explain it. Let the players encounter references to it: a vessel who returns with unusual dreams about silence, a notation in Hierax's reconstructed glyphs that he cannot parse, a question from the Recorder that is slightly less confident than her usual precision, a moment when she pauses before answering, and then says something that is exactly true and exactly insufficient. What happens to the Archive in the future is the secret at the bottom of the setting.

The Recorder has logged it. She has not shared it. There is a reason.

### A Single Mind-Swap, Run Well

The GM guidance in §5.6 of the setting bible is worth repeating here: *a mind-swap is a great character-defining event for a PC. Run it once.* This document adds only the following: frame the swap in two parts. First, the pre-swap period — the PC begins to notice that a recent acquaintance of unusual scholarly intensity has been asking very precise questions. Second, the return — the PC wakes with no memory of the missing time, finds that their affairs have been maintained (by the Yithian researcher, who was methodical about this), and begins, in the days that follow, to dream. The dreams do not explain. The dreams do not comfort. The dreams are Pnakotus, rendered in the limited resolution of human sleep: the phosphorescent vaulting, the shelves, the vast quiet, and the sense of having been, for some months, a letter read aloud by someone who already knew the ending.

Play the return effects slowly, and resist the temptation to resolve them. Some things the Archive takes do not come back.
